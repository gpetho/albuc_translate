¶ De modo cauterizandi Caput in commissura coronali.  
On the method of cauterizing the head at the coronal suture.

Caput II.  
Chapter II.

Confert hic cauterizatio ad dominium humiditatis et frigiditatis super cerebrum: quae duae sunt caussae sodae, et multitudinis fluxus humorum ex capite ad partes oculorum et aurium, et multitudinis somni, et doloris dentium, et doloris gutturis: et uniuersaliter omnis aegritudinis, quae accidit ex frigiditate, sicut paralisis, et sodae, epilepsiae, et apoplexiae, et similia illis ex aegritudinibus.  
This cauterization helps to control moisture and coldness over the brain: which are two causes of headaches, and excessive flow of humors from the head to the parts of the eyes and ears, and excess sleep, and tooth pain, and throat pain; and generally all illnesses that arise from cold, such as paralysis, headaches, epilepsy, apoplexy, and similar illnesses.

Modus cauterizationis est, ut praecipias infirmo: ut euacuetur in primis cum medicina solutiua mundificante caput tribus noctibus, aut quatuor, secundum quod exigit uirtus infirmi, et eius aetas, et consuetudo eius.  
The method of cauterization is to instruct the patient: first to be purged with a cleansing medicine for the head over three or four nights, according to the strength, age, and usual condition of the patient.

Deinde praecipe ei, ut radi faciat caput suum cum nouacula.  
Then instruct him to have his head shaved with a razor.

Deinde fac eum sedere inter manus tuas quadratum, ita quod iam posuit manus suas super pectus suum.  
Then make him sit upright between your hands, so that he has already placed his hands on his chest.

Deinde pone radicem palmae tuae super radicem nasi sui inter oculos suos.  
Then place the base of your palm on the base of his nose between his eyes.

Vbicumque ergo peruenerit digitus tuus medius, signa locum illum cum incausto.  
Wherever your middle finger reaches, mark that place with ink.

Deinde igni Cauterium Oliuare: cuius haec est forma.  
Then heat the olive-shaped cautery tool: which has this form.

Deinde pone ipsum super locum signatum cum incausto, descensione, qua constringatur manus tua paulatim, et tu reuolues ipsum.  
Then place it on the spot marked with ink, with a downward motion, gradually applying pressure with your hand, and rotate it.

Deinde leua manum tuam uelociter, et aspicias locum.  
Then lift your hand quickly, and examine the spot.

Quod si uides iam detegi ex osse quantitatem capitis alkiel, aut quantitatem zeuherbi, tunc eleua manum tuam.  
If you see that an area the size of an alkiel seed or a zeuherb seed has been exposed to the bone, then lift your hand.

Et si non, itera manum tuam ferro ipso, aut cum alio ab ipso si infrigidatur: donec uideantur ossa, quae dixi tibi.  
If not, repeat the process with the same tool, or another one if it has cooled, until the bones I told you of are visible.

Deinde sume aliquid ex sale, et solue illud in aqua, et imbibe in ipso cotum, et pone super locum: et dimitte ipsum tribus diebus.  
Then take some salt, dissolve it in water, soak a piece of cloth in it, and place it on the spot, and leave it there for three days.

Deinde pone super ipsum cotum imbibitum in butyro, et dimitte illum super locum, donec destruatur eschara ignis.  
Then place over it a cloth soaked in butter, and leave it on the spot until the burn scab is removed.

Deinde cura illud cum unguento tetrapharmacon, usquequo sanetur.  
Then treat it with tetrapharmacon ointment until it heals.

Iam dixerunt, quod quanto uulnus plus remanet apertum, emittit pus: quare est melius et magis iuuatiuum.  
They have said that the longer the wound remains open, the more pus it discharges: thus it is better and more beneficial.

Et dixerunt quidam eorum, ut cauterizetur cutis usque ad os: et teneatur Cauterium, donec aduratur quiddam spissitudinis ossis.  
Some of them said to cauterize the skin down to the bone: and hold the cautery until some thickness of the bone is burnt.

Deinde radatur post illud quod adustum est ex osse, deinde curetur.  
Then shave off the part of the bone that has been burnt, then treat it.

Et dicunt alii, quod oportet ut cum cauterio perueniatur ad ultimum, donec imprimat in os impressionem for tem, ita quod cadat os, quod fit sicut forma kirate: aut instrumentum quod mulieres ponunt infuso alphabeti parui: et extimant, quod exspirantur ex hoc loco uapores capitis, et dimittatur uulnus apertum tempore longo.  
Others say that it is necessary to go to the end with the cautery, so that it makes a strong impression on the bone, so that the bone falls away, forming something like the shape of a kirate, or like the instrument women use with small carved letters, believing that the head vapors escape from this spot, and the wound is left open for a long time.

Deinde curetur, donec consolidetur.  
Then treat it until it heals.

Mihi autem non uidentur hae duae species Cauterii omnino nisi in quibusdam hominibus secundum uiam timoris.  
To me, however, these two forms of cautery do not seem advisable, except in certain people as a last resort.

Et dimittere ipsum, apud me est melius cum salute ipsius.  
In my view, leaving it as it is, is better for his well-being.

Debilitatur enim caput ex solutione continuitatis ipsius naturalis: sicut testificati sumus in reliquis membris: et praecipue quando caput infirmi debile est natura: et species quidem Cauterii prima est magis salua et melior apud me, et ea utor: operare ergo cum ea in salute.  
For the head is weakened by the disruption of its natural integrity, as we have observed in other limbs, especially when the head of the patient is naturally weak: and the first form of cautery is indeed safer and better in my opinion, and I use it; therefore, perform it with care for health.

¶ De modo cauterizandi capitis in dolore totius capitis.  
On the method of cauterizing the head in cases of pain throughout the entire head.

Caput III.  
Chapter III.

Quando in toto Capite accidit dolor chronicus: et prolongatur illud in infirmo, et utitur hyeris, et cochiis, et caputpurgiis, et oleis, et empleumatis.  
When chronic pain occurs in the whole head and persists in the patient, and he uses syrups, oils, head purgatives, and ointments.

Et praecipue si iam cauterizatum est cauterio uno, quod narrauimus, et non confert ei aliquid ex illis: tunc considera.  
And especially if it has already been cauterized with the cautery we have described, and none of these treatments help him, then consider the following.

Nam si caput infirmi est fortis creationis natura, et non est debile, et inuenit frigus uehemens: tunc cauteriza cum cauterio alio sub illo parum.  
For if the head of the patient is naturally strong and not weak, and he feels intense cold, then cauterize with another cautery slightly below that spot.

Deinde cauteriza ipsum super omne cornu capitis sui cauterio, donec pertranseat cutis, et discooperiatur ex osse quantitas, quam narrauimus.  
Then cauterize on each side of his head with a cautery, until it passes through the skin and exposes the amount of bone we have mentioned.

Et cauteriza ipsum cauterio uno in occipitio capitis sui, in loco qui nominatur uas: et est locus in quo ponitur uentosa: et eleua manum tuam in hoc, et non discooperias os.  
And cauterize him with one cautery on the back of his head, in the place called "vas," where the cup is applied; lift your hand in this, and do not expose the bone.

Infirmus enim inuenit propter illud dolorem uehementem, et diuersum a dolore reliquorum cauteriorum capitis omnium.  
For the patient will feel intense pain from this, unlike the pain from the other head cauteries.

Et ego dicam hoc Cauterium in loco suo: et oportet quod sit cauterium, quo cauterizantur cornua capitis, et occiput eius subtilius cauterio, quo cauterizatur medium capitis.  
And I will describe this cautery in its place: and it is necessary that it is a cautery used for cauterizing the sides and back of the head, more finely than the cautery used on the center of the head.

Et haec est forma eius.  
And this is its form.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in hemicrania, tam cum medicina adustiua, quam cum ferro.  
On the method of cauterizing in hemicrania, both with burning medicine and with iron.

Caput IIII.  
Chapter IV.

Quando accidit in parte capitis dolor et soda, aut extenditur dolor usque ad oculum, et euacuatur cum medicinis mundificantibus caput, et administratur reliqua curatio, quam dixi in diuisionibus aegritudinum, et non confert illud totum: tunc cauterizatio in eo est secundum duos modos: aut cauterizatio cum medicina acuta comburente: aut cum ferro.  
When pain and headache occur in part of the head, or the pain extends to the eye, and the head is cleared with cleansing medicines, and the rest of the treatment I mentioned in the divisions of ailments is applied and still does not help: then cauterization is done in one of two ways: either with a sharp burning medicine, or with iron.

Cauterium autem cum medicina acuta comburente, est, ut accipias dentem unum allii, et excortices ipsum, et abscindas extremitates eius ex duobus lateribus.  
The cauterization with sharp burning medicine involves taking one clove of garlic, peeling it, and cutting off its ends on both sides.

Deinde scinde locum doloris in tempore cum phlebotomo lato, donec fiat in eo locus sub cute, in quo dens capitur.  
Then make an incision at the site of the pain with a broad lancet, until there is a place under the skin where the clove can fit.

Intromitte ergo eum in ipso sub cute, donec occultetur: deinde stringe super ipsum cum puluillo constrictione decenti, et dimitte ipsum quantitate quindecim horarum deinde solue eum, et extrahe allium, et dimitte uulnus duobus diebus, aut tribus: postea pone super ipsum cotum submersum in butyro, donec locus efficiat pus: deinde cura ipsum cum unguento usquequo sanetur.  
Then insert it under the skin until it is hidden: next apply a pad with appropriate pressure over it, and leave it for about fifteen hours, then remove it, extract the garlic, and leave the wound for two or three days; afterward, place a cloth soaked in butter over it until the place produces pus; then treat it with ointment until it heals.

Et si uis fac illud cum quibusdam medicinis comburentibus, quas ego scripsi in tractatu duodecimo, de Medicinis adurentibus.  
And if you wish, perform it with certain burning medicines that I wrote about in the twelfth treatise, on Burning Medicines.

Cauterizatio uero eius cum ferro, est secundum hunc modum.  
The cauterization with iron is done in this way.

Igni Cauterium, cuius forma haec est.  
Heat the cautery tool, which has this form.

Et nominatur Clauilis: quoniam forma capitis eius est sicut forma claui, in quo est quiddam curuitatis, et in medio eius est quaedam eminentia parua.  
It is called the "Clauilis" because the shape of its head resembles a nail, with a slight curve, and in the middle of it is a small protrusion.

Deinde pone ipsum super locum, et retine manum tuam: et tu reuolue ferrum paulatim cum uelocitate: et sit quantitas quae aduritur ex spissitudine cutis, sicut medietas eius.  
Then place it on the spot, hold your hand steady, and gradually rotate the iron with speed; let the amount burned from the skin thickness be about half of it.

Et eleua manum tuam, ut non aduratur arteria, quae est inferius, et accidat fluxus sanguinis.  
And lift your hand so that the artery beneath is not burned, causing a flow of blood.

Deinde imbibe cotum in aqua salis, et pone super locum, et dimitte ipsum tribus diebus: deinde pone cotum cum butyro: postea cura cum unguento usquequo sanetur.  
Then soak a cloth in salt water, place it on the spot, and leave it for three days; then place a cloth with butter on it; afterward treat with ointment until it heals.

Et si uis, cauteriza hanc hemicraniam cum extremitate cultellari secunda Cauterii.  
And if you wish, cauterize this hemicrania with the blade tip of the second cautery.

Et caue ab incisione arteriae in hac hemicrania, non antiqua proprie.  
And be careful not to cut the artery in this hemicrania, as it is not a typical case.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in hemicrania antiqua.  
On the method of cauterizing for an old hemicrania.

Caput V.  
Chapter V.

Quando curas hemicraniam, cum eo quod diximus ex curatione praecedente, et quod diximus in diuisionibus aegritudinum, et non confert curatio: et uides ex aegritudine, quod non sufficit ei quod diximus ex cauterio primo cum medicina, aut cum cauterio cum igne, tunc oportet ut ignias Cauterium cultellare, donec albificetur postquam signas super locum doloris cum linea, cuius longitudo sit medietas digiti, aut quasi illud: et depone manum tuam uice una, et stringe eam donec incidas arteriam, et peruenias usque ad os.  
When you treat hemicrania using the preceding treatment we mentioned and what we described in the divisions of ailments, and the treatment is ineffective, and you see from the illness that what we said about the initial cautery with medicine or with fire is not enough, then you should heat the knife-shaped cautery until it turns white, after marking the area of pain with a line about half a finger’s length or close to that; place your hand down once, and press it until you cut into the artery and reach the bone.

Verumtamen necesse est tibi, ut caueas a continuatione mandibulae, quae mouetur apud masticationem, ne aduratur latus, aut neruus qui mouet ipsam, unde accidat spasmus.  
However, it is necessary for you to avoid the area of the jaw joint, which moves during chewing, to avoid burning the side or the nerve that controls it, lest spasm result.

Et esto super custotiam, et cautelam de fluxu sanguinis arteriae, quae abscisa est.  
And be cautious and careful regarding the blood flow from the artery that is severed.

In abscisione enim eius est timor, et praecipue ei qui ignorat illud quod facit, et non est studiosus, nec expertus: et dimittit operationem cum eo in primis.  
For there is danger in its cutting, especially for one who does not know what he is doing and is neither diligent nor experienced, and abandons the procedure at the first difficulty.

Et ego ueniam cum rememoratione regiminis fluxus sanguinis accidentis ex arteria secundum modum suum, et in loco suo huius libri.  
And I will address the regulation of blood flow that may occur from the artery in its own proper method and place in this book.

Quod si uides ex aegritudine, quod non sufficit ei hoc Cauterium, et uides corpus infirmi tolerans cauterium in medio capitis, sicut narrauimus, cura uulnus, donec sanetur.  
But if you see from the illness that this cautery is insufficient for him, and you see that the patient’s body can tolerate the cautery in the middle of the head, as we have described, treat the wound until it heals.

Et si uis, administra Cauterium, quod diximus in capitulo extractionis arteriae cum Cauterio habente duos cultellos.  
And if you wish, apply the cautery we described in the chapter on artery extraction, using a cautery with two blades.

Est enim cauterizatio melior hac et magis conferens.  
For this cauterization is better than this one and more effective.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in dolore Aurium.  
On the method of cauterizing in cases of ear pain.

Caput VI.  
Chapter VI.

Quando accidit in Aure dolor a frigore, et curatur cum solutiuis, et reliqua curatione quam diximus in diuisione, et non remouetur dolor, tunc igni Cauterium: et est illud quod nominatur Punctum, cuius forma est haec.  
When pain in the ear occurs due to cold, and it is treated with loosening agents and the remaining treatment mentioned in the division, and the pain does not subside, then heat the cautery, called "the Point," which has this shape.

Deinde puncta cum eo post eius ignitionem in circumitu auris totius, sicut reuoluitur: aut in circumitu utriusque, si simul dolor est in utrisque.  
Then, after heating it, use it to puncture around the entire ear, as it is revolved, or around both if there is pain in both ears at the same time.

Et elonga te cum Cauterio a radice auris parumper, postquam signaueris loca cum encaustro: et sit cauterii quantitas decem punctorum in omni aure, et quasi illa.  
And keep the cautery a little distance from the root of the ear after marking the locations with ink; let the amount of cautery be about ten points on each ear, or so.

Deinde cura loca, donec sanentur.  
Then treat the locations until they heal.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in Tortura.  
On the method of cauterizing in cases of Tortura.

Caput VII.  
Chapter VII.

Tortura quae curatur cum cauterizatione, non est nisi ex speciebus, quae accidunt ex phlegmate, secundum quod diximus in diuisionibus aegritudinum.  
The tortura treated with cauterization is only one of the conditions that arise from phlegm, as we said in the divisions of ailments.

Et deuita cauterizationem speciei, quae accidit ex exiccatione, et spasmo nerui.  
And avoid cauterizing the type that arises from dryness and nerve spasm.

Quando curas hanc speciem torturae cum hyeris, et caputpurgiis, et gargarismis, et non confert curatio tua, tunc oportet ut cauterizes infirmum tribus Cauteriis: uno apud radicem auris, et secundo parum inferius a timpore suo, et tertio apud coniunctionem duorum labiorum.  
When you treat this type of tortura with syrups, head purgatives, and gargles, and your treatment is ineffective, then you should cauterize the patient in three places: one at the root of the ear, the second a little lower near the temple, and the third at the junction of the two lips.

Et pone Cauterium tuum ex contrario partis infirmae.  
And place your cautery on the side opposite the weakened area.

Quoniam mollificatio non accidit, nisi in parte quae apparet sana.  
For the relaxation does not occur except in the part that appears healthy.

Et forma cauterizationis est, ut cauterizes ipsum Cauterio in directo extremitatis auris superioris sub cornu capitis parum: et alio in timpore: cuius longitudo sit secundum longitudinem pollicis.  
And the procedure for cauterization is to cauterize him with the cautery directly below the upper end of the ear near the horn of the head a little; and another at the temple, the length of which is the length of a thumb.

Et depone manum tuam cum cauterio, donec comburatur quantitas medietatis spissitudinis cutis.  
And place your hand down with the cautery until half the thickness of the skin is burned.

Et haec est forma Cauterii, et est species ex cultellari, cuius forma praemissa est: uerumtamen non est subtilior ea parum, sicut uides: et oportet ut sit cultellus in eo superfluae grossitudinis parum.  
And this is the form of the cautery, which is a type of knife, whose shape was previously shown; however, it is a bit less delicate, as you see, and it should be slightly thicker than necessary.

Deinde cura loca, cum eo quod praecessit: donec sanetur.  
Then treat the locations with the same treatment as before until they heal.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in Apoplexia antiqua.  
On the method of cauterizing in cases of chronic apoplexy.

Caput VIII.  
Chapter VIII.

Quando antiquatur Apoplexia, et curas eam cum eo quod diximus, et non confert medicatio tua, et infirmus non habet febrem, tunc cauteriza eum quatuor cauteriis super omne cornu capitis sui, et cauterio in medio capitis, sicut diximus, et cauterio in occipitio capitis, secundum quod praecessit: et modus cauterii sit secundum quod praecessit.  
When apoplexy has become chronic, and you treat it as we have described, and your treatment is ineffective, and the patient does not have a fever, then cauterize him with four cauteries on each corner of his head, a cautery in the middle of the head as we have discussed, and a cautery at the back of the head, as previously outlined. The method for these cauteries should follow the previously described approach.

Etiam cauterizatur cum Cauterio super os stomachi: et est magis ultimum.  
Additionally, apply a cautery over the pit of the stomach, which is the last step in this method.

Deinde curetur, cum eo quod praecessit.  
Then treat the cauterized areas following the earlier instructions.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in Obliuione.  
On the method of cauterizing for cases of memory loss.

Caput IX.  
Chapter IX.

Oportet ut in potu dentur in primis infirmo hyerae magnae, et pillulae mundificantes cerebrum.  
To begin, the patient should be given a drink containing potent hyeerae and pills that purify the brain.

Deinde rade caput infirmi totum, et pone super occipitium eius emplastrum de synapi descriptum in tractatu emplastrorum: et pone ipsum aliquotiens.  
Then, shave the patient's entire head and apply a mustard plaster over the occiput, as described in the chapter on plasters, and repeat this application several times.

Ipsum namque est modus Cauterii: et fac illud secundum ordinem eundem, quem dixi illic.  
This serves as a form of cautery, and perform it according to the sequence described in that section.

Si ergo sanatur per illud, bonum est.  
If the patient is healed by this, it is well.

Si non: tunc fac ei cauteria tria in posteriori parte capitis sui, quae sint descendentia a superiori parte capitis usque ad inferiorem partem oculi: et pone inter omne cauterium grossitudinem digiti.  
If not, then make three cauteries on the back of the head, extending from the top of the head down to the lower part of the eye, spacing each cautery a finger’s width apart.

Deinde cura Cauterium, cum eo quod praecessit.  
Then treat the cauteries as previously instructed.

Si autem uis additionem, et infirmus est tolerans illud: tunc fac ei Cauterium in medio.  
If you want to add more, and the patient can tolerate it, then make an additional cautery in the middle.

Quod si uis additionem: tunc cauteriza ipsum super duo cornua.  
If further treatment is desired, then cauterize over the two horns of the head.

Deinde cura ipsum, donec sanetur: sitque Cauterium oliuare secundum formam, quae praemissa est.  
Then treat the patient until he is healed, using the olive-shaped cautery as previously described.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in Paralisi, et mollificatione.  
On the method of cauterizing in cases of paralysis and muscle relaxation.

Caput X.  
Chapter X.

Oportet ut procedas in mundificatione capitis cum hyeeris, et cum eo quod diximus.  
It is necessary to proceed with cleansing the head using hyeeras and according to the previously described methods.

Deinde rade caput infirmi: postea cauteriza ipsum cauterio in medio capitis, et cauterio super omne cornu capitis, et cauterio super posterius ipsius, et tribus super spondiles colli.  
Then shave the patient’s head; afterward, apply a cautery in the middle of the head, a cautery on each corner of the head, one on the back, and three on the vertebrae of the neck.

Si autem indiges in aegritudine mollificatione corporis pluribus illis, et infirmus est tolerans, et aegritudo est fortis: tunc cauteriza ipsum quatuor cauteriis super spondiles dorsi, et ultima cum cauterio, donec comburatur ex cute plurimum eius, et eleua manum tuam.  
If the ailment requires more extensive muscle relaxation and the patient can tolerate it, and the ailment is severe, then apply four cauteries on the vertebrae of the back, with the last cautery burning deep into the skin until much of it is seared, then lift your hand.

Deinde cura ipsum, donec sanetur: et sic cauterium Oliuare.  
Then treat him until he is healed, using the olive-shaped cautery in this manner.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in Epilepsia.  
On the method of cauterizing in cases of epilepsy.

Caput XI.  
Chapter XI.

Non Cauterizatur epilepticus, nisi ille cuius epilepsia est ex phlegmate.  
Epileptics should only be cauterized if their condition is caused by phlegm.

Oportet ergo ut mundifices cerebrum eius in primis cum hyeris, et cum reliqua curatione quam diximus in diuisione: quando infirmus est magnus, aut est tolerans in accaeptione medicinarum.  
Thus, the brain must first be cleansed with hyeeras and other treatments mentioned in the section on disease division, especially if the patient is an adult or can tolerate medicines.

Si autem est puer, qui non tolerat medicinas, tunc administratur gargarisma, et masticationes mundificantes cerebrum, ante illud diebus multis cum bonificatione ciborum.  
However, if the patient is a child and cannot tolerate medication, administer gargles and chewing agents that purify the brain over several days along with good dietary practices.

Deinde rade caput eius, postea cauteriza ipsum Cauterio uno in medio capitis, secundum quod praecessit in modo: et cauterio alio in occipitio eius, et super omne cornu capitis sui cauterio.  
Then, shave his head, and apply one cautery in the middle of the head as previously outlined, one on the back of the head, and one on each corner of the head.

Quod si infirmus fuerit fortis, et fuerit tolerans, tunc cauteriza ipsum cauteriis, quae dixi in habente paralisim, et mollificationem corporis super spondiles colli, et spondiles dorsi: et sit Cauterium oliuare secundum modum qui praecessit.  
If the patient is strong and can tolerate it, apply cauteries as described for paralysis and body relaxation, along the cervical and dorsal vertebrae, using the olive-shaped cautery as previously described.

Si autem infirmus est puer, tunc pone cauterium subtile, secundum hunc modum.  
For children, however, use a finer cautery, following this approach.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in Melancholia.  
On the method of cauterizing in cases of melancholy.

Caput XII.  
Chapter XII.

Quando caussa melancholiae sunt humiditates corruptae, et phlegma grossum, tunc cauteriza ipsum Cauteriis, quae diximus in habente paralisim.  
When melancholy arises from corrupted humors and thick phlegm, apply cauteries as described for paralysis.

Et si caussa melancholiae est superfluitas decliuis ad melancholiam, et est corpus infirmi humectum, tunc da ei in potu quod mundificet cerebrum eius, secundum quod praecessit in diuisione: deinde rade caput infirmi, et fac postea tortellam decenter factam ex lino, sicut circulum.  
If melancholy is caused by an excess leading to melancholic conditions and the patient's body is moist, then give him a drink to purify the brain as described earlier; then shave his head and make a linen band like a small ring.

Deinde pone ipsam in medio capitis: et infirmus sedens quadratus teneatur ab omni parte.  
Place it on the center of the head, and have the patient sit in a stable, upright position held on all sides.

Deinde praesenta linimentum.  
Next, prepare a liniment.

Et est dignius ex butyro ouium antiquo.  
This is best made from aged sheep’s butter.

Et calefac ipsum super ignem calefactione temperata mensura, quam toleret digitus, quando intromittitur in ipsum.  
Warm it gently over a fire to a temperature that a finger can comfortably withstand when dipped in.

Deinde euacua ipsum in medio capitis eius in circulo, et dimitte ipsum donec infrigidetur.  
Then pour it onto the middle of the head in the ring, and leave it until it cools.

Fac illud infirmo omni hebdomada semel, cum reliquo regimine eius bono, donec sanetur.  
Repeat this treatment once weekly, in combination with a proper regimen, until he is healed.

Aut si uis, cauteriza ipsum faciendo puncta parua plurimum eius, praeter quod teneas manum cum cauterio: imo sit odorando.  
Alternatively, if desired, cauterize the patient by making numerous small punctures, carefully managing the cautery, ideally with a light touch.

Haec enim species cauterii humectat cerebrum cum qualitate.  
This form of cautery moistens the brain beneficially.

Deinde pone super ipsum cotum imbibitum in butyro, aut adipe gallinae.  
Then apply a pad soaked in butter or chicken fat.

On the method of cauterizing for water descending to the eye.

Caput XIII.  
Chapter XIII.

Quando declaratur tibi principium aquae in oculo per signa, quae dixi in diuisione, tunc incipe et pota infirmum, cum eo quod mundificet caput eius.  
When signs of fluid accumulation in the eye become evident, as discussed in the disease divisions, begin by giving the patient something that will purify the head.

Et custodi ipsum ab omnibus humiditatibus, et fac eum sedere in balneo, in ieiunio diebus aliquot.  
Keep the patient away from all sources of moisture, and have him sit in a steam bath while fasting for several days.

Deinde praecipe ei, ut radat caput suum, et cauteriza ipsum in medio capitis sui.  
Then instruct him to shave his head, and apply a cautery to the middle of the head.

Deinde cauteriza ipsum super duo timpora duobus cauteriis, si est incaeptio descensus aquae in duobus oculis simul: aut ex latere uno, si est incaeptio aquae in oculo uno: et abscinde cum cauterio omnes uenas et arterias quae sunt sub cute: et sint cauteria in ea longa in latitudine timporum: et caue a fluxu sanguinis.  
Next, cauterize both temples if the fluid is affecting both eyes or on one side if it is in one eye, severing all veins and arteries under the skin with the cautery. These cauteries should extend along the temples' width; take care to avoid excessive blood flow.

Quod si uides aliquid ex eo, tunc abscinde eum statim cum quacumque medicina est possibile: et ego ueniam cum sapientia, quae est in extractione arteriarum, et abscisione earum, et cautela a fluxu sanguinis.  
If bleeding is observed, immediately cut it off with whatever means possible. I will elaborate further on artery extraction and cutting and ways to prevent excessive blood flow.

Et quandoque cauterizatur in ceruice, sub duobus ossibus, duobus cauteriis ultime productis.  
In some cases, two cauteries should be applied at the back of the neck, beneath the two bones, as a final step.

On the method of cauterizing in cases of chronic tearing.

tunc cauteriza cauterio eodem quod narraui in incaeptione aquae descendentis, cauterio in medio capitis, et duobus cauteriis super duo timpora, et duobus cauteriis in ceruice sub duobus ossibus.

¶ De modo cauterizandi Palpebram cum pili inuersantur pungentes oculum.  
On the method of cauterizing the eyelid when inverted hairs prick the eye.  

Caput XVII.  
Chapter 17.  

Cauterium in eo est secundum duos modos: aut cauterium cum igne: aut cauterium cum medicina comburente.  
The cautery for this is of two types: either cautery with fire or cautery with a burning medicine.  

In cauterio autem cum igne, praecipe infirmo ante illud, ut dimittat pilos suos, si est ex illis qui mundant eos, donec prolongentur et aequentur.  
In cauterizing with fire, instruct the patient beforehand to let his hairs grow out, if he is one who trims them, until they are long and even.  

Si autem pungunt eum apud ortum suum: tunc constringe oculos eius cum uitta, ut non moueantur, donec nascantur.  
But if they prick him at their root, then bind his eyes with a band so that they do not move, until they grow.  

Cum ergo nascuntur et aequantur, tunc pone caput infirmi in sinu tuo: deinde signa super Palpebram oculorum eius cum encaustro, signo quod sit secundum figuram folii myrti: et sit principium signi prope pilos.  
When they grow out and are even, then place the patient’s head in your lap, then mark on his eyelid with ink in the shape of a myrtle leaf, starting the mark near the hairs.  

Deinde pone cotum imbibitum in albumine oui: aut in mucilagine psilii super oculum.  
Then place a cloth soaked in egg white or in psyllium mucilage over the eye.  

Deinde igni cauterium, cuius forma est haec.  
Then heat the cautery, the form of which is this.  

Deinde cauteriza super figuram, quam signasti paulatim in uicibus multis, donec comburatur superficies cutis, quae est sicut figura folii myrti, totam apparentem proprie.  
Then cauterize along the shape you marked gradually in many strokes, until the surface of the skin, shaped like the myrtle leaf, is properly burned entirely.  

Et signum sanitatis operis tui est: ut si uideas Palpebram oculi iam contrahi, et pilos eleuari apud puncturam oculi.  
And the sign of success in your work is if you see the eyelid of the eye begin to contract and the hairs lift away from the puncture of the eye.  

Eleua ergo manum tuam tunc: et dimitte ipsum tribus diebus.  
Then lift your hand and leave it alone for three days.  

Deinde pone super ipsum Cotum cum butyro, donec eradicetur eschara.  
Then place a cloth with butter on it until the scab is removed.  

Deinde cura ipsum cum unguento, donec sanetur.  
Then treat it with ointment until it is healed.  

Quod si redeat aliquod ex pilis post horam, et mollificetur palpebra: tunc itera Cauterium super illum locum, sicut fecisti in primis.  
But if some hairs return after a while, and the eyelid softens, then repeat the cauterization on that spot as you did the first time.  

Si autem pili sunt in inferiori: tunc cauteriza ipsam, donec eleuetur ad locum suum naturalem: et aequetur ut non pungant pili oculum.  
But if the hairs are in the lower part, then cauterize it until it is lifted to its natural position and leveled so that the hairs no longer prick the eye.

¶ De modo cauterizandi cum medicina comburente in eodem casu.  
On the method of cauterizing with a burning medicine in the same case.  

Caput XVIII.  
Chapter 18.  

Est ut praecipias infirmo, ut dimittat pilos, donec prolongentur et aequentur.  
Instruct the patient to let his hairs grow until they are long and even.  

Fac ex charta de panno formam folii myrti.  
Make a myrtle-leaf shape from a cloth paper.  

Deinde accipe ex sapone cognito, et ex calce non imbibita ex unoquoque pondus uncia I aut quasi illud: deinde tere ea simul contritione bona, et fac illud cum festinatione, ut non infrigidetur.  
Then take a known soap and unslaked lime, each in a weight of one ounce or close to that; then grind them together thoroughly, and do this quickly so it does not cool.  

Deinde ex pande ex eo super chartam quam fecisti sicut formam folii myrti, et pone ipsum super palpebram oculi unius, aut duorum.  
Then spread this mixture over the paper you made in the shape of a myrtle leaf, and place it on the eyelid of one or both eyes.  

Et pone sub oculo cotum imbibitum in albumine oui: et caput infirmi sit in sinu tuo.  
And place a cloth soaked in egg white under the eye, and the patient's head should rest in your lap.  

Et pone digitum tuum indicem super medicinam, et preme ipsum parumper.  
Place your index finger on the medicine and press it gently.  

Et tu moueas ipsum quotiens sentit infirmus mordicationem medicinae: quoniam inuenit eius mordicatio sicut ignis.  
Move it each time the patient feels the medicine’s stinging, as he perceives its sting like fire.  

Dum ergo perseuerat hoc quod ipse inuenit mordicatio, tunc dimitte medicinam, et moue ipsam cum digito tuo.  
As long as he continues to feel the sting, leave the medicine in place and move it with your finger.  

Cum ergo quiescit mordicatio, tunc remoue medicinam, et ablue oculum cum aqua.  
When the stinging stops, remove the medicine and wash the eye with water.  

Et considera tunc, si uides palpebram eleuari, sicut eleuatur apud contractionem cum igne, aut cum incisione, est bene: et si non, tunc itera super ipsam ex medicina super locum, in quo non imprimit medicina, et non denigrat, donec aequetur operatio tua, et contrahatur oculus.  
Then observe if the eyelid rises, as it would from contraction by fire or incision; if so, it is good. If not, reapply the medicine to the area where it did not take effect and did not darken, until your work is balanced and the eye contracts.  

Deinde pone super ipsum Cotum cum butyro, donec eradicetur cutis adustionis.  
Then place a cloth with butter over it until the burned skin is removed.  

Deinde cura ipsum cum unguento diaphinicon, aut alio, donec sanetur.  
Then treat it with diaphinicon ointment or another ointment until it heals.  

Et necesse est tibi apud operationem, ut custodias ultima custodia, ne in oculum cadat aliquid ex medicina.  
During the procedure, you must exercise utmost care to ensure that none of the medicine falls into the eye.  

Quod si mollificatur palpebra post dies, et pugnat ex capillis aliquid in oculo, tunc itera medicinam super illum locum, qui mollificatur ex palpebra proprie, sicut fecisti in primis.  
If the eyelid softens after a few days and any hairs prick the eye, then repeat the medicine on the specific part of the eyelid that softened, as you did at first.  

Deinde cura ipsum, donec sanetur.  
Then treat it until it heals.  

Et haec est forma folii myrti.  
And this is the shape of the myrtle leaf.  

Et scias, quod oculi hominum diuersificantur in magnitudine, et paruitate.  
Know that human eyes vary in size and smallness.  

Ergo secundum illud sit consideratio tua: et non occultatur uia rectitudinis illi, cui est studium de hac arte.  
Therefore, let your consideration be according to this, for the way of righteousness is not hidden from one dedicated to this art.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in Fistula lachrymali.  
On the method of cauterizing in the case of a tear duct fistula.  

Caput XIX.  
Chapter 19.  

Quando curas Fistulam, cum eo quod diximus in diuisionibus aegritudinum, et non confert curatio tua: tunc, oportet ut cauterizes ipsam secundum hunc modum.  
When you treat a fistula using what we discussed in the divisions of diseases and your treatment is ineffective, then you should cauterize it according to this method.  

Praecipe infirmo, ut ponat caput suum in sinu tuo, et teneat caput eius minister inter manus suas taliter, ut non moueatur, neque agitetur cum capite suo.  
Instruct the patient to place his head in your lap, and have an assistant hold his head in his hands so that it remains still and does not move.  

Deinde pone Cotum infusum in albugine oui, aut in mucilagine psilii super oculum eius: deinde igni Cauterium, cuius forma est haec.  
Then place a cloth soaked in egg white or in a psillium mucilage over his eye; next, heat the cautery, which has this form.  

Sit concauum, sicut forma cannae pennae uulturis ab extremitate una, cum qua fit Cauterium: et si uis sit penetrans usque ad extremitatem aliam, et si uis sit sursum sic tenta.  
It should be concave, like the shape of the tip of a vulture’s feather on one end, with which the cauterization is performed; and if you wish, it can be penetrating to the other end, and, if you prefer, it should be raised in this way.  

Verumtamen hoc concauum est melius ad opus tuum.  
However, this concave form is better for your work.  

Deinde exprime fistulam, si est aperta, et extrahe ex ea saniem, et exicca ipsam: et si non est aperta, tunc aperi eam, et extrahe pus eius.  
Then squeeze the fistula, if it is open, to drain the fluid, and dry it; if it is not open, then open it and extract the pus.  

Deinde pone tunc super eam Cauterium, quod fit ignitum ualde.  
Then place on it the cautery, which has been heated very much.  

Et tene cum eo manum tuam parumper apud Cauterium ab oculo, ad partem nasi, ut non erret manus tua, aut inquietetur infirmus: et ponas Cauterium in adipe oculi, et corrumpas ipsum.  
And hold your hand steady on the cautery, positioned away from the eye towards the nose, so that your hand does not slip or the patient become disturbed; place the cautery on the fatty tissue of the eye and destroy it.  

Si ergo peruenis in principio Cauterii tui usque ad os, erit bene.  
If with the beginning of your cauterization you reach the bone, it will be good.  

Et si non: tunc itera Cauterium semel, aut bis, si indiges illo: et dimitte illud tribus diebus.  
If not, then repeat the cauterization once or twice as needed, and leave it for three days.  

Deinde pone Cotum cum butyro, et cura ipsum cum unguento exiccatiuo, donec sanetur.  
Then apply a cloth with butter and treat it with a drying ointment until it heals.  

Quod si praeterierint ei quadraginta dies, et non sanatur: tunc pone super ipsum medicinam acutam corrosiuam, donec detegatur os: et rade ipsum secundum quod uenit in capitulo suo.  
If forty days pass and it does not heal, then apply a sharp, corrosive medicine until the bone is exposed, and scrape it as described in the relevant chapter.  

Et est modus alius Cauterii fistulae, quem quidam primi dixerunt.  
There is another method of cauterizing a fistula that some early practitioners have described.  

Intende ad locum fistulae, et finde ipsum: deinde pone in ipsam fissuram camahan subtile, id est, Cannulam subtilem, cuius forma haec est.  
Direct your attention to the location of the fistula, split it open, and place a fine rod, that is, a thin tube, into the fissure, shaped like this.  

Et funde in ipsum quantitatem drachmae ex plumbo liquefacto, et tene manum tuam cum Canna retentione bona, et non moueatur infirmus omnino, ut non currat, uel perueniat plumbum liquefactum ad oculum eius.  
Pour into it an amount of a drachma of molten lead, holding your hand steady on the tube with a good grip, and ensure the patient does not move at all, so that the lead does not flow or reach his eye.  

Et oportet ut ponas super oculum infirmi cotum infusum in albumine oui, aut in aqua.  
It is essential to place a cloth soaked in egg white or water over the patient’s eye.  

Plumbum enim comburit locum fistulae, et sanat ipsam sanatione mirabili.  
The lead burns the site of the fistula and heals it with remarkable efficacy.  

Quod si sanatur fistula, cum eo quod diximus ex Cauterio, et curatione, est bonum.  
If the fistula heals with the cautery and treatment as we described, it is good.  

Et si non, tunc necesse est administrare foramen nasi, et reducere fistulam ad cursum nasi, secundum quod ueniet in loco suo sibi proprio.  
If not, then it is necessary to create an opening in the nose and redirect the fistula towards the nasal passage, as it naturally should be.

On the method of cauterizing an oral fistula.

¶ De modo cauterizandi in dolore Dentium.  
On the method of cauterizing for tooth pain.

Chapter 23.

Quando dolor dentis est propter frigiditatem, aut in ipso est frigus, aut uermis, et non confert curatio cum medicinis: tunc Cauterium in ipso est secundum duos modos: aut Cauterium cum butyro: aut cauterium cum igne.  
When tooth pain is due to coldness or worms in the tooth, and medicinal treatments are ineffective, there are two methods of cauterization: cauterization with butter or with fire.  

Cauterium autem eius cum butyro est, ut accipias butyrum uaccinum, et bullias ipsum in cochleari ferreo, aut in uenera.  
For cauterization with butter, take cow’s butter and boil it in an iron spoon or small vessel.

Then take a piece of cloth and wrap it around the tip of a probe.

For cauterization with fire, use a copper or iron tube.  

Et sit in corpore eius quiddam grossitudinis, ut non perueniat acuitas ignis ad os infirmi.  
The body of the tube should be thick enough to prevent the intense heat from reaching the patient’s mouth.  

Deinde igni Cauterium, cuius forma ueniet: et pone ipsum super illum dentem: et tene manum tuam, donec infrigidetur Cauterium.  
Then heat the cautery in the specified form, place it over the tooth, and hold your hand steady until the cautery cools.  

Fac illud aliquotiens, nam dolor remouebitur, aut in illo eodem die, aut die alio.  
Repeat this several times; the pain will dissipate either that day or the next.  

Et oportet in impressione illius Cauterii, ut impleat infirmus os suum buyro bono, et teneat ipsum hora una: deinde exspuat ipsum.  
After applying the cautery, have the patient fill his mouth with good butter, hold it there for an hour, and then spit it out.  

Et haec est forma Cauterii.  
This is the form of the cautery.
