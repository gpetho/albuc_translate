When dislocation comes to anyone, they should try to reduce it immediately.
For if one delays, the place becomes inflamed, and perhaps it will not be set back in its proper place. Therefore one should not attempt to move it when it is inflamed: many times this brings about spasms and other pains;
When this occurs, nevertheless, wash the patient, then leave him until the inflammation is appeased; then raise the limb with warm water and oil, then gently reset it, and care for each limb as it comes back into its place.
And I have ordered the chapters of the dislocations from head to foot.
of the jaws
Sometimes the jaws become dislocated upwards, and their dislocation occurs in two ways: either they are a little displaced from their places, and softened somewhat; or they are entirely dislocated, hanging towards the chest, with saliva flowing out of the mouth so that it cannot be held back, nor can the mouth be closed, and speech is impeded by the tongue.
When dislocation is slight, it returns spontaneously in most cases.
And if it is complete and total, then you must reset it quickly without delay;
And in this manner that the servant holds the patient's head, and the physician places a pouch of his one hand at the root of the jaw dislocation inside the mouth if the dislocation is on both sides; and the other two hands should be free to keep it even; and instructs the patient to soften his jaws and move them in all directions.
And the physician places it back until it returns to its proper place
And if the dislocation is severe, especially if both jaws are dislocated together, then raise them with hot water and oil until they become slightly softened; do not force the repositioning, as we have said
And when they are repositioned correctly, and the patient closes his mouth without softening, then place wet cloths made with a salve of rose oil and wax on it, and bind it lightly with soft bandages
and the patient should sleep on his back with two pillows under his shoulders so that he does not move to the right or left and does not attempt to chew but takes only soft food until the pain is gone and the jaws are at rest
and then he may chew what he wants easily, and should not force his mouth open while eating or drinking until the jaws are fully healed and strong
And if both jaws cannot be put back in place within an hour and are not returned to their proper positions, then fever and chills may occur, sometimes with diarrhea and occasionally vomiting of clear bile
And when you see these signs, know that it is a loss of life; many times the person dies within eight days

Chapter on the dislocation of the shoulder
Know that the shoulder is dislocated outward in three ways: firstly from the lower part of the socket, secondly from the side of the chest, and occasionally from above, which happens rarely
And the shoulder does not dislocate backwards nor inward towards the nerves; often it dislocates downward toward the socket, especially in those with little flesh, for then it springs out quickly; but in those with abundant flesh, it barely springs out and scarcely returns
And sometimes there occurs a blow or fall which causes the shoulder to swell with a hot hematoma and the person believes that it is dislocated because of this
Therefore you must examine him closely until you are certain; then begin treatment
And it is known when the dislocation has occurred downward toward the socket, for there will be a noticeable difference between the diseased shoulder and the healthy one, with clear manifestations in between and you will find a hollow at the top of the shoulder and beneath the socket a touch like that of an egg, and the patient cannot bring his hand to his neck nor move it in any way
and likewise if it is dislocated upward, it is not hidden but can be felt distinctly upon touching
and this manner of dislocation cannot be reduced except when it is new or if the patient is a child
And the method is for the practitioner to raise the patient's hand upwards, then place his fingers beneath the socket and forcefully raise the joint upwards into its proper place, and then lower the hand downwards
And if it does not come back as we have described within several days afterwards, then the patient must be bathed in warm water and given softening and soothing remedies to drink, such as a decoction of mallow, fenugreek, and melilot in water, and he should be treated accordingly
then have the patient lie on his back with a piece of cloth folded into a roll placed beneath the socket, not too soft nor too hard, then place the practitioner's heel upon the cloth and gently raise the diseased shoulder while pulling the patient's hand in this way
and another practitioner should hold the patient's head so that it does not move downwards; then the shoulder will return immediately into its proper place, and you may reduce it in this manner
and have present one man who is larger than the patient, standing on the diseased side with his shoulder placed beneath the socket of the patient's arm and raising it upwards so that the patient hangs in the air and another practitioner pulls the patient's arm downwards towards his belly. And if the patient is too light, have him hang with some heavy object and the dislocation will return into its proper place
And it may also be reduced in another manner thus: take a long stick and fix it firmly into the ground so that its end is as high as a man's fist and neither too thick nor too thin then place it beneath the patient, but let him have soft cloths underneath to prevent injury, and have him stand upon the end of the stick, then extend his arm downward forcefully on the other side and the joint will quickly return into its proper place
and if it will not return by all the methods we have described, then do this treatment: take a stick two and a half feet long and four fingers wide with a rounded top to allow easy entry into the socket beneath the arm in this way
then place soft cloths upon the top of the stick to prevent injury to the patient, then place it beneath the socket of the diseased arm; extend the patient's other arm upwards along the length of the stick and tie the elbow to the stick with a bandage, then place the forearm upon a support made from a piece of wood about half an inch thick; let the hand hang downwards on one side and allow the body to hang freely on the other side: the joint will now return into its proper place
And when it has returned into its proper place, however this may have happened, then you must place a bandage of wool beneath the socket, sprinkle the whole shoulder and surrounding area with powdered mallow leaves mixed with incense and egg-white, then draw the bandage tight beneath the arm and tie the ends securely around the emplastrum; fasten the hand to its collarbone and let it remain in this position
And it is necessary that he fast for a few days until the member is strengthened, for it will heal more quickly; and then after five or six days he may eat again and move about, and if it remains firm without becoming softened, then it is healed.

And if you do everything, and it remains dislocated after seven days, and the shoulder cannot be raised above the arm, then know that the nerves at the head of the shoulder are cut or stretched or softened; and know that the joint will never again be fixed in its place.
Dislocations that occur toward the chest or back are caused by pushing or pulling with great force, and bandages should be applied to support them until they are healed.
And if after the healing there is stiffness in the limb and difficulty in movement, then let the patient exercise many times until that stiffness is alleviated and returns to its original condition.
of dislocation of the neck

And if the joint is firmly fixed in its place, remove the bandage and leave it alone. But if you see that the joint is not yet stable, then begin again with the compress and bandaging, leaving it on for a few more days until it is securely fixed, before removing it.

If it returns now, that is good.

dislocations of the hip
Know that the joints of the hip and shoulder only suffer dislocations, unlike other joints, and the joint of the hip is dislocated according to four parts: inside and outside, front and back, with more dislocations on the inside than elsewhere, and few dislocations in front and back.
And the sign of an inner hip dislocation is that when the diseased leg is placed next to the healthy one, it appears longer and the diseased knee joint opens wider than the healthy one, and the diseased leg cannot bend at the knee. and the location of this dislocations is indicated by the presence of a hot swelling or abscess near the hip joint
And the sign of one that comes on the outside is that it is opposite to those that have come before
And the sign of one that happens in front is that he walks well with his leg completed; nonetheless, he cannot bend his knee without pain, and if he wants to walk forward, he cannot do so, and he retains his urine, and the angle swells, and when he walks, he walks on his heel.
And the sign of one that happens behind is that he cannot straighten or bend his knee, and his leg is twisted compared to the other one, and the angle is softened, and the hip joint is located beneath the ribs.
The treatment of this type of hip dislocation is that you should ascertain whether the dislocation is recent or old, and if it has remained in its position for some time, then there is no need for treatment; but one must not make assumptions about this.
And in the case of a fresh hip dislocation, which can occur in four ways, you should quickly put the joint back into place, and stretch it inside and outside, and move it to the right or left; perhaps it will return to its proper position, and no other treatment is necessary.
And if it does not return to its proper position in this way, then you should prepare one strong assistant and stretch the leg strongly with both hands or use a rope tied above the kneejoint and another assistant places their hands under the shoulders and then ties a soft rope around the hip, while another holds onto the end of the rope
and all pull upwards and downwards at once so that the patient is lifted off the ground and remains suspended
This method of stretching applies to all four types of hip dislocation, and if the dislocation has returned to its proper position as we have said, then that is good.
But if not, then you must treat it according to its type. If the dislocation is on the inside of the hip joint, then you should have the patient lie on their healthy side, put a rope around the root of the thigh and below the angle, then pull the rope up towards the angle and the groin
then another assistant should take the patient's arms and embrace the large area of the diseased thigh and stretch it outwards forcefully: now it will return to its proper position, and this type of dislocation is easier than the others.
And if you cannot reduce it using the method I have described, then you should tie both of the patient's feet together with a soft rope around both ankles and both knees, and stretch the diseased leg two spans further than the other one
Then he should be hung from the head of a beam, and let him hang two arm-lengths above the ground; then command one minister to embrace the top of the thigh, and let another minister hang with the patient embracing the thigh tightly, for the joints return quickly now.
