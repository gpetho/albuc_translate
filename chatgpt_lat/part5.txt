¶ De utilitate Phlebotomiae, et cautelis in ea obseruandis.	On the usefulness of bloodletting, and the precautions to observe in it.
Qualitas autem Phlebotomiae, et accidentia eius ex ea, in cuius rectificatione oportet te cogitare.	The nature of bloodletting and its complications, about which you must think to correct.
Primum ergo illius est de quo oportet ut scias, quod phlebotomia non administratur, nisi in conseruatione sanitatis, et perseuerantia eius, et cautela ab aduentu aegritudinum.	Firstly, you must know that bloodletting is only practiced for the preservation of health, its persistence, and as a precaution against the onset of diseases.
Et ut fiat phlebotomia in una uenarum trium quae sunt in cubito, scilicet Cephalica, et Fusca, et Basilica.	And so that bloodletting may be done on one of the three veins in the elbow, namely the Cephalic, the Dark, and the Basilic.
Et ut fiat phlebotomia in principio Veris super ieiunium, in uere, et aestate: et autumno, et hyeme post comestionem, quando apparent significationes repletionis.	And so that bloodletting may be done at the start of Spring before fasting, in spring and summer, and in autumn and winter after eating, when signs of fullness appear.
Et fiat phlebotomia in die primo, aut in tertio, postquam praeterierint de die tres horae.	And let bloodletting be done on the first or third day, after three hours have passed since daybreak.
Phlebotomia autem quae administratur in aegritudinibus, non habet horam determinatam: sed quando prouocat conuenientia et necessitas ad illud, in nocte, aut die, et in omni hora, et in omni tempore.	However, bloodletting practiced in illnesses does not have a fixed hour; rather, when convenience and necessity call for it, it may be done at night, or day, and at any hour, and at any time.
Et non oportet, ut phlebotomentur pueri, donec ueniant super eos quatuordecim anni.	And children should not be bled until they have reached fourteen years of age.
Neque phlebotometur mulier praegnans, nisi a mense quarto usque ad septimum.	Nor should a pregnant woman be bled, except from the fourth to the seventh month.
Neque phlebotomentur senes, qui iam pertransierunt sexaginta annos.	Nor should the elderly, who have already passed sixty years, be bled.
Cum ergo aliquis uult phlebotomari propter quemcumque modum sit: oportet, ut mundificet intestina sua ante phlebotomiam cum clisteri leni, si in eis est stercus multum retentum: ut non attrahant uenae apud phlebotomiam ex intestinis superfluitates putridas nocentes membris principalibus.	Therefore, when anyone wants to be bled for any reason, they should cleanse their intestines before bloodletting with a gentle enema, if much stool is retained, so that the veins do not draw from the intestines putrid superfluities that harm the principal organs during bloodletting.
Neque phlebotometur patiens toctina, id est, satietatem nauseatiuam: neque crapulatus, neque euadens ab ebrietate, donec remoueatur illud ab eis.	Nor should a person suffering from toctina, that is, a nauseating fullness, nor a gluttonous one, nor one recovering from drunkenness, be bled until these conditions are cleared from them.
Neque infirmus cum hydropisi.	Nor should a person sick with dropsy.
Et caueatur phlebotomia iterum post colicam passionem, et uomitum, et fluxum uentris, et multiplicationem coitus, et laboris, et exercitii, et uigiliarum, et ieiunii, et omnis quod dissoluit uirtutem ex re corporea aut animali.	And bloodletting should also be avoided after an episode of colic, vomiting, diarrhea, excessive intercourse, labor, exercise, wakefulness, fasting, and anything that depletes strength from the body or mind.
Deinde considera in subtilitate humorum ante illud, si sanguis est grossus cum cibis et potibus, et medicinis: si possibile est ei illud.	Then consider the subtlety of the humors beforehand, if the blood is thick from foods, drinks, and medicines: if that is possible for him.
Deinde introeat balneum, si non prohibet ipsum aliquid: aut exercitetur aliquo exercitio, ut subtilietur sanguis.	Then let him enter the bath, if nothing prevents it; or engage in some exercise, to make the blood more fluid.
Et pone phlebotomiam eius in pectore, circa tertiam diei, sicut diximus: et conare, ut euacuetur pectus eius illa die ex omnibus accidentibus animalibus malis: sicut angustia, et ira, et timor: et ex omnibus accidentibus corporeis, sicut labor, et poena, et superfluicoitus, et illis similia.	And place his bloodletting in the chest, around the third hour of the day, as we said: and strive so that his chest may be cleared that day of all harmful emotional occurrences, such as distress, anger, and fear; and of all physical events, such as labor, pain, excessive intercourse, and similar things.
Et sedi suae faciat esse praesentes res cum quarum usu cucurrit consuetudo hominum ex aromaticitate, et odoramento, et tripudio et similibus illis per quantitatem possibilitatis.	And let him have present at his seat things with which people are accustomed to use, such as spices, perfumes, music, and similar items, as much as possible.
Deinde sedeat phlebotomator super puluinari, quod sit altius quam puluinar phlebotomandi: postea extrahat brachium suum, et fricet ipsum phlebotomator manu sua bis, aut ter.	Then let the bloodletter sit on a cushion that is higher than the cushion of the one being bled; afterward, extend his arm, and the bloodletter should rub it with his hand two or three times.
Deinde stringat ligamentum cum corrigia, et inuoluat eam bis.	Then let him bind the ligament with a strap, and wrap it twice.
Et sit strictura temperata, quoniam strictura quando est inaequalis, aut per superfluitatem in strictura, tunc prohibet cursum sanguinis: aut in mollificatione, tunc etiam prohibet cursum sanguinis.	And let the binding be moderate, for when the binding is uneven, or too tight, it then hinders the flow of blood; or if too loose, it also hinders the flow of blood.
Deinde post stricturam oportet, ut fricet phlebotomandus manus suas simul adinuicem, donec inflentur uenae, et declarentur sensui.	Then, after binding, the one to be bled should rub his hands together until the veins are engorged and visible to the touch.
Deinde abstergat phlebotomator phlebotomum cum pauco oleo, antiquo proprie.	Then the bloodletter should wipe the lancet with a little aged oil.
Deinde ponat digitum suum indicem manus sinistrae super ipsam uenam sub loco cuius phlebotomiam uult, parum: ut non fugiat uena, et cassetur percussio.	Then he should place the index finger of his left hand slightly on the vein itself below the spot where he intends the bloodletting, so that the vein does not slip away and the incision misses.
Quoniam ex uenis est quam inuenis sicut chordam fugientem apud phlebotomiam: et ex eis est quae est plena uentositate: et quando ponis phlebotomum super eam, contrahitur sub phlebotomo, et decipitur phlebotomator, et non aperit phlebotomus uenam: et si aperit eam, est stricta: propter illud ergo oportet, ut firmetur phlebotomator, et sit sollicitus in his rebus omnibus.	For among the veins there are some that you find to flee like a string during bloodletting: and among them are those full of air, and when you place the lancet over it, it contracts under the lancet, deceiving the bloodletter, who then does not open the vein; and even if he does open it, it is narrow. Therefore, the bloodletter must be steady and careful in all these things.
Deinde deponat phlebotomum.	Then he should put down the lancet.
Si ergo aperitur uena ex uice una, tunc bonum est.	If then the vein opens with one attempt, that is good.
Et si non, tunc itera illud uice alia sub illo loco parum, aut supra ipsum cum festinatione: si non apostematur locus.	And if not, then repeat it with another attempt a little below or above that spot, with quickness, unless the area becomes inflamed.
Quod si apostematur, aut expauescit infirmus, tunc dimitte eum die, aut duobus diebus: et non stringat ligamentum.	But if the area becomes inflamed, or the patient grows fearful, then leave him for a day or two, and do not tie the binding.
Fortasse enim attrahet apostema calidum.	For it may draw a warm inflammation.
Neque ingrediatur balneum.	Nor should he enter a bath.
Deinde iteret phlebotomiam, si amat.	Then let him repeat the bloodletting, if he wishes.
Quod si apertio est parua, aut currit sanguis subtiliter: et times ut non egrediatur ex sanguine quantitas quam uis: tunc itera phlebotomum in foramine ipso secundum rectitudinem, et adde in operatione parum.	But if the opening is small, or the blood flows subtly, and you fear the desired amount may not exit, then insert the lancet again in the same opening straightly and add a bit to the procedure.
Et fac illud cum festinatione, antequam apostemetur locus, apostematus enim locus est in multis hominibus apud operationem paruam.	And do this quickly, before the area becomes inflamed, for in many people, a small procedure leads to an inflamed area.
Si ergo uides ipsum iam apostematum, tunc non iteres super ipsum omnino, non enim excusat apud te aliquid.	If you see that it is already inflamed, then do not repeat on it at all, for nothing excuses it to you.
Et pone super ipsum aliquid ex faece olei: quoniam alleuiat cursum sanguinis.	And place something of the oil sediment on it, for it alleviates the flow of blood.
Et est melior in hoc loco quam oleum ipsum, et quam reliqua olea.	And it is better in this case than oil itself or other oils.
Et similiter administra faecem olei in omni phlebotomia tua, qua phlebotomas uenas apud prohibitionem cursus sanguinis: et faciunt illud tyriaca alfarach, et sagzenea, quando ponitur ex una earum super locum.	Similarly, use oil sediment in all your bloodlettings, in which you treat veins with a restriction on blood flow: alfarach antidote and sagzenea do this when one of them is placed on the area.
Sanguis enim subtiliatur et resoluitur, quando est grossus.	For the blood is thinned and dissolved when it is thick.
Quod si accidat in loco phlebotomiae apostema magnum, nam multotiens accidit, praecipue ei qui non est phlebotomatus nisi uice illa, aut est apertio uenae parua, tunc propera et pone super locum spongiam infusam in aqua et sale, calefacta parumper: et stringe eam hora una: quoniam soluitur.	But if a large inflammation occurs at the site of bloodletting, as often happens, especially to one who has only been bled that time, or if the vein opening is small, then quickly place a sponge soaked in water and salt, slightly heated, on the area, and press it for an hour, for it will dissolve.
Et oportet, ut facias illud post exitum sanguinis ex uena ipsa, cum complemento sui, aut ex uena alia.	And you should do this after the blood has exited from that vein fully, or from another vein.
Quod si remanet in loco post dies aliquid nigredinis, aut uiriditatis, non nocet illud: et si uis, pone super ipsum aliquid aloe, aut myrrhae solutorum: aut aliquid ex succo menthastri, id est, calamenti aut simile illius.	If, after some days, something dark or green remains in the area, it is not harmful; and if you wish, place on it some dissolved aloe or myrrh, or something of the juice of pennyroyal, that is, calamint or something similar.
Et multotiens quidem accidunt apostemata et eminentiae apud phlebotomiam Basilicae.	And indeed, often inflammations and swellings occur with bloodletting of the Basilic vein.
Pone ergo manum tuam super ipsam, tunc si inuenis molliciem apud compressionem tuam super ipsam, tunc illa eminentia est mala.	Therefore, place your hand on it, and if you find softness upon your pressing it, then that swelling is bad.
Caue ergo, ut ponas super eam aliquid ex eo quod diximus.	Be careful, then, not to place on it anything from what we have mentioned.
Nam fortasse fluet ex ea sanguis arteriae.	For blood from the artery may flow from it.
Sed fac emplastrum ei cum eo in quo est stipticitas, ut induretur locus.	But make a plaster for it with something astringent, so that the area becomes firm.
Deinde cura ipsum cum reliqua curatione, donec sanetur.	Then treat it with the remaining treatment until it heals.
Et oportet, ut extrahas omni homini ex sanguine secundum quantitatem uirtutis suae.	And you must extract blood from every person according to the measure of his strength.
Et quod apparet ex colore uincente super sanguinem.	And from what appears as the dominant color in the blood.
Nam sanguis si est niger, tunc dimitte ipsum exire, donec rubeat: et similiter si uides ipsum grossum: tunc dimitte eum, donec subtilietur: et similiter si est acutus, donec auferatur acuitas eius.	For if the blood is black, then let it flow out until it becomes red; similarly, if you see it thick, then let it flow until it becomes thinner; and similarly if it is sharp, until its sharpness is removed.
Et oportet ei qui est plenus, fortis, et indiget extractione sanguinis uice una, ut amplificetur phlebotomia uenae, et sit phlebotomus latus: et in eo qui est debilis, sit econtrario illius.	And if someone is full, strong, and requires blood extraction all at once, the bloodletting incision should be widened, and the lancet should be broad; and for someone who is weak, the opposite should be done.
Et oportet, ut extrahat ipsum in uicibus, et ut sit foramen strictum: et melius quod administratur in phlebotomia uenae est, ut sit transuersa sectio, non punctura.	And it should be drawn out in stages, and the opening should be narrow: the best method in venous bloodletting is a transverse cut, not a puncture.
Et iste modus phlebotomiae est saluus a fluxu sanguinis, et ab incisione nerui: et est laudabilior, et saluior quam phlebotomia per latitudinem et longitudinem.	And this method of bloodletting is safe from excessive blood flow and from cutting a nerve: it is more praiseworthy and safer than bloodletting along the width or length.

For one who is accustomed to experiencing fainting during bloodletting, it is necessary to feed him before bloodletting a small amount of bread soaked in water with pomegranate juice, or secaniabin if he is warm; and draw blood from him in three or four sessions.

If his constitution is cold, then he should take before the bloodletting bread soaked in syrup of quince, or in syrup of honey aromatized with spices, or in a fragrant aromatic syrup.
Quod si accidat syncopis apud phlebotomiam, et est caussa eius exitus sanguinis multi: tunc oportet, ut sumat in potu aquam carnis, et syrupum odoriferum subtilem: et utatur aromaticitate cum algalia, et liniatur pectus eius cum ea: et utatur reliquis quae diximus in diuisione in capitulo syncopis ex euacuatione.	But if fainting occurs during bloodletting, and it is due to excessive blood loss, he should then drink meat broth and a fine, fragrant syrup, use aromatics with musk, anoint his chest with it, and use the other remedies we have discussed in the section on fainting from evacuation.

Uentosae fiunt ex cornibus, et ex aere, et ex uitro.	Cups are made from horns, metal, and glass.

Cum scarificatione, et extractione sanguinis: et alter est uentosatio sine scarificatione.	With scarification and blood extraction; and the other is cupping without scarification.
Et haec Ventosatio quae est sine scarificatione, est secundum duos modos.	And this cupping, which is without scarification, is also done in two ways.
Aut enim est cum igne.	For it is either with fire.
Aut est absque igne.	Or it is without fire.
Et uentosis quae administrantur cum scarificatione et extractione sanguinis sunt quatuordecim loca corporis.	And there are fourteen places on the body where cupping is applied with scarification and blood extraction.
Quorum unus est, Ventosatio Alnocrati, et est postremum capitis: et Alkehel, et est medium colli: et uentosae Alhagdahim, et sunt duae laminae colli a duabus partibus simul: et uentosae menti, et est sub mandibula in inferiori oris: et uentosae duarum spatularum: et uentosae Alachosos super finem caudae: et uentosae duorum focilium, et sunt medium duorum brachiorum: et uentosae duorum crurium: et uentosae duarum cauillarum.	One of these is the cupping of Alnocrati, which is at the back of the head; and Alkehel, which is at the middle of the neck; and the cupping of Alhagdahim, which is on the two sides of the neck at the two blades; and the cupping of the chin, which is below the jaw at the lower part of the mouth; and the cupping of the two shoulder blades; and the cupping of Alachosos above the end of the tail; and the cupping of the two elbows, which is the middle of the two arms; and the cupping of the two thighs; and the cupping of the two ankles.
Et Ventosatio quidem non extrahit sanguinem tibi ex uenis minutis sparsis in carne, et propter illud non cadit uirtus ex eis, sicut est casus eius ex phlebotomia.	And indeed, cupping does not draw blood from the small veins scattered in the flesh, and therefore the strength does not diminish from them, as it does in phlebotomy.
Neque oportet, ut administretur uentosatio siue cum igne, aut absque igne in aliqua aegritudinum quae fiunt ex repletione, donec euacuetur totum corpus.	Nor is it appropriate to administer cupping, either with fire or without fire, for any illness caused by fullness, until the entire body is evacuated.
Quod si necessitas prouocat ad Ventosationem ex aegritudine, aut propter consuetudinem, administramus eam in omni hora in principio mensis, in fine eius, et in medio ipsius, et in quocumque tempore fuerit.	But if a need for cupping arises due to illness or habit, we administer it at any time, at the beginning of the month, at its end, and in its middle, and at any other time.
Quod est, quia ex hominibus est qui quando multiplicatur in eo sanguis, donec indiget extractione eius per uentosationem, inuenit in capite suo grauedinem, et sodahan.	For there are people in whom, when blood multiplies to the extent that cupping extraction is needed, they experience heaviness and dizziness in their head.
Et ex eis est qui inuenit repletionem et rubedinem in facie sua, capite suo, et collo suo.	And some of them feel fullness and redness in their face, head, and neck.
Et ex eis est qui inuenit pruritum in facie sua, et fronte sua, et tenebrositatem, et hebetudinem in oculis suis.	And some of them experience itching in their face and forehead, dimness, and dullness in their eyes.
Et ex eis est qui scalpit locum uentosationis suae.	And some of them scratch the place of their cupping.
Et ex eis est cuius risus multiplicatur.	And some of them experience an increase in laughter.
Et ex eis est qui inuenit gustum sanguinis in ore suo: aut apostematur lingua, et expuit sanguinem.	And some of them taste blood in their mouth; or their tongue swells, and they spit blood.
Et ex eis est cuius somnus multiplicatur.	And some of them experience an increase in sleep.
Et ex eis est qui uidet in somno suo sanguinem et rubedinem, et interfectos, et plagas, et quae sunt similia illis.	And some of them see blood and redness, the dead, wounds, and similar things in their dreams.
Quando ergo uidemus aliquid ex illis, et proprie si est in tertia media mensis: praecipimus tunc uentosationem fieri, postquam praeterierunt de die duae horae, aut tres.	When, therefore, we observe any of these signs, particularly if it is in the middle third of the month, we then order cupping to be performed after two or three hours of the day have passed.
Iuuamentum autem Ventosationis nuchae, est: quoniam ipsa confert ad grauitatem in capite, et quod effunditur ad oculos.	The benefit of cupping the nape is that it relieves heaviness in the head and benefits the eyes.
At uero oportet, ut sit illud post euacuationem totius corporis.	However, it should be done after the entire body has been evacuated.
Et haec quidem Ventosa est loco phlebotomiae Cephalicae.	And this cup indeed serves in place of the cephalic phlebotomy.
Et non licet, ut utatur ea qui est frigidae complexionis, aut in quo est catarrus.	And it is not permitted for those of cold complexion, or those with catarrh, to use it.
Nam infert ei nocumentum magnum.	For it brings great harm to them.
Et propter illud non oportet, ut utantur ea senes, et in cuius capite sunt aegritudines frigidae.	And for this reason, the elderly and those with cold illnesses in the head should not use it.
Qui autem super eam fit assiduus, generatur super eum obliuio.	And whoever uses it frequently will develop forgetfulness.
Et propter illud oportet, ut praecipias Ventosatori, ut deponat manum suam cum uentosa parumper ad inferiora, timendo ex generatione obliuionis.	And because of this, you should instruct the cupper to lower his hand with the cup a bit, for fear of causing forgetfulness.
Ventosatio aut Alkehel, est loco phlebotomiae Fuscae, et phlebotomiae Basilicae, et propter illud confert asmati, et constrictioni anhelitus, et fissurae instrumenti anhelitus, et tussi, et repletioni.	The cupping of Alkehel serves as an alternative to dark and basilic phlebotomy, and thus it helps with asthma, difficulty in breathing, fissures of the respiratory organ, cough, and fullness.
Et oportet, ut impellatur Ventosa Alkehel parumper, quoniam si ponitur inferius, generat debilitatem in corde, et stomacho.	And the Alkehel cup should be applied gently, for if it is placed too low, it weakens the heart and stomach.
Ventosatio uero alhagdahim, confert doloribus accidentibus in capite, et obtalmiae, et hemigraneae, et perforationi, et dolori in radicibus dentium: et est loco phlebotomiae Basilicae.	The cupping of Alhagdahim helps with pains occurring in the head, eye inflammations, migraines, perforations, and pain at the roots of the teeth; and it serves as an alternative to basilic phlebotomy.
Et oportet, ut praecipias Ventosatori, ne premat manum suam cum scarificatione: ut non incidat arteriam, et accidat fluxus sanguinis.	And you should instruct the cupper not to press his hand during scarification, so that he does not cut an artery and cause a blood flow.
Ventosatio autem sub mento, confert alcolae in ore, et corruptioni gingiuae, et similibus eis ex aegritudinibus quae sunt in ore: et stat loco phlebotomiae alieherich, quae sunt in duobus labiis.	Cupping under the chin benefits conditions in the mouth, such as ulcers and gum disease, and similar illnesses in the mouth; it serves in place of the phlebotomy of alieherich, which is in the two lips.
Ventosatio autem spatularum, confert ad alchafacham, quae fit ex replexione, et caliditate.	Cupping on the shoulder blades benefits alchafacham, which arises from fullness and heat.
Ventosatio uero interiorum partium duorum focilium, confert iuuamentum phlebotomiae uenarum trium, Basilicae, et Fuscae, et Cephalicae: quoniam ipsa attrahit sanguinem ex omnibus illis uenis minutis, quae sunt in carne: et attrahunt illae uenae minutae ex uenis aliis grossioribus eis, donec peruenit attractio ad uenas tres.	Cupping on the inner parts of the two elbows serves as an aid to the phlebotomy of the three veins: basilic, dark, and cephalic; for it draws blood from all the small veins in the flesh, and these small veins draw from the larger veins, until the attraction reaches the three main veins.
Et oportet, ut praecipias Ventosatori, ne multum procedat in scarificatione: quoniam locus est denudatus carne, et sub eo sunt nerui et arteriae.	And you should instruct the cupper not to cut too deeply in scarification, for the area is exposed without much flesh, and underneath it are nerves and arteries.
Ventosa autem quae uentosatur super alchausos, confert hemorroidibus Ani, et ulceribus inferioribus.	The cup placed over alchausos benefits hemorrhoids of the anus and lower ulcers.
Et oportet, ut praecipias Ventosatori, ut sit uentosa magna, et ut sit ex aere: quoniam locus indiget suctione forti, et fortasse franguntur Ventosae subtiles: et scarificetur scarificatione magna.	And you should instruct the cupper to use a large cup made of metal, for the area requires strong suction, and delicate cups might break; and it should be deeply scarified.
Ventosae autem crurium minuunt diminutione manifesta: quoniam attrahunt sanguinem ex toto corpore, et conferunt doloribus antiquis in renibus, et matricibus, et uesica: prouocant menstrua, et conferunt bothor, et ademenul: et stant loco phlebotomiae duarum saphenarum, et alhorcobeni.	Cupping on the thighs brings significant reduction, for it draws blood from the whole body, and it helps with chronic pains in the kidneys, womb, and bladder; it stimulates menstruation, and helps with bothor and ademenul; and it serves as a substitute for the phlebotomy of the two saphenous veins and alhorcobeni.
Verumtamen debilitant corpus multum, et faciunt accidere syncopim in pluribus hominibus.	However, it weakens the body greatly and causes fainting in many people.
Et Ventosarum alhorcobeni iuuamentum est propinquum iuuamento Ventosarum duorum crurium.	And the benefit of the alhorcobeni cupping is similar to that of cupping on the two thighs.
Qualitas positionis Ventosarum est, ut ponas uentosam in primis uacuam, et sugas suctione temperata: et non prolonges positionem uentosarum, sed pone eas uelociter, et remoue eas uelociter, ut ueniant humores ad locum aduentu aequali: et non cesses iterare illud et continuare, donec uideas locum iam rubere et inflari, et appareat rubedo sanguinis: tunc ergo scarifica, et itera suctione paulatim.	The method of placing the cups is as follows: first place the cup empty, and draw with moderate suction; do not leave the cups in place for too long, but apply and remove them quickly, so that the humors come to the site with a balanced flow; and continue repeating this until you see the area becoming red and swollen, and the redness of blood appears; then scarify, and gradually repeat the suction.
Deinde considera in dispositione corporum.	Then consider the condition of the bodies.
Nam qui ex hominibus est tenerae carnis, rarorum pororum, oportet ut scarifices ipsum semel, non plus: ut non ulceretur locus.	For a person with tender flesh and sparse pores should be scarified only once, no more, so that the area does not ulcerate.
Et praecipe Ventosatori, ut amplificet scarificationem, et profundet parum: et temperet suctionem in facilitate, et uelocitate, et motu suaui.	And instruct the cupper to make a larger scarification and to cut slightly deeper, and to adjust the suction with ease, speed, and gentle movement.
Quod si in sanguine est grossitudo, tunc oportet, ut scarifices ipsum bis.	If the blood is thick, then scarify twice.
In uice quidem prima aperitur uia subtiliationi sanguinis, et aquositati eius: et in secunda quidem perscrutationi exquisitae extractionis sanguinis grossi.	In the first instance, the path is opened to refine and thin the blood; and in the second, to precisely extract the thicker blood.
Si uero sanguis est faeculentus ualde, tunc fiat scarificatio uice tertia: ut consequatur ultimum.	If the blood is very impure, then perform a third scarification to reach the final goal.
Et ad ultimum, quando uolumus extrahere sanguinem paulatim, uel parumper, sufficit nobis scarificatio una.	And finally, when we wish to extract blood gradually, or in a small amount, one scarification is sufficient.
Quod si uolumus extractionem sanguinis multi, scarificamus scarificatione plurima.	But if we want to extract a large amount of blood, we scarify several times.
Et si nos mensuramus, quod sanguis est grossus: tunc oportet, ut scarificetur scarificatione profunda: et terminus aequalis in scarificatione, est profunditas cutis.	And if we determine that the blood is thick, then it should be deeply scarified; the proper depth in scarification is the thickness of the skin.
¶ Quid sit illud quod oportet administrari ex oleis apud positionem Ventosarum, et quid caueat uentosandus.	What should be applied in oils at the time of placing the cups, and what the person to be cupped should avoid.
IN illo quidem cuius cutis est grossa, dura, fusca, et pori eius sunt stricti, oportet ut ungantur loca Ventosare cum oleis apertiuis, lenitiuis, resolutiuis: si est in tempore aestatis, sicut est oleum alkeiri, aut oleum uiolatum, aut oleum de amigdalis dulcibus, aut oleum de semine cucurbitae.	For one whose skin is thick, tough, dark, and whose pores are tight, it is necessary to anoint the places to be cupped with opening, softening, and resolving oils; if it is summertime, use oils such as alkeiri oil, violet oil, sweet almond oil, or gourd seed oil.
Et si est in tempore hyemis, tunc oleum narcissinum, aut oleum de lilio, aut oleum camomellinum, aut sarbatinum: et eis similia.	And if it is winter, then use daffodil oil, lily oil, chamomile oil, or sarbatinum oil, and the like.
Si uero superfluitas est grossa, frigida: tunc oleum sit amaracinum, aut oleum de balsamita aut oleum alben, aut oleum anetinum: et eis similia.	If, however, the excess is thick and cold, then use oil of marjoram, balm oil, alben oil, or dill oil, and the like.
Si uero uentosandus est amplorum pororum, tenerae carnis: tunc oportet, ut prohibeatur ab oleo, et oportet ut isti abluant loca Ventosarum earum post uentosationem cum aqua rosata, aut cum aqua frigida, aut cum aqua solatri, aut cum aqua cucurbitae, aut cum aqua portulacae: et similibus eis.	If, however, the person to be cupped has wide pores and tender flesh, then he should avoid oil, and instead wash the places to be cupped after cupping with rose water, cold water, nightshade water, gourd water, purslane water, and similar things.
Ille autem cuius sanguis est multae humiditatis, abluat uentosas cum aceto, aut cum aqua myrti, et sumach: et similibus eis.	The one whose blood is very moist should wash the cups with vinegar, myrtle water, and sumac, and similar things.
Cuius autem superfluitates sunt grossae, abluat uentosas suas cum uino antiquo, aut cum aqua samsuci, aut decoctione aneti, aut camomillae: et eis similibus.	But one whose excess is thick should wash his cups with aged wine, elderflower water, dill decoction, chamomile, and the like.
Et oportet, ut caueatur uentosatio in balneo, aut in uestigio balnei, imo oportet, ut administretur post exitum ex balneo per horam, aut per horas duas.	And cupping should be avoided in the bath, or immediately after the bath; rather, it should be done an hour or two after leaving the bath.
Et non oportet, ut dormiat aliquis post uentosationem.	And one should not sleep after cupping.
¶ Quid est quo oportet ut regatur Ventosandus, aut Phlebotomatus ante uentosationem.	What should be observed for the person to be cupped or phlebotomized before cupping.
ET post eam oportet, ut consideres in primis: tunc si Ventosatus, aut Phlebotomatus sit cholericus, et uincunt super sanguinem eius acuitas et inflammatio: tunc oportet, ut accipiat infrigidantia, sicut granatum muzum: et etiam endiuiam cum aceto et lactuca, et secaniabin, et iuleb: et eis similia.	And afterward, it is necessary to consider first: if the cupped or phlebotomized person is choleric and sharpness and inflammation prevail in his blood, then he should take cooling agents, such as pomegranate muzum, as well as endive with vinegar, lettuce, sekaniabin, julep, and the like.
Et pone cibaria eius pullos, et carnes ouium sekebiget, et cibos conditos cum agresta: et eis similia.	And his food should be chicken, lamb meat prepared sekebiget-style, and foods seasoned with verjuice, and the like.
Et cuius complexio est frigida, oportet ut sumat in potu syrupum de melle, aut de almiua, aut alsecaniabin de seminibus factum: et sumat uinum odoriferum medium, quod est inter uetus et nouum: et praecipiatur, ut utatur paucitate cibi.	And for one whose temperament is cold, he should drink syrup of honey, almiua, or alsecaniabin made from seeds; he should drink fragrant wine of medium age, between old and new, and be instructed to consume food sparingly.
Et pone cibum eius pullos, et canebir: et passeres, id est, paruas aues: et pullos columborum issiadebet.	And his food should be chicken, partridge, sparrows, that is, small birds, and young pigeons prepared issiadebet-style.
Et oportet, ut potus die uentosationis et phlebotomiae plus sit quam cibus: et oportet, ut detur in potu in quibusdam hominibus in horis tyriaca alfaroch, aut medicamen de musco, id est, muscata, aut alesselithe ante uentosationem: et ante phlebotomiam, aut post: ut confortentur membra principalia, et subtilietur sanguis.	On the day of cupping or phlebotomy, drink should be more than food; and it is necessary that some people take tyriaca alfaroch in drink, or a medicine of musk, that is, nutmeg, or alesselithe before cupping and before or after phlebotomy, to strengthen the main organs and refine the blood.
Et non oportet, ut sumant eam in potu calefacto.	And they should not take it in heated drink.
¶ De Ventosis sine scarificatione.	On cupping without scarification.
Uentosae autem quae sunt sine scarificatione, sunt Ventosae quae ponuntur super epar, et splenem, et podagram, et super uentrem, umbilicum, et loca renum, et pixidem anchae: quoniam ista membra non sufferunt scarificationem super se.	Cups without scarification are those placed over the liver, spleen, gouty areas, stomach, navel, the regions of the kidneys, and the hip socket, because these parts do not tolerate scarification.
Et non quaeritur per eas, nisi aut attactio ex membro sanguinis ad membrum: sicut ponimus Ventosam super mammillas in aegritudine fluxus sanguinis narium.	Their purpose is to transfer the humor from one part of the body to another; as when we place a cup over the breasts in the condition of nasal blood flow.
Et utimur eis, ut soluamus a membro uentositatem frigidam inuiscatam in membro: sicut ponimus uentosam super uentrem, et umbilicum.	And we use them to release cold trapped wind in a part; as when we place a cup over the stomach and navel.
Ipsa enim rarificat membrum, et calefacit ipsum, et remouet dolorem propterea, quia resoluit illam uentositatem.	This thins and warms the part, and removes pain by resolving the trapped wind.
Etiam ponitur super renes, quando accidit in eis opilatio, aut lapis.	It is also placed over the kidneys when there is obstruction or a stone in them.
Uirtute enim attractionis suae fortasse aperit opilationem, aut eradicat lapidem ex loco suo.	By the strength of its attraction, it may open the obstruction or dislodge the stone from its place.
Et similiter facit quando ponitur super epar, et splenem apud uentositatem terendam in eis utrisque.	It works similarly when placed over the liver and spleen to dispel wind trapped in both.
Et istae Ventosae administrantur uacuae cum sectione tantum.	These cups are applied empty with section only.
Et quandoque administrantur cum igne.	And sometimes they are applied with fire.
Et quandoque administrantur plenae aqua tepida in aegritudinibus pleuresis: et illud est, ut impleatur Ventosa, et sit magna, aqua calida sola, aut aqua in qua decoctae sint herbae quae conueniunt.	And sometimes they are applied filled with warm water for conditions of pleurisy; this involves filling a large cup with hot water alone, or water in which suitable herbs have been boiled.
¶ Forma Ventosae administratae cum igne.	The form of a cupping glass applied with fire.
Sit amplitudo orificii eius duorum digitorum apertorum, secundum quod formauimus: et quantitas eius in profundo sit medietas palmi: et sit in latere eius in loco qui est circiter medium eius, foramen paruum, secundum quantitatem quod ingrediatur ipsum acus: fiat ex aere simul, aut citrino, grossorum marginum, lenium, aequalium, tersorum: ut non laedat membrum apud positionem suam super ipsum: et sit in medio eius uirga transuersa ex aere, aut ferro, ubi ponitur candela cum igne.	The opening of the cupping glass should be about the width of two open fingers, as we have specified, and its depth should be about half a palm. In the side, approximately at the middle, there should be a small hole, large enough for a needle to enter. It should be made from brass or citrine metal, with thick, smooth, and even edges so as not to injure the body when applied. In the center, there should be a crosswise rod made of brass or iron, on which a candle with a flame can be placed.
Et iam fit haec uentosa magna, maior ea quam narrauimus, et parum minor illa, secundum aegritudines et aetatem eius qui utitur.	This cup should be larger than the ones described earlier, though slightly smaller in some cases, depending on the illness and the age of the user.
Ventosae enim puerorum, et extenuatorum sunt aliae, quam uentosae uirorum, et grossorum corporum.	The cups used for children and those with thin bodies are different from those used for adults and those with larger bodies.
Qualitas autem ponendi hanc uentosam cum igne super membrum, est, ut accendas licinium cum igne ex lino decenter, aut candelam paruam ex caera: et pone eam super medium uirgae transuersae quae est in medio uentosae: ut sit accensus ignis circiter inferiorem partem uentosae, ut non comburatur corpus infirmi.	To apply this cup with fire to the body, ignite a small wick made from linen or a small wax candle, and place it in the middle of the transverse rod in the center of the cupping glass. This should produce flame near the lower part of the cupping glass, ensuring the patient’s body is not burned.
Deinde ponatur super membrum: et digitus sit super foramen quod dixi, donec quando tenemus uentosam: dum indigemus, auferamus digitum, et egredietur uapor super illud foramen, et soluetur uentosa statim.	Then place the cup on the body, keeping a finger over the small hole previously mentioned. When the cup is in position and ready, remove your finger, allowing steam to escape through the hole, and the cup will release immediately.
Deinde igni licinium secundum modum suum, et itera eam, si indiges illa.	Then, if necessary, reignite the wick as described and reapply the cup.
In Ventosa autem quae administratur in aegritudine pleuresis cum aqua, non est uirga transuersa, neque foramen: et non administratur, nisi quia impletur aqua, et ponitur super membrum tantum.	In the case of a cup applied for pleurisy and filled with water, there is no transverse rod or small hole. It is used only by filling it with water and placing it directly on the body.
Et haec est forma eius.	This is its form.
Et haec Ventosa quanto plus est magna, ut capiat aquam multam, est melior.	The larger this cup is, allowing it to hold more water, the more effective it is.
¶ De suspensione Sanguisugarum.	On the application of leeches.
Caput XCIX.	Chapter 99.
Sanguisugae non administrantur in pluribus, nisi in quibus membris non est possibilis positio uentosarum, propter paruitatem eorum, sicut labium, et gingiua, et eis similia.	Leeches are not applied in many places, except in areas where cupping is not possible due to their small size, such as the lip, gum, and similar areas.
Aut in membro denudato a carne, sicut digitus, et nasus, et eis similia.	Or in parts lacking flesh, such as the finger, nose, and similar areas.
Et qualitas utendi eis est: ut ueneris ex Sanguisugis quae sunt in aquis dulcibus, mundis a putredinibus.	The quality of leeches to be used is that they come from fresh waters, free from impurities.
Deinde dimittantur die et nocte in aqua dulci, donec famelicae sint, et non remaneat in uentre earum aliquid.	Then, let them stay day and night in fresh water, until they are hungry, and nothing remains in their stomachs.
Deinde euacuetur corpus in primis cum phlebotomia, et cum uentosis.	Then cleanse the body first with bloodletting and cupping.
Deinde absterge membrum infirmi, donec rubeat.	Then cleanse the affected area until it is red.
Postea ponantur super ipsum.	Afterwards, place them on it.
Cum ergo implentur, et cadunt, et est possibilis suctio loci cum uentosa, tunc est magis ultimum in iuuamento: et si non, tunc ablue locum cum aceto.	When they are full and fall off, if suction at the site with a cup is possible, this is more beneficial; if not, wash the site with vinegar.
Deinde cum aqua multa, et fricetur, et exprimatur.	Then with plenty of water, rub and squeeze it.
Quod si prolongatur cursus sanguinis post casum Sanguisugarum, et est illud resudando: tunc infunde pannum lini in aqua frigida, et pone ipsum desuper, donec abscindatur resudatio.	If the blood flow continues after the leeches fall off, and it seeps, then soak a linen cloth in cold water and place it on top, until the seepage stops.
Quod si multiplicatur sanguis, tunc pulueriza super ipsum zegi tritum, aut gallas, et similia eis ex stipticis: donec abscindatur sanguis.	If the blood increases, then sprinkle powdered alum or galls or similar astringents over it, until the bleeding stops.
Aut ponantur super locum medietates fabarum excorticatarum: et dimittantur, donec adhaereant fabae in loco, nam sanguis abscinditur.	Or place halves of peeled beans over the site and leave them until the beans adhere, for the bleeding will stop.
Et oportet si necessaria est iteratio Sanguisugarum, quando est possibile, mutare eas.	If the repetition of leech application is necessary, they should be changed when possible.
Si autem prohibentur Sanguisugae a suspensione, tunc abstergatur locus cum sanguine recenti.	If leeches are prevented from adhering, then clean the site with fresh blood.
Aut fige acum in locis, donec egrediatur aliquid ex sanguine.	Or insert a needle at the site until some blood flows out.
Deinde ponantur, nam ipsae quando sentiunt aliquid ex sanguine, adhaerent statim.	Then place them, for as soon as they sense some blood, they will immediately adhere.
Quando ergo uis ut cadant, pulueriza super eas aliquid ex aloe trito, aut salem, aut cinerem: et cadent statim.	When you want them to fall off, sprinkle powdered aloe, salt, or ashes over them, and they will fall off immediately.

¶ Sermo uniuersalis de restauratione, et dislocatione, et curatione torsionis, et similibus.	Universal treatise on restoration, dislocation, treatment of twisting, and similar matters.
Caput I.	Chapter 1.
Hoc Capitulum etiam multotiens est necessarium in arte Medicinae, et est restauratio fracturae, et dislocationis accidentium in ossibus.	This chapter is often necessary in the art of medicine and concerns the restoration of fractures and dislocations in bones.
Scitote o Filii, quia iactant se de hoc Capitulo stolidi medicorum et uulgi, et qui non inspexerunt unquam antiquorum librum, neque legerunt ex eo literam.	Know, O Sons, that foolish doctors and the common people boast of this chapter, though they have never looked into any ancient book nor read a single letter from it.
Et propter hanc caussam fit, quod iste modus ex scientiis in terra nostra est priuatus.	And for this reason, this method of knowledge is deprived in our land.
Et ego non obuiaui in eo unquam bono omnino, et non adeptus sum ex eo illud, quod sum adeptus nisi propter longitudinem lectionis librorum antiquorum, et studium super intellectum eorum, donec extraxi scientiam illius ex eis.	And I have never found anything good in it, nor did I gain anything from it except through extensive reading of ancient books and studying their understanding, until I extracted its knowledge from them.
Deinde adhaesi experimento et studio longitudine uitae meae.	Then I applied myself to experimentation and study throughout my life.
Et iam descripsi uobis ex illo in hoc Capitulo totum quod continet scientia mea: et super quod praeteriit experimentum meum, postquam abbreuiaui illud uobis, et liberaui illud ex occupatione elongationis, et abbreuiaui illud fine abbreuiationis, et ostendi illud fine declinationis: et formaui in eo formas multas ex formis instrumentorum quae administrantur in eo, cum sit ex additione declinationis, sicut feci in duobus Capitulis praecedentibus.	And now I have written for you in this chapter all that my knowledge contains on this matter, based on my experiments, after I have abbreviated it for you, freed it from unnecessary length, abbreviated it to the point of abbreviation, and demonstrated it clearly; I have also designed many forms of instruments used in it, with added details, as I did in the two preceding chapters.
¶ Summae, et collectiones ex esse fracturae ossium, ex eis quarum praemissio necessaria est, antequam incipiamus rememorationem membrorum fractorum, et diuersorum, si uniuscuiusque.	Summaries and collections on the nature of bone fractures, among those whose knowledge is necessary before we begin the discussion of fractured and various members, if of each one.
Caput II.	Chapter 2.
Oportet, ut dicamus in prologo huius Capituli, summas sermonis, et capitula, ad quarum intellectum cogitur in primis artifex ad stationem super earum ueritatem: scilicet ut uos, et qui sunt prompti, sciant, uel addiscant hanc artem nobilem, et alii a uobis.	It is necessary that we state in the prologue of this chapter the main points and headings, so that the practitioner is directed first to an understanding of their truth, so that you, and those who are eager, may know or learn this noble art, and others from you.
Dico, ergo, quod quando accidit alicui fractura, aut dislocatio, aut torsio, aut casus: tunc oportet, ut properes in primis ad phlebotomandum eum, et soluendum ipsum, aut utraque simul: si non prohibeat aliquid, sicut debilitas uirtutis: aut est ille cui accidit aliquid ex illis puer, aut senex decrepitus: aut est tempus uehementis caloris, aut uehementis frigoris ualde.	I say, therefore, that when someone suffers a fracture, dislocation, twist, or fall, it is necessary first to hasten to bloodletting and relaxing the patient, or both together, unless something prevents it, such as weakness of strength, or if the patient is a child or very old person, or if it is a time of intense heat or intense cold.
Deinde sit contentus in cibo suo, oleribus frigidis, et carnibus auium, et haedorum: et prohibe uinum, et carnes grossas, et repleri ex cibis et omni cibo qui replet uenas sanguine.	Then be moderate in food, with cool vegetables and the meats of birds and kids; prohibit wine and heavy meats and foods that fill the veins with blood.
Donec quando securus sis ex apostemate calido, et non times effusionem materiei ad membrum: tunc redeat infirmus ad regimen suum primum cum quo cucurrit consuetudo eius.	Until you are sure that there is no risk of hot swelling and do not fear the spreading of matter to a limb, then let the patient return to his original diet to which he is accustomed.
Cum ergo incipit os fractum restaurari, tunc oportet, ut cibetur infirmus cibis nutrientibus nutrimento multo, grosso, graui, in quibus sit uiscositas: sicut cibaria facta ex tritico cocto cum aqua, et rizum, et capita, et pedes, et uentres uaccarum, et oua, et pisces recentes: et uinum grossum, et similia illis.	When the broken bone begins to heal, then it is necessary to feed the patient with nourishing foods that are substantial, thick, and heavy, with a certain viscosity, such as porridge made from wheat cooked with water, rice, heads, feet, and stomachs of cows, eggs, and fresh fish, along with thick wine and similar items.
Cum hoc enim regimine fit coagulatio fracturae uelocius, et melius.	With this regimen, the bone fracture consolidates more quickly and effectively.
Et scias, quod ossa fracta, quando sunt in uiris qui sunt in prima senectute, et decrepitis, non est possibile, ut continuentur et consolidentur secundum naturam suam semper, propter exiccationem membrorum eorum: et consolidantur et continuantur quae ex eis sunt in fine lenitatis, sicut ossa puerornm paruorum.	And you should know that broken bones, when in men of advanced or very old age, cannot always rejoin and consolidate fully due to the dryness of their tissues; they consolidate and join in a less firm manner than those in young children.
Verumtamen natura annectit super os fractum ex omni parte aliquid simile glutino, in quo est grossitudo, quo adhaeret et stringit ipsum, donec conglutinantur adinuicem, et ligantur adinuicem, donec uenit in fine fortitudinis et firmitudinis, sicut fuit in primis, donec non prohibet ipsum aliquid ab operationibus suis: et propter hanc caussam est necesse, ut ponas nutrimentum infirmi cibaria, in quibus sunt grauitas, et uiscositas, et grossitudo, sicut diximus.	Yet nature attaches something like glue on all sides of the broken bone, where there is thickness, so that it adheres and holds it, until the parts coalesce and bind together, restoring it to full strength and firmness as it was initially, until nothing hinders its function. For this reason, it is necessary to provide the patient with food that is heavy, viscous, and thick, as we have said.
Et scias, quod fracturae species sunt diuersae, secundum diuersitatem membrorum: quoniam fractura cruris, est diuersa a fractura ossis capitis: et fractura ossis pectoris, est diuersa a fractura ossis dorsi: et similiter reliqua membra omnia diuersa existunt adinuicem: et ego ueni cum rememoratione omnis speciei fracturae expositae in Capitulo suo distinctae ab alia.	And know that types of fractures differ according to the diversity of body parts: a leg fracture is different from a skull fracture; a fracture of the chest bone differs from that of the spine; similarly, all remaining parts vary from one another. I will cover every type of fracture, each presented in its chapter, distinct from the others.
Et diuersificantur species fracturae ossis etiam in seipsa.	And the types of bone fractures themselves vary as well.
Quoniam fit fractura eius arcuata, praeter quod accidant in ea astellae.	For sometimes it breaks in an arched manner, in addition to splintering.
Et quandoque fit fractura eius secundum longitudinem ossis: et sunt fracturae eius satagiae, id est, astellae, et additiones procedentes, sicut sunt in calamo, cum aptatur cultello ad scribendum.	Sometimes the bone fractures along its length; and it splinters into shards and projections, as in a reed when sharpened with a knife for writing.
Et non procedentes: et est fractura cum uulnere et disruptione in capite: et est fractura fissura parua.	Others do not project outward; there is the fracture with a wound and rupture in the skull, and a small fissure fracture.
Et omni speciei est ingenium, uel instrumentum proprium in restauratione eius, secundum quod ueniet rememoratio eius, distincta in locis suis.	Each type has its own method or tool for repair, which will be discussed in detail in its respective place.
Et ex eo quo cognoscitur fractura ossis, est tortuositas eius, et ipsius eminentia: et apparitio eius sensui, et sonitus eius quando comprimis ipsum manu tua.	One way to recognize a bone fracture is by its twisting, its prominence, its perceptible appearance, and the sound it makes when you press on it with your hand.
Quando ergo non est in loco tortuositas apparens, neque sonitus apud tactum tuum cum tangis os cum agitatione, neque inuenit infirmus magnum dolorem: tunc non est illic fractura, imo possibile est, ut sit tortuositas, aut fissura parua: non ergo oportet, ut moueas ipsum cum manu et compressione omnino, imo pone super ipsum ex medicinis, quarum uenit rememoratio, post momentum illud quod conuenit loco: deinde stringatur strictura subtili.	Therefore, when there is no apparent twisting in the area, no sound at the touch when you move the bone, and the patient feels no great pain, then there is no fracture; it may be a twist or a small fissure. In such cases, avoid moving it or applying pressure at all. Instead, apply the suitable medications, which will be discussed later, and then bind it gently.
Et scias, quod locus quando arcuatur, et scinditur in duo, praeter quod accidant in eo satagiae, uerumtamen remouetur omnis pars a compari sua: tunc oportet tibi, ut incipias ab hora tua ad rectificationem eius, aequando ipsum antequam accidat ei apostema calidum.	And know that when the place curves and splits into two, even if injuries happen there, nevertheless, each part is separated from its counterpart. Then it is necessary for you to begin immediately to set it straight, aligning it before a hot abscess forms there.
Si autem accidat ei apostema calidum, tunc dimitte ipsum diebus aliquot, donec quiescat apoestema.	But if a hot abscess does form, then leave it for several days until the abscess subsides.
Deinde aequa ipsum secundum quemcunque modum potest ex lenitate et ingenio.	Then set it straight in whatever way you can, with gentleness and skill.
Et scias, quod restauratio eius, et ipsius aequatio, est facilior quam ossis, in quo accidunt satagiae.	And know that the repair and alignment of it is easier than that of bone, in which injuries occur.
Deinde stringe ipsum, secundum quod ueniet tibi rememoratio eius.	Then bind it, as it comes back to your memory.
Si uero in osse sunt satagiae, tunc necessaria est extensio huius membri fracti ex duabus partibus, si manus sit, aut pes: aut cum manu tua, si membrum est paruum: aut cum duobus funibus, aut cum fune et manu.	If, however, there are dislocations in the bone, then it is necessary to extend this broken limb from both sides, whether it be a hand or foot: with your hand if the limb is small, or with two ropes, or with a rope and a hand.
Et sit positio tua qua ponis membrum super locum aequalem, secundum figuram suam naturalem, donec quando extenduntur partes membri fracti: tunc conare, ut redire facias illas additiones omni ingenio, et secundum quemcunque modum potes in locis suis: et caue secundum fortitudinem tuam, ne accidat super infirmum per operationem tuam dolor, neque laesio: et conare secundum fortitudinem tuam, ut applices unum duorum ossium cum socio suo, secundum meliores formas.	And let your position be such that you place the limb on a level place, in its natural shape, until the parts of the broken limb are extended: then try to restore those protrusions to their places by every means you can manage: and take care, with all your strength, that neither pain nor injury falls upon the patient due to your operation: and try, to the best of your ability, to apply one of the two bones to its companion, according to the best shapes.
Et oportet in illa hora, ut tentes et tangas ipsum manu tua.	And at that time, you must test and touch it with your hand.
Si ergo uides illic aliquid diuersum, rectifica ipsum, et aequa illud per quantitatem tuae uirtutis.	If, therefore, you see something different there, set it straight and align it to the best of your ability.
Et caue extensionem uehementem, et compressionem fortem, sicut faciunt multi stolidorum.	And beware of vigorous extension and strong compression, as many fools do.
Multotiens enim faciunt accidere per operationem illam suam apostema calidum, aut contractionem in membro: sicut iam uidimus illud ex operationibus eorum multotiens.	For often they cause, by that operation of theirs, a hot abscess or a contraction in the limb, as we have already seen from their operations many times.
Deinde annecte post aequationem, et decentem operationem, et stricturam illius membri, et quietem, et tranquillitatem: et mone infirmum super ipsum.	Then, after alignment, suitable operation, and securing of that limb, and rest, and tranquility, instruct the patient concerning it.
Et fac ipsum cauere, ne moueat ipsum in hora suarum uigiliarum, et somni sui apud reuolutionem suam, et agitationem suam, et apud egestionem suam, et omnes motus suos ultima uirtute sua.	And make him be cautious, so that he does not move it during his waking hours, his sleep, his turning, his movements, his bowel movements, and all his motions, with all his strength.
Et ut caueat, ne sit positio membri positio cum qua sequatur dolor.	And let him beware, lest the position of the limb be one that leads to pain.
Quod est, quia quando sentit in dispositione positionis membri dolorem, aut laesionem: tunc oportet, ut permutes ipsum ad dispositionem aliam ab illa cum qua non sentiat dolorem.	That is, when he feels pain or injury in the positioning of the limb: then you must adjust it to another position in which he does not feel pain.
Et caue cum illo, ut sit positio eius illa aequalis, recta: ut non accidat in membro tortuositas, quando restauratur.	And beware with him, that its position is level and straight: so that there is no twisting in the limb when it heals.
Et qualitas stricturae membri fracti est, secundum quod narro.	And the nature of securing the broken limb is as I describe.
Scias, quod membra fracta diuersificantur in paruitate sua, et eorum magnitudine, et ipsorum forma.	Know that broken limbs vary in their smallness, size, and shape.
Quae ergo ex eis sunt parua, sicut brachium, et digitus, et focile, et similia eis, tunc oportet, ut sit inuolutio panni mollis, subtilis.	Those of them which are small, like the arm, finger, shoulder blade, and similar, must be wrapped in a soft, thin cloth.
Quae autem ex eis sunt grossa, sicut coxa, et crus, oportet ut sit in eis inuolutio lata, dura.	Those which are large, like the thigh and leg, must have a wide, hard wrapping.
Quoniam ligamentum latum, connectit membrum magnum, et stringit ipsum ab omni parte strictura aequali, ita quod non ingreditur ipsum macula.	For a wide bandage joins a large limb and binds it with even tightness on every side, so that no mark enters it.
Deinde incipe post euacuationem tuam ab aequatione ponere linimentum conueniens ad illud, in stuppa leni super locum fracturae ipsius.	Then begin, after your setting has been completed, to place a suitable dressing for it, in soft tow over the site of the fracture.
Deinde incipe inuoluere ligamentum super locum fracturae ipsius tribus inuolutionibus, aut quatuor, secundum quod meretur membrum: et stringe manum tuam parumper cum ligamento.	Then begin to wrap the bandage over the site of the fracture with three or four wrappings, according to what the limb deserves: and gently tighten with your hand along with the bandage.
Deinde uade cum eo ad partem superiorem a loco fracturae, et stringe minus quam fuit strictura tua, qua strinxisti locum fractum.	Then move with it to the upper part from the site of the fracture, and bind it less tightly than your binding with which you secured the fracture site.
Deinde elonga te cum inuolutione a loco fracturae parumper, et mollifica stricturam paulatim, donec capias ex loco sano rem magnam.	Then extend yourself with the wrapping slightly from the site of the fracture, and gradually loosen the binding, until you take a large section from the healthy area.
Deinde accipe uittam aliam, et inuolue eam iterum super locum fractum inuolutionibus.	Then take another band, and wrap it again over the fractured site with wrappings.
Postea uade cum inuolutione ad partem inferiorem a fractura.	Afterward, move with the wrapping to the lower part from the fracture.
Et sit operatio tua in strictura inuolutionis, et mollificatione eius, secundum quod diximus in inuolutione prima superiore.	And let your operation in binding the wrapping and loosening it be as we said in the first wrapping above.
Deinde pone inter inuolutiones ex stuppa leni, aut panno, cum quo aequetur tortuositas fracturae, si est in ea tortuositas: et si non, non ponas in ea aliquid.	Then place between the wrappings some soft tow or cloth to balance any twisting in the fracture, if there is any twisting: and if not, do not put anything there.
Deinde inuolue super ipsam uittam aliam.	Then wrap another band over it.
Postea aequa super has inuolutiones astellas decenter factas statim, si non est in loco inflatio, neque apostema calidum.	Afterward, place decently made splints over these wrappings at once, if there is no swelling or hot abscess in the area.
Si autem in ea est inflatio, aut apostema: tunc pone super eam quod sedet illud apostema, et auferat inflationem: et dimitte eam aliquot diebus.	If, however, there is swelling or an abscess: then place over it what soothes the abscess and reduces the swelling: and leave it for a few days.
Deinde stringe super ipsam tunc astellas.	Then bind the splints over it at that time.
Et sint Astellae ex medietatibus cannae latae, rasae, formatae sapienter: aut sint ex ligno unde fiunt cribra, quae fiunt ex pino, aut geraid, id est, ramis palmae, aut calingi: et sit amplitudo omnis Astellae, secundum hanc formam, et hanc eandem figuram.	And let the splints be from the halves of broad reeds, scraped and skillfully shaped: or be from wood used for making sieves, which come from pine, or geraid, that is, palm branches, or calingi: and let the size of each splint be according to this form and figure.
Verumtamen oportet, ut Astella quae ponitur super ipsam tracturam, sit grossior et latior parumper, quam reliquae Astellae: et longitudo Astellae sit secundum quod licet membro ex magnitudine et paruitate.	However, it is necessary that the splint placed over the actual fracture be somewhat thicker and wider than the other splints: and the length of the splint should be appropriate to the size and smallness of the limb.
Deinde stringe super Astellas cum uitta alia, secundum stricturam primam eandem.	Then bind over the splints with another bandage, with the same tightness as the first one.
Deinde liga desuper cum filis decenter, secundum quod diximus de strictura: et est, ut sit strictura tua super locum fracturae plus: et quanto plus elongatur a fractura sit, strictura minor.	Then tie it decently above with threads, as we said regarding the tightness: and it is such that your binding is tighter over the site of the fracture: and the farther it is from the fracture, the looser the binding should be.
Et oportet, ut sint fila media in grossitudine et subtilitate, et sint ex lino molli.	And the threads should be of medium thickness and fineness, and made of soft linen.
Quoniam si fila sunt grossa, sicut uidi ex operatione stolidorum ponentium fila sua ex chordis lini tortis, est error magnus: quoniam cadit strictura cum eis extra qualitatem.	For if the threads are thick, as I have seen in the operations of fools who place their threads of twisted linen cords, it is a grave error: for with these the binding falls outside the quality needed.
Et fila subtilia iterum non conueniunt: quoniam tu non consequeris per stricturam cum eis illud quod uis.	And very thin threads also are not suitable: for you will not achieve with them the binding you desire.
Et non oportet, ut sit inter astellam et astellam minus quam digitus.	And there should be no less than a finger's width between each splint.
Quod si laeditur infirmus per extremitates astellarum post stricturam in locis sanis: tunc pone sub eis ex stuppa leni, aut lana carminata, donec nihil laedatur ex illo.	If the patient is harmed by the ends of the splints after the binding in healthy places, then place under them some soft tow or carded wool, so that nothing is injured from it.
Quando autem est fractura cum uulnere, et accidit disruptio in cute, ego ueniam cum rememoratione eius in capitulo secundum singularitatem.	But when there is a fracture with a wound, and a rupture happens in the skin, I will address it with recollection in a specific chapter.
Et scias, quod non omne membrum fractum oportet ut stringatur cum astellis a primo die.	And know that not every broken limb must be bound with splints on the first day.
Quod est, quia membrum quando est magnum, non oportet ut ponas superipsum astellas, nisi post quinque dies, aut septem, aut plus, secundum securitatem tuam ab euentu apostematis calidi.	This is because when a limb is large, splints should not be placed on it until after five days, or seven, or more, depending on your assurance against the occurrence of a hot abscess.
Et iste est modus emplastrorum, quibus restaurabant antiqui, quae ponuntur super fracturam et dislocationem.	And this is the method of plasters by which the ancients restored fractures and dislocations.
¶ Modus Emplastri communis abbreuiati, restaurans fracturam, conueniens pluribus complexionibus, praecipue mulieribus, et pueris: quoniam est ex eo super quod non uincit calor, neque frigus.	¶ Method of the common abbreviated plaster, restoring fractures, suitable for many constitutions, especially for women and children: for it is made from a material that neither heat nor cold overpowers.
Et est, ut accipias ex puluere molendini, et est medulla farinae quae pendet in parietibus molendini apud motum molae: et conficias ipsum sicut est, praeter quod cribelles ipsum cum albugine oui: et pone confectionem eius non spissam, neque subtilem.	And it is thus: take mill dust, which is the essence of flour that clings to the walls of the mill as the wheel turns: prepare it as it is, except sift it with egg white: and make the mixture neither thick nor thin.
Deinde utere eo.	Then use it.
¶ Modus Emplastri alius, restaurantis fracturam, et dislocationem, et torsionem.	¶ Another method of plaster, restoring fractures, dislocations, and twisting.
Recipe almess, et laudani, et acaciae, et enulae, et mugath: et suc, omnium ana ʒ X myrrhae, et aloes amborum ana ʒ V et alethil ʒ XX terantur omnia, et cribellentur, et misceantur cum aqua alethil: aut cum albugine oui, si complexio infirmi est calefacta.	Take almess, ladanum, acacia, elecampane, and mugath: and resin, each 10 drachms, myrrh, and aloe, each 5 drachms, and alethil 20 drachms; grind all, sift, and mix with alethil water: or with egg white if the patient's constitution is heated.
Deinde utere hoc emplastro.	Then use this plaster.
Nam ipsum est bonae compositionis, restaurat ossa fracta uelociter, et confert pluribus hominibus.	For it is of good composition, restores broken bones quickly, and benefits many people.
¶ Modus Emplastri alius, quod etiam confert fracturae et torsioni.	¶ Another method of plaster, which also aids fractures and twisting.
Recipe mugath, et mess, et alteae albae, omnium ana ʒ X myrrhae, et aloes amborum ana ʒ V acaciae ʒ VI boli armeni ʒ XX terantur omnia contritione multa, et cribellentur, et conficiantur cum aqua, aut albugine oui: et administrentur.	Take mugath, mess, and white mallow, each 10 drachms; myrrh and aloe, each 5 drachms; acacia 6 drachms; Armenian clay 20 drachms; grind all thoroughly, sift, and mix with water or egg white: and apply.
¶ Modus Emplastri ad iuncturas, et ossa separata ex locis suis: et sedat dolorem accidentem eis, et scissuram ossis, et fracturam.	¶ Method of plaster for joints and bones displaced from their places: it soothes the pain affecting them, a bone fissure, and a fracture.
Sumatur lana succida, et submergatur in aceto, et oleo decocto: et ponatur super locum.	Take raw wool, and soak it in vinegar and cooked oil: and place it over the area.
Et in hoc quidem Emplastro non est uirtus restaurationis, sed est bonum in sedatione apostematis calidi: et expellit dolores proprie.	And indeed in this plaster there is no restoring power, but it is good for soothing a hot abscess: and it effectively relieves pain.
¶ Modus Emplastri, quod restaurat os fractum.	¶ Method of plaster, which restores broken bone.
Sume folia fici surdi, et folia papaueris syluestris: et tere omnia, et fac emplastrum ex utrisque.	Take the leaves of wild fig and wild poppy: grind all, and make a plaster from both.
¶ Modus Emplastri abbreuiati, administrati apud restaurationem fracturae ossis: et quando tu uis resolutionem residui apostematis.	¶ Method of abbreviated plaster, applied for the restoration of bone fracture: and when you want the resolution of a remaining abscess.
Recipe radicis alteae, et camomillae, et florum uiolarum, et farinae herbi, omnium ana partem unam: terantur omnia, et conficiantur cum uino, si non est membrum calefactum.	Take marshmallow root, chamomile, violet flowers, and herb flour, each one part: grind all, and mix with wine, if the limb is not heated.
Si uero est calefactum, tunc confice illud cum aqua coriandri humidi, aut cum aqua, et utere eo.	But if it is heated, then prepare it with fresh coriander water, or with water, and use it.
¶ Modus Emplastri alius fortioris in resolutione, quam istud primum, quando accidit apostema durum apud restaurationem membri.	On the method of a stronger plaster in loosening than the first one, when a hard swelling occurs at the restoration of a limb.
Recipe radicis alteae, et seminis lini, et balaustiae, et melliloti, et samsuci, et floris uiolarum, et camomillae, omnium ana, partem unam, terantur omnia, et conficiantur cum aqua salicis, aut cum aqua dulci, aut cum uino.	Take marshmallow root, linseed, pomegranate flower, melilot, elderflower, violet flowers, and chamomile, one part of each, grind them all, and prepare with willow water, or fresh water, or wine.
Totum illud secundum caliditatem membri, et quietem caloris eius.	All of this is according to the warmth of the limb and the calm of its heat.
Hippocrates uero non dixit in libro suo, quod ponatur super membrum fractum apud restaurationem eius, nisi caerotum factum ex caera, et oleo: non aliud: et narrauit, ut sit medium inter grossitudinem et subtilitatem.	Hippocrates, however, did not say in his book that anything should be applied to the fractured limb at its restoration except a caeretum made of wax and oil, nothing else, and he stated it should be of moderate thickness and thinness.
Galeno autem uisum est, ut ponantur super membrum fractum apud restaurationem eius res, in quibus est exiccatio caliditatis, sicut myrrha, et aloes et olibanum, et similia eis ex eis quae narrauimus.	Galen, however, deemed it suitable to place on the fractured limb at its restoration substances with drying warmth, like myrrh, aloe, frankincense, and similar items among those we have described.
¶ Modus Emplastri quod confert ad laesionem et dolorem.	On the method of a plaster that helps with injury and pain.
Recipe mugath, et ciceris, et capillorum hominis incisorum, aut pennae auis, et alteae, et salis partes aequales: terantur, et cribellentur, et conficiantur: et fiat ex eo emplastrum.	Take equal parts of mugwort, chickpea, human hair cuttings or bird feathers, marshmallow, and salt; grind them, sieve them, and prepare them into a plaster.
Quantitas autem secundum quam oportet, ut remaneat ligatura, deinde soluatur, est, ut consideres tunc, si non accidit infirmo dolor, neque pruritus, neque mouetur os fractum a loco suo: tunc non resoluas ipsum diebus multis.	The duration for which the bandage should remain, then be loosened, is determined by observing whether the patient experiences no pain, no itching, and the fractured bone is not moved from its place; then do not loosen it for many days.
Quod si accidat ei in loco pruritus uehemens, aut dolor inquietans, aut inflatio: tunc propera, et solue in hora ipsum, et aufer emplastrum ab eo: et accipe pannum lenem, aut spongiam marinam lenem, humidam: et submerge eam in aqua tepida, et ablue cum ea locum, donec quiescat pruritus, et sedetur dolor.	But if there occurs intense itching in the area, or disturbing pain, or swelling, then hurry and loosen it immediately, and remove the plaster from it; and take a soft cloth or a gentle sea sponge, dampen it, soak it in warm water, and wash the area with it until the itching subsides and the pain is soothed.
Deinde dimitte membrum quiescere una hora.	Then let the limb rest for one hour.
Deinde pone super ipsum lanam succidam, submersam in aceto et oleo, aut oleo rosato: et liga super ipsum nocte una, donec sis securus ab apostemate calido, et quiescat inflatio membri, et remoueatur dolor eius.	Then place fresh wool soaked in vinegar and oil, or rose oil, over it, and bind it overnight until you are sure of being free from hot swelling, and the swelling of the limb subsides and its pain is relieved.
Deinde itera ipsum ad stricturam subtilem, et emplastrum paruum: et non stringas ipsum constrictione tua prima, et subtilia cum eo, donec sanetur.	Then reapply a light bandage and a small plaster; do not tighten it as you did at first, and apply gentle bandaging until it heals.
Quod si uides, quod apostema, et rubedo, et dolor, et inflatio, et omnia accidentia iam remota sunt penitus, et indiges emplastro et strictura, tunc itera illud sicut fecisti in primis aequaliter.	If you see that swelling, redness, pain, and all other symptoms are completely removed, and you need the plaster and bandage, then reapply it as you did at first, evenly.
Quod si non accidit membro aliquid ex eis quae diximus, tune non soluas ipsum, nisi post tres dies, aut quatuor, aut quinque, aut septem.	If none of those things happen to the limb, then do not loosen it until after three days, or four, or five, or seven.
Et quando que dimittitur uiginti diebus totum illud, secundum quod apparet tibi ex solutione membri, sicut diximus, donec quando adhaeret fractura, et approximat coagulatio carnis super eam: adde etiam tunc in strictura plus quam strictura tua prima tota: et adde etiam in ingrossatione cibi infirmi, secundum quod praecessit rememoratio eius.	And sometimes it is left for twenty days, all according to what appears to you upon loosening of the limb, as we have said, until the fracture adheres and the flesh coagulates over it; then increase the tightness more than your initial bandage completely, and also increase the intake of nourishing food, as previously mentioned.
Si autem uides locum fracturae iam exiccari, et extenuari plus quam oportet: tunc scias, quod nutrimentum prohibetur peruenire ad ipsum.	If, however, you see that the site of the fracture is becoming too dry and thin, then know that nourishment is prevented from reaching it.
Embroca ergo ipsum cum aqua tepida apud omnem uicem qua soluis ipsum, et sit illud in omnibus tribus diebus, et alleuia stricturam parumper.	Therefore, moisten it with warm water every time you loosen it, and do this every three days, and ease the bandage slightly.
Per hanc ergo operationem currit ad membrum nutrimentum, et sanatur uelociter.	Through this operation, nourishment flows to the limb, and it heals quickly.
Illud autem quod faciunt stolidi restauratores ex fractura membri uice alia, quando non restauratur in primis, secundum quod oportet, et restauratur secundum tortuositatem, est error ex operatione eorum, et timor magnus: et si esset rectum, dixissent illud antiqui in libris suis, et operati fuissent cum eo.	What foolish restorers do by setting the fracture improperly when it is not initially restored as it should be, and instead set it crookedly, is a mistake in their operation and a great danger; if it were correct, the ancients would have said so in their books and worked with it.
Et ego non uidi alicubi eorum uestigium in illo omnino, et rectius est, ut non fiat illud.	And I have not seen any trace of this in any of their writings at all, and it is better that it not be done.
¶ De factura accidente in capite.	On fractures occurring in the head.
Caput III.	Chapter III.
Species fracturae sunt diuersae, et figurae eius multae: et caussae eius sunt distinctae.	The types of fractures are diverse, and their forms are many, and their causes are distinct.
Nam ex fractura est quae fit a percussione cum ense: et percussio aufert os totum usquequo peruenit ad siphac, quod est sub osse, sicut facit axia in ligno: et propter illud nominatur haec species fracturae Axialis.	There is a type of fracture that occurs from a blow with a sword: and the blow removes the entire bone until it reaches the membrane beneath the bone, just as an ax does in wood; because of this, this type of fracture is called Axial.
Aut ensis incidit quiddam ossis, et incidit faciem eius, et non penetrat incisio usque ad ultimum eius: et nominatur haec species fracturae Kalaan, id est, Leuatio absolute.	Or a sword cuts a part of the bone, and cuts its surface, but the cut does not penetrate to its depth: and this type of fracture is called Kalaan, that is, a simple Lifting.
Et est uulnus duarum fracturarum, aut magnum, aut paruum.	And it is a wound of two fractures, either large or small.
Et ex fractura est, quae est contritio, aut attritio: et caussa eius est percussio cum lapide, aut casus super lapidem, aut eis similia.	And there is a fracture which is a crushing or abrasion: and its cause is a blow with a stone, or a fall onto a stone, or something similar.
Et haec fractura est etiam aut penetrans, quae iam appropinquat panniculo, quod est sub osse: aut est in facie ossis: et est uulnus harum duarum fracturarum etiam aut paruum, aut magnum.	And this fracture is either penetrating, which is already approaching the membrane beneath the bone, or it is on the surface of the bone: and the wound from these two types of fractures can also be either small or large.
Et ex fractura est, quae est occulta in osse in subtilitate capilli: et est fissura parua, et propter illud nominatur haec species fracturae Capillaris.	And there is a fracture which is hidden in the bone in the thinness of a hair: it is a small fissure, and for this reason, this type of fracture is called Capillary.
Et ex eis est fractura, quae fit a casu, aut percussione lapidis, et eis similium, quae faciunt ingredi laminam ossis ad interiora, et fit loco concauitas: sicut accidit caldariis aeris, quando occurrit eis percussio, et ingreditur pars eorum ad interiora.	And there is a fracture that occurs from a fall, or a blow with a stone, or something similar, which causes a layer of the bone to enter inward, creating a hollow in the area: just as happens to copper cauldrons, when they are struck, and a part of them goes inward.
Et plurimum quidem fit illud in capitibus humidorum ossium, sicut sunt capita puerorum.	And this most often happens in heads with soft bones, as in the heads of children.
Et fiunt omnibus istis speciebus fracturae satagiae separatae, et ego ueniam cum rememoratione illius totius in loco suo.	And in all these types of fractures, separate steps are needed, and I will address each of them in its place.
Et cognoscimus omnes has species fracturae per detectionem super eam, et inquisitionem earum cum tentis, et ablationem carnium corruptarum, quae sunt super eas.	And we recognize all these types of fractures by examining them, and by probing them with tenting, and by removing the corrupted flesh that is upon them.
Species uero Capillaris cognoscitur per hoc, quod detegitur super os, et abstergitur, et linitur super ipsum cum encaustro: fractura enim apparet nigra.	But the Capillary type is recognized by exposing the bone, wiping it, and applying ink over it: for the fracture appears black.
In curatione autem fracturae, considera in primis accidentia infirmi: tunc si uides ex accidentibus quod significet significatione manifesta super timorem, sicut uomitus cholerae, et extensio, et amissio intellectus, et abscisio uocis, et syncopis, et febris acuta, et egressus oculorum, et rubedo eorum, et similia eis ex accidentibus in pluribus dispositionibus, proculdubio non sanatur: et si uides accidentia non terrentia te, et speras salutem: tunc incipe curationem eius.	In the treatment of the fracture, first consider the symptoms of the patient: then if you see among the symptoms a clear sign of danger, such as vomiting bile, convulsions, loss of consciousness, loss of voice, fainting, acute fever, bulging of the eyes, redness of the eyes, and similar symptoms in various conditions, there is no doubt it is incurable: but if you observe non-threatening symptoms, and you hope for recovery, then begin treatment.
Quod est, si uenit ad te uulneratus in primis cum uulneratur: et est illud in diebus hyemis, tunc oportet, ut studeas in ablatione ossis ante diem quartumdecimum secundum omnem dispositionem.	That is, if the wounded person comes to you immediately when wounded: and it is during the days of winter, then you should take care to remove the bone before the fourteenth day, following all preparations.
Et si est in diebus aestatis, tunc oportet, ut properes remouere os ante diem septimum, antequam corrumpatur quod est sub osse ex panniculo, et accidant illa accidentia quae narrauimus.	And if it is during the days of summer, then you must hurry to remove the bone before the seventh day, before the membrane beneath the bone becomes corrupted, and those symptoms we have mentioned occur.
Quod si fractura ossis iam peruenit ad panniculum tangentem cerebrum, et est cum minutiis, et attractione: tunc oportet, ut incidas partem comminutam, et attritam, secundum quod narro tibi.	But if the bone fracture has already reached the membrane touching the brain, and is accompanied by fragments and pulling, then you must cut away the shattered and damaged part, as I instruct you.
Et est, ut radas caput uulnerati: et detege super os secundum quemcunque modum possibile est tibi: et secundum figuram uulneris, et quod leue est super infirmum.	This means you shave the head of the wounded: and expose the bone in whatever way possible for you, according to the shape of the wound, and whatever is least distressing for the patient.
Quod si accidat tibi apud detectionem ossis fluxus sanguinis, aut apostema calidum: tunc oppone ei cum eo quod oportet.	If, upon exposing the bone, there occurs a flow of blood, or a warm swelling, then apply what is necessary to it.
Et est, ut impleas locum cum pannis infusis in uino, et oleo rosato: et facias quiescere uulnus, donec sedetur apostema, et sis securus a fluxu sanguinis.	This is to fill the area with cloths soaked in wine and rose oil: and you let the wound rest until the swelling subsides, and you are safe from bleeding.
Deinde incipe in curatione ossis, et ablatione eius.	Then begin treating the bone, and removing it.
Et illud fit secundum unum duorum modorum.	And this is done according to one of two methods.
Modus quidem unus est, ut incidas os cum Incisorio strictae aciei: et haec est forma eius.	One method is to cut the bone with a knife of sharp edge: and this is its form.
Deinde administra Incisorium aliud post Incisorium latius eo parumper: et haec est forma eius.	Then use another knife after the first, slightly wider than it: and this is its form.
Deinde administra etiam Incisorium aliud latius etiam quam secundum.	Then use yet another knife, even wider than the second.
Et modus secundus est, ut sit apud te numerus Incisoriorum diuersorum: quorum quaedam sint latiora quibusdam, et quaedam eorum breuiora quibusdam: et sint in ultimo acuitatis extremitatum suarum, et sint ex ferro indo, aut fuled: et utere facilitate in percussione super Incisorium, ut non concutiat caput, et laedat ipsum.	And the second method is that you have a number of different knives: some of which are wider than others, and some shorter than others; and let the ends of their tips be sharp, and let them be made of Indian steel or hardened steel: and strike lightly upon the knife, so that it does not shake or injure the head.
Si autem os est forte, durum: tunc oportet, ut perfores in circumitu eius, antequam tu administres Incisoria, cum Terebris quae nominantur Terebra non profundantia: et non nominantur ita, nisi quoniam ipsa non pertranseunt terminum ossis carnei ad illud, quod est post ipsum: propterea, quod Terebro est extremitas rotunda super illud, quod est sub capite eius acuto, similis margini, et circulus paruus prohibet submergi, et pertransire spissitudinem ossis.	But if the bone is strong and hard, then you must drill around it, before you apply the knives, with drills called "non-penetrating drills": and they are called this because they do not go past the boundary of the bone to the tissue behind it; for the drill has a rounded end over the sharp head, like a border, and a small ring prevents it from going deep and passing through the thickness of the bone.
Et conuenit tibi, ut accipias ex istis Terebris numerum multum, quorum unumquodque conueniat quantitati spissitudinis ossis, donec praesens sit tibi omni craneo Terebrum: cuius extremitatis acutae quantitas in longitudine et breuitate sit, secundum quantitatem spissitudinis illius cranei.	And it is suitable for you to take a large number of these drills, each corresponding to the thickness of the bone, so that for any skull you have the appropriate drill: with the length and width of its sharp tip matching the thickness of that skull.
Et haec est forma trium specierum Terebrorum, uel Trepanorum.	And this is the form of three types of drills, or trepans.
Qualitas autem perforandi in circumitu ossis fracti, est: ut ponas Terebrum super os, et reuoluas ipsum digitis tuis, donec scias, os iam penetratum esse.	The manner of drilling around the broken bone is to place the drill on the bone, and rotate it with your fingers, until you know that the bone is already penetrated.
Deinde permuta Terebrum ad locum alium, et pone quod est inter omne foramen quantitatem grossitudinis radii, aut circiter illud.	Then move the drill to another spot, and place between each hole the width of a finger, or about that distance.
Deinde incide cum Incisoriis quod est inter omne foramen ex osse: et fac illud subtilius quam potes super illud ex fine leuitatis, sicut diximus, donec eradices os, aut manu tua, aut cum aliquo alio ex quibusdam instrumentis, quae parasti ad illud: sicut tenaliae, et forpices subtiles.	Then cut with the knives the bone between each hole: and do this as finely as you can, with gentle precision, as we said, until you remove the bone either by hand or with some other instrument you have prepared for this: like small forceps and fine scissors.
Et oportet, ut caueas omni cautela, ne tangat Terebrum, aut Incisorium aliquid ex siphac.	And you must take every caution that the drill or knife does not touch any part of the membrane.
Cum ergo circumieris os perforando, et separatum fuerit a siphac, si fuerit continuum cum eo, et extraxeris ipsum, oportet ut radas, et aeques extremitatem eius quod remanet in osse cum instrumento alio simili Incisorio.	When you have drilled around the bone, and it has been separated from the membrane, if it is attached to it and you have removed it, you should shave and smooth the edge of what remains in the bone with another instrument similar to a knife.
Verumtamen oportet, ut sit tenuis et subtilis magis aliis Incisoriis.	However, it should be thinner and more delicate than the other knives.
Quod si remanet aliquid ex ossibus paruis, et satagiae: tunc accipe ea cum facilitate, cum eo quod est apud te ex instrumentis.	If there remain any small bones or fragments, then remove them carefully with whatever instrument you have at hand.
Deinde cura uulnus post illud cum liciniis, et unguentis quae diximus.	Then treat the wound afterward with lint and ointments as we have described.
Et modus alius operationis est facilis, prorsus longinquus a timore: quem dixit Galenus, et laudauit ipsum laude magna: et iste est sermo eius.	And another method of operation is easy, entirely free from fear: which Galen mentioned and praised highly, and this is his description.
Necese est tibi in primis, ut incipias detegere partem ossis ex loco, in quo fractura est uehementior, et manifestior: donec quando detexeris illam partem, et posueris sub eo extremitatem huius Incisorii lenticularis, cuius haec est forma.	It is necessary for you to begin by exposing the part of the bone where the fracture is most severe and apparent: so that, when you have exposed that part, you place under it the end of this lenticular knife, of which this is the form.
Et pars lenticularis, lenis non incidit aliquid: et pars acuta eius in duabus lateribus eius uadens in longitudinem, sicut uides: ut sit pars lenticularis innixa ad siphac, facie eius incidente acuta in osse.	And the lenticular part, smooth, does not cut anything: and its sharp part goes lengthwise on two sides, as you see: so that the lenticular part rests upon the membrane, with its sharp side cutting the bone.
Deinde percute super Incisorium a parte una cum malleo paruo, donec incidas totum os cum facilitate, sicut reuoluitur: et tu sis securus ex panniculo, quod non accidat in eo accidens, quod timeas prorsus.	Then strike on one side of the knife with a small hammer, until you cut the whole bone with ease, as it rotates: and you can be certain regarding the membrane, that nothing will happen to it that would cause you fear at all.
Et si medicans non esset stultior hominum, et magis ignarus eius, nec esset dormiens, tunc si remaneret aliquid annexum ossi ex panniculo in quibusdam locis, incideret ipsum ab eo cum extremitate incisorii lenticularis ipsius, et liberaret ipsum ab eo cum facilitate.	And if the doctor were not the most foolish or ignorant of men, nor asleep, then if anything from the membrane remained attached to the bone in certain places, he would cut it away from there with the tip of his lenticular knife, and free it from there with ease.
Liberatur enim ab eo sine nocumento, et absque timore.	For it is freed from it without harm, and without fear.
Et si fractura ossis non penetret usque ad panniculum, et illud quod abscinditur ab osse est facies eius, et remanent in ipso asperitas et satagiae subtiles: tunc oportet, ut radas illam asperitatem, et eradices illas satagias cum rasoriis subtilibus ex quibus praeparasti numerum diuersarum quantitatum, ut sit tibi possibile administrare ex eis in omni loco conuenientiora eorum, et rectiora ipsorum, secundum illud ad quod perducit te operatio, et ipsum os fractum, et figura eius.	And if the fracture of the bone does not reach the membrane, and what is removed from the bone is its surface, and slight roughness and small fragments remain on it: then it is necessary to shave that roughness and remove those fragments with fine rasps, of which you have prepared a number in various sizes, so that you are able to use whichever is most suitable and appropriate in every place, according to what the operation leads you to and the shape of the broken bone.
Et oportet, ut administres in rasione tua, qua in primis radis os, latius illorum rasoriorum.	And you should begin your rasping with the widest of those rasps.
Deinde utere post ipsum subtiliore eo.	Then use after it one that is finer.
Et non cesses facere illud secundum continuitatem, donec peruenias ad administrandum tenuius, et subtilius eorum omnium.	And do not stop doing this in sequence, until you reach using the finest and most delicate of them all.
In unaquaque autem fissurarum reliquarum paruarum capillarium, et fractura subtili, oportet ut administres curam, secundum illud quod perducit ad rectificationem eius.	And in each of the remaining small cracks or hairline fractures, and in slight fractures, it is necessary to apply care according to what will bring it to proper alignment.
Et est res quae non occultatur super omnem, cui in hac arte est quodlibet studium, et stetit super illud quod scripsimus, et ostendimus in fracturis magnis.	And this is something that is not hidden from anyone who has any experience in this art and has followed what we have written and demonstrated regarding major fractures.
Si autem panniculus detectus retinetur, quando tu eradicas os: tunc oportet, ut accipias pannum lini, secundum quantitatem uulneris, et submerge ipsum in oleo rosa, et pone ipsum super orificium uulneris.	But if the exposed membrane remains intact when you remove the bone, then you should take a linen cloth, according to the size of the wound, and soak it in rose oil, and place it over the opening of the wound.
Deinde accipe pannum alium duplicatum, aut triplicatum, et submerge ipsum in uino et oleo rosa, et pone ipsum super pannum primum: et fac illud quam facilius potes super illud, ut non grauetur siphac.	Then take another cloth folded double or triple, and soak it in wine and rose oil, and place it over the first cloth: and do this as gently as you can over it, so that the membrane is not burdened.
Deinde administra supra ipsum ligamentum latum: et non stringas ipsum, nisi per quantitatem quae teneat uulnus tantum: et dimitte ipsum cum hac cura die uno, aut diebus duobus, donec sis securus ab apostemate calido.	Then place a wide bandage over it: and do not tighten it, except to the degree that it holds the wound only: and leave it with this care for a day or two, until you are free from the risk of a hot abscess.
Deinde solue ipsum, et administra post illud medicinas cum quibus est superflua siccitas: sicut radices lilii, et farina herbi, et puluis thuris, et aristolochia, et similia eis: et est, ut facias ex istis medicinis puluerem, qui puluerizetur super uulnus sicut sunt siccae.	Then loosen it, and afterwards apply medicines that provide excess dryness: such as lily roots, flour of herbs, frankincense powder, birthwort, and the like: and you should make a powder from these medicines, which is sprinkled over the wound while they are dry.
Et uniuersaliter administra in illo omnem medicinam de proprietate cuius est ut abstergat, et non mordicet.	And generally, administer any medicine to it whose property is to cleanse without biting.
Et caue secundum tuam uirtutem, ut uulnus in curatione tua sit limpidum: neque sit in ea sordes unguenti, neque sordicies.	And take care, to the best of your ability, that the wound in your treatment remains clear: let there be no filth of ointment or foulness.
Neque dimittas uirus aggregari in eo omnino: quoniam quando uirus aggregatur super panniculum cerebri, corrumpit eum, et putrefacit ipsum, et accidit super infirmum post illud malicia magna: et iam accidit in siphac capitis, cum mundificatur ab eo os, praecipue quando negligitur cura eius, nigredo in superficie eius.	Do not allow any pus to gather there at all: for when pus gathers over the cerebral membrane, it corrupts it and decays it, and a great affliction befalls the patient afterward: and it has already happened in the membrane of the head, when the bone is cleaned from it, especially when its care is neglected, that blackening occurs on its surface.
Considera ergo tunc, si accidunt infirmo accidentia quae diximus, scias, quia est pernitiosum proculdubio.	Therefore, consider then, if the patient suffers from the symptoms we have mentioned, you will know without a doubt that it is dangerous.
Et si nigredo non accidit, nisi a medicina quae ponitur super ipsum, et est in uirtute illius medicinae, ut faciat illam nigredinem: tunc oportet, ut accipias ex melle partem unam, ex oleo rosato partes tres, et linias cum eis pannum.	And if blackening occurs only from the medicine applied to it, and it is in the power of that medicine to cause that blackening, then it is necessary to take one part of honey, three parts of rose oil, and dip a cloth in them.
Deinde pone ipsum super siphac: postea cura ipsum cum speciebus curationis quae conueniunt, donec sanetur.	Then place it over the membrane: afterwards, treat it with appropriate healing substances until it heals.
¶ De restauratione Nasi quando frangitur.	On the restoration of the nose when it is broken.
Caput IIII.	Chapter IV.
Scias, quod non franguntur ex Naso nisi duae partes eius superiores simul, aut una earum: propterea, quod ipse sunt duo ossa: quoniam inferius eius est cartilaginosum, non frangitur, et non accidit ei nisi attritio, et tortuositas, et simitas.	Know that only two upper parts of the nose can break together, or one of them: this is because it consists of two bones, since the lower part is cartilage, it does not break, but only suffers abrasion, twisting, and bending.
Si frangitur una duarum partium eius, tunc oportet, ut intromittas digitum paruum in foramen Nasi, et aeques illam fracturam digito tuo indice, et pollice deforis, donec redeat Nasus super figuram suam naturalem: et fiat illud arte, cum facilitate.	If one of its two parts is broken, then you should insert your little finger into the nasal cavity and align the fracture with your index finger and thumb from the outside, until the nose returns to its natural shape: and this should be done skillfully, with ease.
Et caue, ne accidat per operationem tuam illam super infirmum dolor.	And be careful that no pain is caused to the patient by your operation.
Quod si fractura est in superiore parte Nasi, et non perueniat ad illam digitus: tunc oportet, ut aequetur extremitate radii, in quo sit grossitudo parua.	But if the fracture is in the upper part of the nose and your finger cannot reach it, then it should be aligned with the end of a rod, which has a small thickness.
Si uero fractura est in duabus partibus, tunc fac simile illius: et propera cum restauratione eius in die primo fracturae, si est possibile.	If the fracture is in both parts, then do the same: and hasten to restore it on the first day of the fracture, if possible.
Et si non, tunc post diem septimum, aut decimum apud quietem apostematis calidi.	If not, then after the seventh or tenth day, when the swelling has calmed.
Deinde intromitte in extremitate Nasi licinium ex panno lini, si fractura est in parte una: aut intromite duo licinia si fractura est in duabus partibus: et sit in licinio grossitudo tantae quantitatis, quae impleat foramen Nasi.	Then insert into the end of the nose a linen strip if the fracture is in one part, or two strips if the fracture is in both parts: and let the strip have enough thickness to fill the nasal cavity.
Et dixerunt quidam antiquorum restauratorum, ut infundantur licinia in butyro, et permutentur omni die.	And some of the restorers of old have said that the strips should be soaked in butter and changed every day.
Illud uero non uidetur mihi, imo oportet, ut infundatur licinium in albugine oui, confecto cum puluere molendini.	However, this does not seem advisable to me; rather, the strip should be soaked in egg white, prepared with millstone powder.
Deinde dimitte licinium, donec firmetur os, et induretur cartilago.	Then leave the strip in place until the bone sets and the cartilage hardens.
Et quandoque intromittitur in Nasum loco licinii penna anseris, postquam inuoluitur super ipsam pannus lenis: et fit, quod ipsa tenet fracturam Nasi uehementius, et ut non prohibeatur infirmus ab inspiratione.	Sometimes, instead of a strip, a goose feather wrapped with a soft cloth is inserted into the nose, which holds the nasal fracture more firmly and does not prevent the patient from breathing.

If the nasal bones are fractured into small pieces or are crushed, then you must make an incision over them and extract them with suitable instruments.

¶ De restauratione Mandibulae inferioris, quando disrumpitur.	On the restoration of the lower jaw when it is fractured.

Deinde pone super mandibulam fractam caerotum.	Then apply cerate over the fractured jaw.

Then bind it from above, according to how the bandage has been prepared for you, so that it is appropriately applied and does not move; and instruct the patient to rest quietly: and provide them with soft broths for nourishment.
Si autem estimas, quod iam alteratur aliquid figurae aliquo modorum: tunc propera, et solue ipsum in die tertio.	If you think that some part of its shape has already changed in any way, then hasten and loosen it on the third day.

Then correct what has been altered, and apply a plaster made of millstone powder with egg white, or with sesame flour, after you have removed the cerate: and place soft wool over the plaster.
Dum ergo permanet annexum illud emplastrum super ipsum, et non alteratur ossis dispositio, dimitte ipsum, ne soluas, donec sanetur: aut stringe fracturam.	As long as the plaster remains attached over it and the bone’s alignment does not change, leave it undisturbed; do not loosen it until it heals: or tighten the fracture.
Multotiens enim stringitur haec fractura in tribus septimanis.	For often this fracture is set within three weeks.
Quod si interim accidat apostema calidum, tunc administra illud quod diximus multotiens in sedatione eius, donec remoueatur illud apostema.	If a hot swelling occurs in the meantime, then apply what we have frequently mentioned to alleviate it, until the swelling subsides.
Et si fractura est cum uulnere, considera.	And if the fracture is accompanied by a wound, consider it.
Namsi iam separatur ex osse frustum, aut a satagie: tunc subtilia te in hoc, ut auferas illa frusta satagiae cum eo quod conuenit tibi in ablatione eorum ex instrumentis.	For if a piece of bone or splinter is already separated, then proceed carefully to remove those bone fragments with the instruments suitable for their extraction.
Si uero orificium uulneris est strictum, tunc amplifica ipsum cum phlebotomo secundum quantitatem necessitatis tuae.	But if the wound opening is narrow, then enlarge it with a lancet according to your need.

¶ De restauratione Furculae, quando frangitur.	On the restoration of the clavicle when it is fractured.

Plurimum quidem frangitur Furcula ab anteriore parte ex parte humeri, et fractura eius fit secundum unum trium modorum.	The clavicle is most often fractured from the front, near the shoulder, and its fracture occurs in one of three ways.
Aut enim frangitur et separatur in duo, praeter quod accidant in ea satagiae: et est facilius ad restaurationem eius.	Either it is fractured and split into two without splinters occurring; this is the easiest type to restore.
Aut ita, quod accidunt in osse satagiae: et est difficilius ad restaurationem.	Or it occurs with splinters in the bone; this is more difficult to restore.
Aut ita, quod est fractura cum uulnere.	Or it occurs as a fracture with an accompanying wound.

When the fracture is without a wound, the procedure is to have two assistants present, one to hold support near the fractured clavicle, and the other to extend the neck toward that side.
Deinde aequa fracturam digitis tuis, donec fiat forma eius, secundum quod oportet: et non sit in ea eminentia, neque concauitas.	Then align the fracture with your fingers until it takes its proper shape; let there be no bump or hollow.

If you need more extension, then you should place a ball of cloth or wool under the patient’s armpit, its size according to your needs; extend, raise the clavicle, and press the ball with your hand until you align the fracture as required.
Quod si non potes trahere extremitatem Furculae ad exteriora: propterea, quia peruenit ad profundum: tunc oportet, ut resupinetur infirmus super ceruicem suam: et ponas sub humero eius puluinar medium in magnitudine, et premat minister humerum eius ad inferiora, donec eleuetur os Furculae quod est in profundo ad sursum: atque tunc rectifica fracturam, et aequa eam digitis tuis.	If you cannot pull the end of the clavicle outward because it lies too deep, then the patient should be laid back on their neck, and you should place a medium-sized pillow under their shoulder, and the assistant should press down on their shoulder until the clavicle bone, which is deep, rises upward; then set the fracture and align it with your fingers.
Si autem sentis sathaiatum ex Furcula, et mouetur: tunc oportet, ut findas super illud, et extrahas ipsum alsathaiatum cum facilitate.	If you feel a splinter in the clavicle and it moves, then you should make an incision over it and carefully remove the splinter.
Si autem est retentum in osse, tunc ingenia in incisione eius cum uno Incisoriorum quae praeparasti ad illud, postquam tu posueris sub Furcula instrumentum quod seruat sifach: et est instrumentum ex ligno, aut ex ferro.	But if it is lodged in the bone, then proceed with the incision using one of the chisels you have prepared for this, after you have placed under the clavicle an instrument called a “sifach support,” which is a tool made of wood or iron.
Et haec est forma eius.	And this is its shape.
Assimilatur cochleari cui non est concauitas.	It resembles a spoon without a concavity.
Et sit latitudo eius secundum illud quo indiges eo ex magnitudine ossis, et paruitate eius.	Its width should match what you need, depending on the size and thickness of the bone.
Longitudo uero eius etiam secundum quod possibile est tibi ad operationem.	Its length should be as long as is practical for the procedure.
Et habeat duas extremitates, sicut uides: et extremitas una sit ampla, et altera stricta.	It has two ends, as you see: one end is wide, and the other narrow.
Si autem disruptio uulneris quod scidisti quando extraxisti sathaiatum ossis, est ampla, et securaris ab apostemate calido: tunc aggrega duo labia uulneris cum sutura.	If the wound you incised to remove the bone splinter is wide, and you are free from concern about a hot swelling, then bring together the two edges of the wound with a suture.
Et si disruptio est parua, aut times apostema calidum: tunc imple uulnus cum pannis, et puluillis secundum quantitatem scissurae uulneris.	If the wound is small or you fear a hot swelling, then fill the wound with cloths and small pads according to the size of the wound incision.
Quod si accidat apostema calidum, tunc infunde pannum in oleo rosato, et aceto, et uino, et pone super ipsum.	If a hot swelling occurs, then soak a cloth in rose oil, vinegar, and wine, and place it over the area.
Strictura uero ossis quando est absque uulnere, et absque sectione, est, ut ponas super os emplastrum factum ex farina molendini cum albugine oui.	If the bone needs setting without a wound and without an incision, place over the bone a plaster made of millstone flour with egg white.
Et pone stuppam lenem super ipsum.	And place soft tow over it.
Deinde pone spheram sub titillico eius, si indiges illa.	Then place a ball under the armpit if you need it.

Deinde pone puluillum duplicatum super stuppam et emplastrum.	Then place a folded pad over the tow and plaster.
Deinde accipe astellam ex tabula subtili, cuius latitudo sit trium digitorum, et in longitudine similiter.	Then take a splint made from a thin board, about three fingers wide and of similar length.
Deinde inuolue eam in panno.	Then wrap it in cloth.
Deinde inuolue pannum illum cum astella in loco alhimemati qui cadit super fracturam et locum.	Then wrap that cloth and splint in place with the himematem over the fracture site and location.
Deinde pone alhimemati super fracturam sicut reuoluiur, et inuolue eam super collum eius, et sub titillico eius sano, et sub titillico eius infirmo: et reduc eam super omnem partem, et quomodo uides quod strictura tenet fracturam strictura decenti: et est illud, quod non occultatur super te, et totum, ut non remoueatur astella desuper os fractum.	Then place the himematem over the fracture as it wraps around, and wind it over the patient’s neck, under the healthy armpit, and under the injured armpit; adjust it around so that the binding holds the fracture in a fitting way. This is what will ensure the splint is not removed from above the fractured bone.
Et propter hoc oportet, ut aspicias infirmum in omni die.	For this reason, you must check the patient every day.
Quotiens ergo mollificatur ligamentum, et uides astellam iam remoueri: tunc rectifica illud, et stringe ligamentum.	Whenever the bandage loosens and you see the splint has already shifted, then adjust it and tighten the bandage.
Deinde pone somnum infirmi super dorsum eius, et pone sub titillico eius apud somnum eius puluinar paruum in nocte, ut cum eo eleuetur adiutorium eius a latere ipsius.	Then place the patient's sleep on his back, and place a small cushion under his head near his sleep at night, so that his support is lifted from his side with him.
Eleuatur ergo fractura furculae per eleuationem humeri.	Thus, the fracture of the collarbone is raised by the elevation of the shoulder.

Deinde renoua emplastrum, si uideris faciem eius ad illud.	Then renew the dressing if you see his face inclined toward it.
Et adde in strictura, et dimitte ipsum, donec restauretur et coaguletur.	And add to the compression, and leave it until it heals and hardens.

And indeed, the fracture of the collarbone is generally secured and strengthened in twenty-eight days: and sometimes it happens in some people in less time.

¶ De restauratione fracturae Pectoris.	¶ On the restoration of a chest fracture.
Caput VIII.	Chapter VIII.
Pectus quandoque frangitur in medio sui, et raro accidit illud.	The chest is sometimes fractured in the middle, and this rarely occurs.
Extremitates eius quidem plurimum franguntur, et comminuuntur ex accidentibus eius.	Indeed, its ends are mostly fractured and crushed due to accidents.
Quando frangitur eius medium, quod declinat ad inferiora, et accidit ei dolor uehemens, et difficultas in anhelitu, et tussis: et fortasse euomit sanguinem, et declaratur concauitas in osse fracto, et non occultatur sensui, Restauratio eius est, ut resupinetur infirmus super dorsum suum: et pone inter Spatulas eius puluinar.	When its middle part fractures, which slopes downward, and severe pain, difficulty in breathing, and cough occur: and perhaps he vomits blood, and a concavity is visible in the broken bone, and it is not hidden from the senses. Deinde preme humeros eius, et aggrega costas a duabus partibus uel lateribus cum manibus: et subtiliare in aequatione eius, secundum omnem modum quo possibile est illud et conuenit, donec redeat figura ossis, secundum quod oportet.	Then press his shoulders and bring together the ribs on both sides with your hands: and smooth them in alignment, in every way possible and fitting, until the shape of the bone returns to what is required.
Deinde pone super ipsum emplastrum et stuppam, et pone desuper astellam ex tabula subtili ex salice, aut chalingi, et ex eis similibus in leuitate, postquam tu inuoluis eam in panno.	Then place a plaster and wool over it, and place a splint from a thin board of willow, or poplar, and others similar in lightness, after wrapping it in cloth.

Then carefully bind it over the broken bone, so it does not move: and take the bandage around to the back several times, and tighten it with appropriate compression.
Deinde considera ligaturam in omni hora, et quotienscumque mollificatur, stringe eam: et si necessitas eius prouocat ad solutionem eius apud pruritum, uel corrosionem quae accidit in loco, aut dolorem, aut apostema: tunc propera, et solue eam, et eradica emplastrum, et rectifica quod accidit cum modo curationis eius. Deinde reduc emplastrum, si uides ad illud faciem: et annecte ei stricturam, donec sanetur.	Then replace the plaster, if you see it necessary: and attach the compression until it heals.

Et dixerunt quidam eorum: ponatur super locum uentosa, deinde sugatur cum fortitudine, et est similius ratiocinationi.	And some of them said: place a cupping glass on the site, then draw with strength, and this is more reasonable.
Verumtamen timetur, quod attrahat uentosa superfluitates ad locum, propter dispositionem debilitatis.	However, it is feared that the cupping glass may draw excess fluids to the site, due to the weakened condition.
Et dixerunt quidam eorum: oportet ut cooperiatur locus cum lana quae iam submersa est in oleo calido: et ponantur puluilli inter Costas, donec impleatur: ut sit ligatura aequalis quando inuoluis eam secundum rotunditatem. But if a severe condition arises that the patient cannot bear, and the bone pierces the membrane with an injurious prick, and we fear for the patient, then we must cut above the site and expose the fractured rib.
Deinde ponamus super ea instrumentum quod conseruat sifac, cuius rememoratio praecessit: et incidamus os cum facilitate, et incidamus ipsum.	Then place on it the instrument that preserves the tissue, previously mentioned: and cut the bone with ease, and remove it.
Deinde aggregemus duo labia uulneris, si est magnum cum sutura: et curemus ipsum cum emplastris, donec sanetur.	Then bring together the two lips of the wound, if it is large, with a suture: and treat it with plasters until it heals.

¶ De restauratione fracturae Spondilium dorsi et colli.	¶ On the restoration of fractures of the vertebrae of the back and neck.
Caput X.	Chapter X.
Ossibus quidem colli quandoque aduenit fractura, et raro accidit eis attritio: et similiter Spondilibus dorsi etiam.	Fracture sometimes happens to the bones of the neck, and rarely do they suffer attrition: the same is true of the vertebrae of the back.
Cum ergo accidit illud alicui, et uis scire, an sanetur, an non sanetur: tunc confidera.	When this happens to someone and you wish to know whether it will heal or not, then consider this.
Nam si uides manus eius iam mollificatas, et stupefactas, et mortuas: et non potest mouere eas, neque extendere eas, neque constringere eas: et quando constringis eas cum digitis tuis, et pungis eas cum acu, non sentit illud, et non sentit in eis dolorem: tunc scias, quia non sanatur, et in pluribus dispositionibus est mortale.	For if you see that his hands are softened, numb, and lifeless, and he cannot move them, nor extend, nor grip them; and when you press them with your fingers and prick them with a needle, he feels nothing, nor any pain: then you know it will not heal, and in most cases, it is fatal.
Quod si mouet eas, et sentit in eis constrictionem cum digitis et puncturam: tunc scias, quia nucha ossis salua est, et quod infirmus sanabitur cum curatione.	But if he moves them and feels compression and pricking in them when touched with fingers, then you know that the spinal marrow is intact, and the patient will heal with treatment.
Quod si eueniat Spondilibus dorsi simile illius, et uis scire, an sanetur etiam, an non: tunc considera ad pedes eius: tunc si uides quod mollificati sunt, et accidit in eis illud quod accidit in manibus: deinde quando iacet super dorsum suum, egreditur uentositas et egestio absque uoluntate: et quando resupinatur super uentrem suum, egreditur egestio sine uoluntate: et quando resupinatur super dorsum suum, et uult mingere, et non potest: tunc scias, quia est mortale.	And if something similar occurs with the vertebrae of the back and you wish to know whether he will heal or not, then look at his feet: if you see that they are softened and what happened in the hands also occurs in them; then, when he lies on his back, he passes gas and waste involuntarily, and when he lies on his stomach, waste exits without control; and when he lies on his back and wishes to urinate but cannot, you know it is fatal.
Non ergo labores in curatione eius.	Do not therefore put effort into his treatment.
Si uero non accidit ei aliquid ex illo, res est leuior.	But if none of that happens to him, the case is lighter.
Et curatio eius quod accidit ex illo est, ut coneris sedationem apostematis calidi: ita, ut ponas super Spondilem attritam oleum rosatum solum: aut cum uitellis oui assati.	And the treatment for what happens in such cases is to attempt to reduce any hot swelling: place rose oil on the affected vertebra alone, or with the yolk of roasted eggs.
Pone super ipsam illud aliquotiens in die, donec quando sedatur apostema calidum: tum pone super locum unum ex emplastris confortantibus, exiccantibus, et stringe super ipsum cum ligamento: et praecipe infirmo, ut utatur quiete et tranquillitate.	Apply this several times a day, until the hot swelling subsides: then place on the spot one of the strengthening, drying plasters, and bind it with a bandage: and instruct the patient to rest in peace and quiet.
Nec dormiat super partem cum qua inuenit dolorem, donec sanetur.	He should not sleep on the side where he feels pain until he is healed.
Quod si accidit apud attritionem ossis sathaiatum, aut aliquid quod iam separatum fit ex eo: tunc oportet, ut seces super cutem, et auferas illud.	If a bone fragment has loosened at the site of attrition, or anything else becomes detached, you must cut above the skin and remove it.
Deinde aggrega duo labia uulneris, si est magnum, cum sutura.	Then bring together the two lips of the wound, if it is large, with sutures.
Deinde cura ipsum cum aliquo incarnatiuo, ex unguentis, donec sanetur.	Then treat it with a healing ointment until it heals.
Si uero frangitur postremum ossis alhausos, et est finis caudae: tunc oportet, ut intromittas pollicem manus sinistrae in anum, et aeques os fractum manu altera, secundum quod possibile est, et secunda aequatione.	If the last bone of the spine is fractured, at the end of the tail: you must insert the thumb of your left hand into the anus and align the broken bone with the other hand as much as possible, in a second adjustment.
Deinde pone super ipsum, et aufer ipsum: et cura uulnus cum curatione quae praecessit, donec sanetur.	Then place over it, and remove it: and treat the wound as previously described, until it heals.

Tunc oportet, ut ponas emplastrum et stricturam, si non accidit membro apostema.	Then it is necessary to apply a plaster and a binding, if there is no swelling in the member.
Quod si accidat in eo apostema: tunc dimitte ipsum diebus aliquot, donec sedetur apostema calidum, deinde redi ad curationem tuam.	If swelling occurs, then leave it for a few days until the swelling subsides, then return to your treatment.
Strictura autem eius ita sit.	The binding should be done as follows.
Oportet ut inuoluas super fracturam imema duram, id est, bindam: et dupla eam bis, aut ter: et remaneat ex ea superfluitas.	You should wrap a hard binding over the fracture, that is, a binding band, and fold it two or three times, leaving an excess.
Deinde inuolue crus, donec perueniat calcaneus apud radicem natis.	Then wrap the leg until it reaches the heel at the base of the buttocks.
Et intromitte filum longum inter Coxam et crus prope genu ad inferiora, et ueniant extremitates fili a superiori parte duarum partium.	And insert a long cord between the hip and the leg near the knee downward, so that the ends of the cord emerge from the upper part of the two sides.
Deinde reuolue super Crus et Coxam quod superfluit ex alimema.	Then wrap the excess of the binding over the thigh and hip.
Deinde pone super Coxam in loco fracturae ipsius astellas, et pone ex eis astellam unam super os cruris.	Then place splints on the hip at the site of the fracture and place one splint on the thigh bone.
Deinde imple uacuitates inter crus et coxam cum pannis lenibus, ut aequetur strictura.	Then fill the gaps between the thigh and hip with soft cloths to balance the binding.
Deinde incipe cum strictura forti a medio super locum fracturae tribus inuolutionibus, aut quatuor.	Then begin a firm binding from the middle over the fracture site with three or four wraps.
Et quanto plus elongaris cum ligamento a loco fracturae, sit strictura tua minor et mollior, et lenior.	And as you extend the bandage farther from the fracture site, let the binding be looser, softer, and gentler.
Deinde intende ad duas extremitates fili, quod tu intromisisti inter coxam et crus.	Then tighten the two ends of the cord that you inserted between the hip and the thigh.
Liga ergo cum utrisque tres astellas quae sunt desuper.	Then tie both ends to the three splints above.
Deinde uade cum duabus extremitatibus fili ad inferiora, donec peruenias ad cauillam pedis.	Then move both ends of the cord downward until you reach the ankle of the foot.
Et cum utrisque stringe etiam extremitates astellarum a parte altera: ut non remoueatur ligamentum ex loco suo.	And with both, secure the ends of the splints on the other side, so that the binding does not move out of place.
Deinde dimitte stricturam super illud, dum permanet, ita quod non accidat membro corrosio, neque apostema, neque inflatio, et similia eis.	Then release the bandage over it while it remains, so that there does not occur corrosion, nor swelling, nor inflammation to the limb, or anything similar.
Quod si accidat aliquid ex illo.	But if something happens from it.
Propera ergo cum solutione eius, et rectifica illud quod accidit inde totum, secundum quod diximus multotiens.	Then proceed quickly with its release, and correct all that happened from it, according to what we have said many times.
Si uero in osse est frustum ex osse quod pungat: tunc oportet, ut aeques illud, si est tibi possibile.	If there is a piece of bone in the bone itself that is sharp: then it is necessary that you smooth it, if it is possible for you.
Et si non, finde super illud, et remoue illud: et cura cum eo cuius praecessit rememoratio, donec sanetur.	And if not, cut upon it and remove it, and treat it with that which was previously mentioned, until it heals.
Et quandoque stringitur haec fractura Coxae, praeter quod addantur ei astellae, sicut diximus in adiutorio et brachio.	And sometimes this fracture of the hip is bound, with splints added to it, as we have said for the arm and thigh.
Verumtamen in restauratione eius taliter fracta, non accidit infirmo claudicatio.	However, in the restoration of it being thus fractured, limping does not happen to the patient.
Et si restauratur sola, praeter quod applicetur ei crus alterum, tunc necesse est, ut claudicet ille cuius est, semper.	But if it is restored alone, without the other leg being applied to it, then it is necessary that he to whom it belongs limps, always.
Et scias, quod Coxa stringitur in quinquaginta diebus, uel additur parum, aut minuitur parum: totum illud est secundum diuersitatem complexionum, et reliquarum dispositionum.	And know that the hip is set in fifty days, or a little more or less; all of it depends on the diversity of temperaments and other conditions.
¶ De restauratione fracturae Rotulae genu.	On the restoration of the fracture of the kneecap.
Caput XVI.	Chapter XVI.
Scias, quod Rotulae genu raro accidit fractura, sed accidit ei attritio multotiens.	Know that a fracture of the kneecap rarely happens, but it often suffers abrasion.
Si uero accidit ei fractura, tunc non nisi aut fissura, aut foramen in partibus suis.	If indeed a fracture occurs, then it is nothing other than a crack or hole in its parts.
Et est illud cum uulnere, et absque uulnere.	And it is either with a wound or without a wound.
Et statur super illud totum cum sensu.	And it is all felt with sensitivity.
Et restauratio eius est ita, ut aequetur quod disruptum est ex partibus eius cum digitis, donec aggregetur, et coniungatur secundum quod possibile est ex aequatione et facilitate, et fabricatione.	And its restoration is such that the parts which are disrupted are aligned with the fingers, until they are joined and connected as evenly and easily as possible.
Deinde pone emplastrum ex farina sisami, uel molendini, cum albumine oui: et pone super ipsum astellam rotundam, si indiges illa: et stringe desuper strictura conueniente ad illud.	Then place a plaster made from sesame flour, or mill flour, with egg white; place a round splint over it if needed, and bind it with a bandage suitable for it.
Deinde considera omnes dispositiones quas narrauimus in reliquis fracturis: sicut apostema calidum, et similia illi: ita, ut opponas omni accidenti cum eo quod conuenit ei, donec sanetur.	Then consider all the instructions we have described for the other fractures, like the hot swelling and similar; thus, treat each occurrence with what is suitable for it, until it heals.
¶ De restauratione fracturae Cruris.	On the restoration of the fracture of the leg.
Caput XVII.	Chapter XVII.
Scias, quod Cruri sunt duo ossa, quorum unum est grossum, et nominatur nomine Cruris: et alterum subtile, et nominatur Focile.	Know that the leg has two bones, one of which is thick and called by the name of Cruris, and the other is thin and called Focile.
Et accidit utrisque ex speciebus fracturae quod accidit duobus ossibus brachii: et propter illud fit restauratio eius, sicut restauratio brachii aequaliter, et operatio est una.	And each of these bones can suffer fractures similar to those that happen to the two bones of the arm, and for that reason its restoration is carried out just as the restoration of the arm, and the operation is the same.
Quod si franguntur duo ossa simul, conuertitur Crus ad omnes partes.	But if both bones are broken together, the leg turns in all directions.
Et si frangitur os subtilius, conuertitur Crus ad anteriorem partem.	And if the thinner bone is broken, the leg turns toward the front.
Et si frangitur os grossius, inuenis illud inferius: et est ex eis quae non occultantur tibi.	And if the thicker bone is broken, you will find it below; and it is among those things that are not hidden from you.
Deinde administra extensionem, et aequationem, et ligationem astellarum secundum illud aequaliter.	Then apply extension, alignment, and binding of splints evenly according to it.
Verumtamen oportet, si fractura Cruris est fractura foeda, habens sethagie multa: ut sit extensio minor, et lenior alleuiatione tua.	However, if the fracture of the leg is a severe fracture, having much swelling, the extension should be less and gentler with your relief.
Et in Crure quidem est ex operatione quiddam super brachium: et est, ut quando tu aequas astellas, et comples omnem operationem tuam, tunc accipe duas fascias ex ligno pini, quod administratur in fabricatione solariorum quod ponitur sub tabulis: et fiunt ex spatulis palmae: et elige ex eis quibus est grossitudo parua, et neque sint ex minutis: et sit longitudo earum secundum longitudinem Cruris a genu usque inferius.	And indeed in the leg there is a part of the operation beyond the arm: and it is such that when you arrange the splints and complete all your work, then take two strips of pine wood, which is used in the making of sunshades placed under the planks, and they are made from palm leaf stems, and choose those with small thickness, and they should not be from the thinnest; and their length should match the length of the leg from the knee down.
Deinde inuolue super unamquamque pannum duabus inuolutionibus secundum longitudinem eius.	Then wrap each one in cloth with two wrappings according to its length.
Et pone unam partem Cruris a parte una, et alteram a parte altera: et sint ex genu usque inferius pedis.	And place one part of the leg on one side and the other on the opposite side, and they should extend from the knee to the lower part of the foot.
Deinde liga duas fascias in tribus locis ex duabus extremitatibus et medio.	Then bind the two strips at three places, at both ends and in the middle.
Cum enim strictura prohibetur, Crus declinat ad dextram et sinistram, et stat statione bona.	For when the bandage is prevented, the leg leans to the right and left and stays in a good position.
Et iam quidem administretur cunabulum ex ligno secundum longitudinem Cruris, et ponatur in eo Crus, ut conseruet ipsum a motu.	And already a wooden cradle should be prepared according to the length of the leg, and the leg is placed in it to keep it from moving.
Et plurimum quidem oportet, ut facias illud in fractura quando cum ea est uulnus proprie.	And most certainly it is necessary to do this in a fracture when there is indeed a wound with it.
Deinde considera Crus in omni die, et stude cum eo studio ultimo ab apostemate, aut inflatione, aut reliquis aliis.	Then observe the leg every day and diligently guard it from abscess, swelling, or any other conditions.
Si ergo accidit aliquid ex illis, tunc oppone ei cum eo quod oportet, usquequo sanetur, si Deus uoluerit.	If, then, anything of those should happen, then apply to it what is necessary until it heals, if God wills.
De fractura ossium Pedis, et Digitorum.	On the fracture of the bones of the foot and toes.
Caput XVIII.	Chapter XVIII.
Calcaneo quidem non accidit fractura penitus.	A fracture does not at all occur in the heel.
Ossibus uero Pedis accidit quandoque fractura, et Digitis parum etiam accidit fractura: et non accidit eis nisi attritio in pluribus dispositionibus.	But a fracture sometimes occurs in the bones of the foot, and a fracture happens in the toes only rarely, and they generally only suffer abrasion in most conditions.
Quod si accidit ossibus Pedis fractura, et uides illa ossa sublimari super se adinuicem, tunc ponat infirmus Pedem suum super terram praeparatam, sicut ambulans.	If a fracture occurs in the bones of the foot, and you see those bones lifting over each other, then the patient should place his foot on prepared ground, as if walking.
Deinde sta tu, et pone Pedem tuum super illud quod eleuatur ex illis ossibus: postea preme, uel calca eam: et aequa ipsam, donec redeat in locis suis.	Then stand, and place your foot over the elevated part of those bones; afterward, press down or tread upon it, and align it until it returns to its proper place.
Deinde pone emplastrum, et stuppam desuper: et pone sub uentre Pedis tabulam paruam, cui sint duo capita plana.	Then place a plaster and tow on top, and put a small board under the arch of the foot, with two flat ends.
Deinde stringe eam cum Pede strictura decenti, postquam tu inuoluis eam cum pannis, et reliquis quibus indiget.	Then bind it with a suitable bandage around the foot, after wrapping it with cloth and other things needed.
Cum ergo praetereunt ei tres dies, aut quatuor, tunc solue ligamentum.	After three or four days have passed, then release the bandage.
Nam tu inuenies ossa aequata, siue sint fracta, aut sint dislocata.	For you will find the bones aligned, whether fractured or dislocated.
Operatio ergo in eis est, sicut uides.	Thus, the procedure for them is as you see.
Si autem franguntur aliqui digiti, tunc restaura eos, et aequa ipsos secundum quod narraui tibi in restauratione digitorum manus.	If some toes are broken, then restore and align them as I have described to you for the restoration of the fingers of the hand.
Deinde pone digito tuo astellam secundum longitudinem digiti, et sit latior eo parumper: deinde pone sub Pede hanc tabulam quam narraui tibi, et stringe eam strictura decenti.	Then place a splint along the length of the toe, slightly wider than it, and then put under the foot the board I mentioned, and bind it with a suitable bandage.
Si uero quod frangitur ex ossibus est duo, aut tria, aut plus, tunc pone super omnem digitum astellam suae quantitatis, inuolutam in panno lini: et stringe tabulam in inferiori Pedis: et sit stans super unum capitum tabulae extra uentrem Pedis, ut teneat retentione bona.	If, however, two, three, or more bones are broken, then place a splint over each toe, of its respective size, wrapped in linen cloth, and bind the board to the bottom of the foot, standing on one end of the board outside the arch of the foot, so that it holds with good support.
Et conuenit tibi, ut non obliuiscaris, quin consideres omnia quae dixi tibi in reliquis fracturis ex accidentibus quae diximus: et oppone omni accidenti cum eo quod oportet.	And it is suitable for you not to forget but to consider everything I have told you in the other fractures regarding the injuries we have described, and apply what is appropriate for each incident.
¶ De fractura Vuluae mulieris, et ossis pectinis, et uirgae uiri.	On the fracture of the woman’s vulva, the pubic bone, and the man’s member.
Caput XIX.	Chapter XIX.
Quando frangitur Vulua mulieris, tunc fac eam sedere quadratam.	When a woman’s vulva is fractured, have her sit in a square position.
Deinde inclina eam ad partem dorsi ipsius parumper, et teneatur retro.	Then tilt her back slightly, and let her be held from behind.
Deinde impleat obstetrix Vuluam eius coto, donec impleat eam: et fiat in Vulua ex eo sicut sphera.	Then let the midwife fill her vulva with cotton until it is full, and let it become spherical within the vulva.
Deinde concutiatur mulier, et eleua dorsum eius paulatim.	Then shake the woman, and slowly lift her back.
Illud enim cotum egreditur, donec fiat ante portam Vuluae sicut sphera, et os fractum redit.	For the cotton will come out until it is at the entrance of the vulva like a sphere, and the broken bone will return.
Deinde pone puluillum super dorsum eius.	Then place a cushion under her back.
Quando ergo uult mingere, remoueat cotum cum facilitate, deinde mingat: et reduc ipsum secundum modum, quo intromisisti ipsum in primis: deinde redeat ad puluillum suum primum.	When she wishes to urinate, remove the cotton with ease, then let her urinate; then replace it as you first inserted it, and let her return to her original cushion.
Faciat illud septem diebus, aut circiter, os ipsum enim restauratur.	Let her do this for seven days, or about that, for the bone itself will be restored.
Et si uis, accipe uesicam ouis, et stringe super foramen eius cannulam arundinis, et intromitte uesicam totam in Vuluam eius.	And if you wish, take an eggshell bladder, and bind a reed tube over its opening, and insert the whole bladder into her vulva.
Deinde suffla in cannulam cum uirtute, donec infletur uesica intra Vuluam, fractura enim redit.	Then blow forcefully into the tube until the bladder inflates inside the vulva, for the fracture will return.
Deinde impleatur cum coto, et stet aliquot diebus, secundum quod narrauimus, donec sanetur.	Then fill it with cotton, and let it remain for a few days, as we have described, until it heals.
Et quando frangitur os Pectinis uiri, aut mulieris, tunc administra in restauratione eius, et ipsius aequatione illud quod narrauimus in osse Anchae.	And when the pubic bone of a man or woman is broken, then administer its restoration and alignment as we have described for the hip bone.
Et non occultatur rectum in his fracturis propinquis, quae parum contingunt, praecipue cui est quiddam studii, uel usus.	And it is not hidden that in these close fractures, which rarely occur, especially for someone with some study or practice.
Et intellige librum meum istum multum, quoniam fractura ipsa significat tibi in pluribus dispositionibus uiam rectam in restauratione sui, et strictura sui: intellige ergo.	And understand my book well, for the fracture itself shows you in many circumstances the proper way to its restoration and bandaging; therefore, understand.
In Virga autem uiri quando frangitur, accipe guttur anseris: et intromitte uirgam in ipso.	But when the man’s member is fractured, take the neck of a goose and insert the member into it.
Deinde pone super ipsum inuolutionem ex panno, et inuoluatur, et dimittatur tribus diebus, aut circiter eos, et sanabitur, si Deus uoluerit.	Then place a wrap made of cloth around it, wrap it, and let it rest for three days, or about that, and it will heal, if God wills.
