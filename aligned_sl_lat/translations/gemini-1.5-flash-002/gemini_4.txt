After I have completed for you, O my sons, this book, which is the last of medical science with its completion, and have reached its end through its expositions and its explanations, it seemed to me that I should complete it for you with this treatise, which is a part of the operation, that is, surgery with the hand.
Because manual operation has been neglected in our region and in our time, completely forgotten, until perhaps its science perished and its trace was cut off, and there remained of it only small descriptions in the books of the ancients, which hands have altered; and error and hesitation have befallen them, until their intentions have been obscured, and their force has been weakened.  It seemed to me that I should revive it with the arrangement of this treatise according to the way of exposition, explanation, and abbreviation; and that I should come with the forms of cautery instruments and other surgical instruments, when it is done by the addition of explanation and by hastening what is necessary.
The reason why a good manual surgeon is not found in our time is that the art of medicine is long, and its author must be exercised beforehand in the science of anatomy, as Galen has related, until he knows the supports of the limbs and their shapes, and their connections and separations, and has knowledge of the bones, and nerves, and muscles, and their number, and their courses, and of the pulsating and quiet veins, and the places of their exits.
And for that reason Hippocrates said that the name is much with the name, and little with the work, and especially in the art of the hand.
We indeed already said that in the introduction of this book.
Because he who is not knowledgeable about what we have said concerning anatomy cannot avoid falling into error, by which men are killed: just as I have seen many of those who are formed in this science, and boast of it without knowledge and without experience.
That is, because I saw a foolish physician incise a scrofulous abscess in a woman's neck, and he cut some arteries in the neck: wherefore the woman's blood flowed until she fell dead in his hands.
And I saw another physician who presumed to extract a stone from a man who was advanced in age, and the stone was large.
Therefore he reached it and extracted it with a piece of the bladder.

And indeed I had already been called to extract it: and I saw from the size of the stone, and the condition of the patient, that he would die.

Part I.
Concerning the benefit and harm of cautery: and to which complexions it is suitable, and at what time: and if with iron or gold.
Chapter I.
But before I recall the operation with it, it is necessary that I speak of the quality of its benefits and harms: and in what complexion it should be exhibited.
Therefore I say that the discussion of the quality of the benefit of cautery, and its harm, is a lengthy and subtle discussion, a science, and a hidden secret.
Many wise men have also spoken on it, and disagreed: and indeed a little from their discussions suffices me, fearing prolixity.
Therefore I say that cautery universally benefits every complexion which is with matter, and without matter: except for two complexions, which are the hot complexion without matter, and the dry complexion without matter.
However, in the hot and dry complexion with matter they disagreed.
For some have said that cautery is beneficial in it.
And others said the opposite of that, namely that cautery is not suitable in the illness which is from heat and dryness: because the nature of fire is heat: and it is unsuitable that a hot and dry illness be treated with something hot and dry.
And he who says the contrary said: that benefit is achieved through cautery in every hot and dry illness which occurs in human bodies: because when you compare a human body and its humors to the complexion of fire, you find the human body cold.
And I indeed say according to his statement: because experiment has already revealed that to me several times.
However, it is not fitting that one undertake this unless one is already proven and experienced in the final chapter of the practice of cautery, and has known the diversity of human complexions, and the disposition of illnesses in themselves, and their causes, and their accidents, and their duration.
From the other complexions, however, there is no fear for you; and especially in cold and moist illnesses. For indeed all physicians have agreed on them: and they did not disagree on the benefit with cautery in them.
And know, sons, that from the secret of treatment by cautery with fire, and its goodness over cautery with burning medicine, is that fire is simple, whose action does not reach except to the member which is cauterized: and it does not harm another member joined with it, except with small harm.
The action of burning medicines, however, reaches that which is distant from the members: and perhaps it causes an illness difficult to heal in the member, and perhaps it kills.
Fire, however, because of its subtlety and the goodness of its substance, does not do that, unless it overflows.
Now indeed that has become known to us through experiment, because of the length of service and study with the art, and standing upon the truth of things: and because of that I excuse myself from lengthy speech.
And know, sons, that they disagree on the time in which it is fitting to perform cauterization, and they put the better time in the spring.
And I say that cauterization is suitable at all times: because the harm happening because of time is submerged in the benefit which is drawn with cauterization.
And especially if cauterization is for pains from necessary, strong, urgent pains, which do not tolerate slowness: because it is feared from them that a greater harm may happen than the small harm entering because of time.
And let not that fall into your minds, O sons, which only the common people and foolish physicians fear, namely that the illness which is healed by cauterization does not always return: and they posit that as a consequence, and the matter is not as they think: because cauterization is nothing but a medicine which alters the complexion and dries the humors which are the causes why pains happen.
Nevertheless cauterization surpasses medicine in the speed of its healthfulness and the strength of its operation and the vehemence of its mastery: and it is possible that the illness may return at some time according to the complexion of the infirm, and the location of his illness, and its strength: and what is prepared in his body from the aggregation of superfluities in it: and what arrives at it in its acquisition from foods and similar causes, unless it is an illness in which cauterization is administered. An illness subtle in a limb with few superfluities and humors, such as cauterization of a tooth for pain, and things similar to it, it is possible that that pain may not return in it.
But that happens in few cases.
But truly the speech of the common people again, that cauterization is the last resort of medicine, is correct speech: not to that which they intend in it: since they believe that there is no cure which can be compared with medicine, nor with anything else after the application of cauterization.
But the matter is contrary to that, and the intention that cauterization is the last resort of medicine is not so, except that when we administer methods of cure in some illnesses, and those medicines do not help: then we administer cauterization at the end of the matter, and it helps: then from that it falls out that cauterization is the last resort of medicine, not according to the intention which the common people and many foolish physicians understand.
The ancients, however, said that cauterization with gold is better than cauterization with iron: and they did not say that, except because of the evenness of gold and the excellence of its substance: and they said that the site of cauterization does not produce pus. That is not true absolutely: because I have now proved it, and found that it does not do that in some bodies without others.
And cauterization with it is good, and better than with iron, as they said.
However, when you cauterize with fire from gold, it is not clear to you when it is heated to the degree you wish, because of the redness of the gold: and when it is quickly cooled, and if you add more heating to it, it melts in the fire and turns into sheets.
Therefore the craftsman falls into difficulty with it: and because of that, cauterization with iron is quicker and more convenient for us, because of its straightforwardness for the operation.
And I indeed have now arranged this Chapter on Cauterization according to chapters: and I have arranged them from head to foot: so that they may ease the one seeking what he wishes from it. Concerning the method of cauterizing the head at the coronal suture.

Of these, sixteen are in the head: two pulsating veins, which are behind the ears, called the two Herbae; and two arteries, which are apparent in the two temples; and two veins which are in the two lachrymal ducts of the two eyes, which are called Aspicientes; and a prepared vein in the middle of the forehead; and a vein which is at the tip of the nose.
And two Guidegi, which are in the neck.
And two veins which are in the upper lip of the mouth.
And two veins which are in the lower lip.
And these are the veins which are called Algecherich.
And two veins which are under the tongue.
The veins, however, which are bled in the arms, are five veins in each arm.
One of them is the Cephalic, and it is from the outer side: and the common people call it the vein of the head.
And the dark one.
And there is the Median vein, and it is composed of a branch of the Basilic, and a branch of the Cephalic: and the common people call it the vein of the body.
And the Basilic, and it is situated on the inner side: and it is also called Titillicaris.
And the common people call it the vein of the belly.
And the rope of the arm, and it is situated above the forearm: and it is that in which bloodletting is performed, and it is that which appears above the thumb with clear application.
And the Salvatella: and it is a vein which is between the ring finger and the little finger: and it has two branches.
And in the leg and in the foot there are three veins.
One of them is that which is under the curve of the thumb from the outer side.
And the second is the Saphena: and its place is near the heel from the inner side.
And the vein Nesse: and its place is near the heel from the outer side.
And in the other leg there are three veins similar to those.
Concerning the phlebotomy of two veins behind the ears.
Phlebotomy of the two veins which are behind the ears, however, is a benefit for old catarrhs, and hemicrania, and sahafati, and old bad head ulcers.
And the quality of bleeding it is according to what I relate.
And it is so that the head of the sick person is shaved: and his occiput in the place of the two veins is rubbed with a rough cloth with a good rubbing.
Then let the sick person bind his neck with a band, until the two veins appear.
And their locations are behind the ears in two depressed places of the head.
Therefore seek them with your finger, and where you feel their pulse under your finger, there mark with cautery.
Then take a lancet phlebotome, and it is that which is called Incidens.
Then insert it under the vein in the skin, until the phlebotome reaches the mouth.
Then raise your hand with the vein and skin upwards, and incise the vein with the skin with an incisive incision.
And let the length of the incision be the size of two joined fingers, or thereabouts: and let out as much blood as you wish.
Then bind it with lint, and leave it until it heals.
And already the incision and cauterization of them preceded in the beginning of the book.
Concerning phlebotomy of two arteries in times.
Truly, the two arteries which are in the two times of phlebotomy are a help for old hemicrania, and bad toothache, and constant ophthalmia, and the flow of sharp effused superfluities to the eyes. And the quality of phlebotomizing them is according to what I relate to you.
Let the sick person strongly constrict his neck until two veins appear manifestly to the sense of sight: and let their pulse be demonstrated under your finger.
Then mark them with a cautery.
Then lift the skin above the vein upwards with your index finger, and insert the cutting phlebotomist underneath, and lift the vein upwards.
And incise it, as you did in the other two veins: and make the blood flow according to the amount of your need. Then loosen the constriction of the sick person, and place your finger on the vein for one hour.
Afterwards place a compress and pledgets on it, and bind it tightly above with a strong bandage: and leave it until it heals.
And now the recollection of them, and their incision, and their extraction has preceded in the beginning of the book.
Concerning the phlebotomy of the vein of the forehead after the phlebotomy of the Cephalic vein.
The benefit, however, of phlebotomy of the vein of the forehead is, after phlebotomy of the Cephalic vein, for old facial ailments: and for hemicrania, and redness: and foul ulcers.
And the manner of its phlebotomy is, according to what I relate to you.
The infirm person constricts his neck with a fillet until the vein appears.
Then take the instrument which is called a Fossorium.
Whose form is this.
Place the prominent point which is on the head of the Fossorium upon the vein itself, and strike upon it with a comb, or with something else in its manner: and let the blood flow according to the quantity which you wish.
Then loosen the perforation of the infirm, and bind it until it heals.
And sometimes it is opened with a broad phlebotome.
However, it is not necessary that the phlebotome be of a sharp extremity, like the other phlebotomes, but rather of a broad extremity.
And phlebotomize it according to the transverse; because the bone is near; and perhaps in it the phlebotome will be broken when it is thin.
Concerning the phlebotomy of the veins in the two tear ducts.
Moreover, the help of the two veins which are in the two tear ducts of the two eyes, is for the eye from scab and redness; and sebil in the eye, and sicknesses of the face.
And the manner of phlebotomizing them is that the infirm person should bind his neck with a fillet.
Then phlebotomize them; and you be standing over his head.
And let the phlebotomy be made across, a little lengthwise, with a small, somewhat broad phlebotome, for there is no flesh in the place.
For if the phlebotome is of a slender point, perhaps it will break.
Then let blood flow as much as is necessary for you.
And place a pledget on them, and bind them for one night, and unbind them.
Concerning the phlebotomy of the veins of the nose.
Phlebotomy of the veins of the nose, however, is beneficial for acute fever, and violent headache, and facial illnesses, such as hemicrania, and redness which occurs in the face: especially when it is longstanding.
And the manner of its phlebotomy is that the sick person should constrict his neck.
Then hold his nose with your left hand, and take a slender, long phlebotomy instrument; and insert it in the middle of his lip, between that which divides the nose according to its straightness.
Because the vein does not appear to the sense there, and because the blood begins to flow immediately.
And it is necessary that you should move your hand with the phlebotomy instrument a little; and you should make the blood flow according to your need.
Then bind it with one knot.
For it is restored quickly.
Concerning the phlebotomy of the veins of the Guidegi.
Indeed, the phlebotomy of two Guidegi veins helps with shortness of breath, and the beginning of leprosy, and melancholic illnesses, and impetigo and bad ulcers, and those that corrode, that is, herpetic esthiomenes.
And the manner of their phlebotomy is that the infirm one binds himself under both [veins] on his neck with a ligature; and the surgeon stands over the head of the infirm one, and the infirm one sits on a seat.
Then the phlebotomist opens the vein to a length somewhat ample for the phlebotomy.
Then draw out a moderate quantity of blood, or according to what you see is necessary for it.
Then do likewise with the other vein.
Afterwards, loosen the ligature, and bind the two veins with a moderate constriction, so that the infirm one is not strangled.
And leave it until morning.
For the wound heals.
Concerning the phlebotomy of veins, Algeberich.
Moreover, Algeberich's phlebotomy is helpful because it contributes to canker in the mouth and corruption of the gums, and bad ulcers, and fissures of the lips, and bad ulcers also which are in the nose and in its circumference.
And the manner of their phlebotomy is that you make the infirm one sit before you and bind his neck with a band.
Then roll back his lip and look at the two veins: of which one is on the right of the lip and the second on the left of it; and the indication of both is by the blackness of both.
And this is true: because in their circumference are small, black veins.
Therefore incise each with an incisive incision. But if there is doubt upon you, and you do not know which of them it is, then attend to the incision of the larger of them, and the more manifest of them.
And similarly do with the two veins which are in the upper lip: and indeed custom runs mostly with it: and incise the two veins which are in the lower lip.
Concerning phlebotomy of the vein under the tongue after phlebotomy of the cephalic vein.
Truly, the help of phlebotomy of the two veins which are under the tongue is that phlebotomy of the cephalic vein is for obstructions which are in the throat, and sickness of the uvula, and sicknesses of the mouth.
And the manner of phlebotomizing them is that the infirm person should sit between your hands before the sun: and raise his tongue, and look under the tongue on one side of it for a vein, and on the other side of it for a vein: and the color of each inclines towards blackness.
Therefore phlebotomize both: and be careful lest you wound your hand in phlebotomizing them.
For under them are arteries, and perhaps a flow of blood from those arteries will happen.
Concerning the three veins in the elbow.
Now the three veins which are phlebotomized in the elbow, are those in whose phlebotomy custom runs in all men.
And their phlebotomy is done according to two methods.
Either by pricking with a broad myrtle phlebotome: or an olive-shaped one to a sloping thinness: or by cutting with a knife-like phlebotome, and that is Alnesil.
And this is its form.
Thus Alnesil is that which is suitable for section.
And of them are broad species, and thin ones according to the width of the veins and their narrowness. The form of the myrtle phlebotome is broad, as you see.
And it is suitable for opening concave, full, prominent, apparent, thick veins, and those which contain thick, turbid blood.
And this phlebotome is of less width, and of a more slender point.
And it is suitable for the phlebotomy of thin veins, which contain thin, yellow blood.
Concerning the phlebotomy of the basilica.
Now the basilica, which is one of these three veins, is an aid to phlebotomy. Because it draws blood from illnesses which are under the throat and neck, from that which follows the chest, and the belly: and it befits the phlebotomist, when he phlebotomizes it, that he be cautious, and be on guard against it, for under it is an artery.
Therefore if he errs, and adds to the puncture of the phlebotomy, he will cut that artery, and a flow of blood will result.
Therefore it is necessary that he not phlebotomize it with a pointed phlebotomy instrument; rather, let it be cut with an Alnesil.
But if the basilica does not appear in manifest appearance, then it is necessary that you abandon it, and change to another, or seek some branch of it: or phlebotomize in its place the vein of the arm.
For it is manifest, and cut it with the Alnesil phlebotomy instrument, as we said.
But if you wish the phlebotomy of the same [vein], then it is necessary before constriction of the arm, that you touch the place, until you know the place of the pulse.
Then mark the place with a cautery, afterward the arm, and cut the vein with a concave incision with the Alnesil phlebotomy instrument, as we said.
And beware lest the incision fall through the space from the place of the artery.
And when you see, when you tighten the ligature, swelling in the place which you marked with the cautery, then that swelling is the swelling of the artery: therefore avoid it.
But if you see the blood at the phlebotomy leaping up, like the urine of a boy, and it is thin blood, red: then know that it is from the blood of the artery.
Then therefore hasten, and put your finger on it for a long space, then remove your finger.
If therefore the blood is cut off (for it is often cut off) then bind the arm, and release it: and let the patient beware of its movement: and let him be in fear of it.
And let him not move it for some days, until it is healed.
But if the blood is not cut off, and it overcomes you: and you do not have medicine present at that moment, then cut the artery, if it appears to you.
For its extremities are contracted, and the blood is cut off.
Or take the bark of a pistachio, and split it; take one half, and bind it over the place of the constriction of the vein decently, with a bandage and pledgets until the next day.
Then if the blood is cut off, it is good.
And if not: then treat it with that whose mention preceded from the place of powders cutting off the flow of blood: and the cutting off of its blood is not difficult in most cases, because of the smallness of the wound, and the possibility of a bandage from the arm.
Concerning the phlebotomy of the median basilic vein.
Moreover, the benefit of phlebotomy of the median basilic vein is that it draws blood from the upper part of the head and the lower part of the body; therefore, because it is composed of a branch of the basilic and a branch of the cephalic, as we have said.
And it is fitting for the phlebotomist that he be careful about its phlebotomy, for under it is a nerve.
If therefore he adds to the puncture of the phlebotomy, and finds a nerve, numbness occurs in it, and its healing becomes difficult, and perhaps it is not completely healed.
And this nerve often appears to the sense.
But if it is hidden in some men, and is thin, and is not shown: then it is necessary that you place your phlebotomy, by which you perform the phlebotomy, with an incision with an Alnesil, and avoid the nerve with your strength.
But if the vein is between two nerves, then split the vein lengthwise.
Concerning the Cephalic vein.
Moreover, phlebotomy of the Cephalic vein is helpful, because it draws blood from the head, and contributes to the illnesses of the eyes.
And it is necessary in this vein especially, if you wish, that you phlebotomize it by puncturing with an olive-shaped phlebotomy instrument, or with a myrtle-shaped phlebotomy instrument: because it is the safest of all veins, because there is no artery beneath it, nor nerve. Nevertheless it is fitting for you in phlebotomy, that you avoid with the phlebotomy instrument only the head of the humerus, and seek a softer place: and it does not harm it, if it is not found with the first incision, that it is repeated upon it with the phlebotomy instrument several times. Nevertheless perhaps it becomes inflamed in some men when it is not phlebotomized in the first incision, but that inflammation does not harm it in any way.
Concerning the usefulness of phlebotomy, and the precautions to be observed in it.
Moreover, the quality of phlebotomy, and its accidental occurrences, in whose rectification it is necessary for you to think.
First therefore of that it is necessary that you know, that phlebotomy is not administered except in the preservation of health, and its perseverance, and caution from the arrival of illnesses.
And that phlebotomy be done in one of the three veins which are in the elbow, namely the Cephalic, and the Median, and the Basilic.
And that phlebotomy be done at the beginning of Spring on an empty stomach, in spring and summer: and in autumn and winter after a meal, when signs of repletion appear.
And let phlebotomy be done on the first day, or on the third, after three hours have passed from the day.
Phlebotomy however which is administered in illnesses, does not have a determined hour: but when suitability and necessity provoke it, at night, or by day, and at every hour, and at every time.
And it is not fitting that boys be bled until fourteen years come upon them.
Nor let a pregnant woman be bled, except from the fourth month to the seventh.
Nor let old men be bled, who have already passed sixty years.
Therefore when anyone wishes to be bled for whatever reason: it is necessary that he cleanse his bowels before phlebotomy with a gentle clyster, if much retained stool is in them: lest the veins attract during phlebotomy from the bowels putrid superfluities harmful to the principal members.
Nor let a patient suffering nausea, that is, a sickening fullness, be bled; nor one suffering from a hangover, nor one recovering from drunkenness, until that is removed from them.
Nor one ill with dropsy.
And let phlebotomy be avoided again after a colic attack, and vomiting, and diarrhoea, and excessive intercourse, and labor, and exercise, and wakefulness, and fasting, and everything that weakens the bodily or animal strength.
Then consider the subtlety of the humors beforehand, if the blood is thick with foods and drinks, and medicines: if that is possible for him.
Then let him enter a bath, if nothing forbids it: or let him engage in some exercise, so that the blood may be thinned.
And place his phlebotomy in the chest, around the third hour of the day, as we have said: and strive that his chest be evacuated on that day of all bad animal accidents: such as anguish, and anger, and fear: and of all bodily accidents, such as labor, and pain, and excessive intercourse, and the like.
And let him have present in his seat things with whose use the custom of men runs, from aromatics and fragrance, and dancing and similar things, in the quantity possible.
Then let the phlebotomist sit on a cushion which is higher than the cushion of the phlebotomized; afterwards let him pull out his arm, and let the phlebotomist rub it with his hand twice, or thrice.
Then let him tighten the ligature with a strap, and wrap it twice.
And let the constriction be moderate, because when the constriction is unequal, or by excess in the constriction, then it prevents the flow of blood; or in softening, then also it prevents the flow of blood.
Then after the constriction it is necessary that the phlebotomized rub his hands together until the veins swell and are apparent to the sense.
Then let the phlebotomist wipe the phlebotomized with a little old oil, properly.
Then let him place the index finger of his left hand on the vein itself below the place where he wishes to phlebotomize, a little: so that the vein does not slip away, and the incision is missed.
Because among the veins there is one which you find like a fleeing cord during phlebotomy: and among them there is one which is full of wind: and when you place the phlebotomized over it, it contracts under the phlebotomist, and the phlebotomist is deceived, and does not open the vein: and if he opens it, it is constricted: therefore it is necessary that the phlebotomist be firm, and be careful in all these things.
Then let him put down the phlebotomized.
And if not, then repeat it another time a little below that place, or above it quickly: if the place is not festering.
But if it festers, or the patient is frightened, then leave him for a day or two days: and do not tighten the ligature.
For perhaps it will draw a hot abscess.
Nor should he enter a bath.
Then let him repeat the phlebotomy, if he wishes.
But if the opening is small, or the blood flows thinly: and you fear that the quantity of blood you wish will not come out: then repeat the phlebotomy in the same hole according to the straightness, and add a little to the operation.
And do this quickly, before the place becomes abscessed, for an abscessed place is difficult in many men with a small operation.
Therefore if you see it already abscessed, then do not repeat it at all, for it will excuse nothing for you. And put something of the dregs of oil on it: because it alleviates the flow of blood.
And it is better in this place than oil itself, and than other oils.
And similarly administer the dregs of oil in all your phlebotomy, which phlebotomizes veins against the prohibition of blood flow: and they do this with theriac alfarach, and sagzenea, when one of them is placed upon the place.
For blood is thinned and resolved when it is thick.
But if a large abscess happens at the place of phlebotomy, for it happens very often, especially to him who is not phlebotomized except at that time, or the opening of the vein is small, then hasten and put on the place a sponge soaked in water and salt, warmed a little: and bind it for an hour: because it is dissolved.
And it is necessary that you do this after the exit of blood from the vein itself, with its completion, or from another vein.
And very often indeed abscesses and swellings happen near the phlebotomy of the basilic vein.
Therefore put your hand upon it, then if you find softness in your compression upon it, then that swelling is bad.
Beware therefore, that you put upon it something of that which we have said.
For perhaps blood of the artery will flow from it.
But make a plaster for it with that in which there is astringency, so that the place may be hardened.
Then treat it with the remaining treatment, until it is healed.
And it is necessary that you extract from every man from the blood according to the quantity of his strength.
And that which appears from the color prevailing over the blood.
For if the blood is black, then let it go out until it reddens: and similarly if you see it thick: then let it out until it is thinned: and similarly if it is sharp, until its sharpness is removed.
And it is necessary for him who is full, strong, and needs bloodletting once, that the vein phlebotomy be widened, and the phlebotomy be wide: and in him who is weak, it should be the opposite of that.
And it is necessary that he extract it in turns, and that the opening be narrow: and better that which is administered in vein phlebotomy is that it be a transverse section, not a puncture.
And this method of phlebotomy is safe from the flow of blood, and from the cutting of a nerve: and it is more laudable, and safer than phlebotomy by width and length.
And for whom it is accustomed to arrive at syncope during phlebotomy, it is necessary that he eat before phlebotomy a little bread soaked in pomegranate juice: or sugarcane, if it is heated: and extract from his blood in three times, or four.
And if he is of a cold complexion, then let him take before phlebotomy bread soaked in quince syrup, or in syrup of honey spiced with spices, or in fragrant spiced syrup.
But if syncope happens during phlebotomy, and its cause is the exit of much blood: then it is necessary that he take in drink meat water, and a subtle fragrant syrup: and he use aromatherapy with agarwood, and let his chest be anointed with it: and let him use the other things which we said in the division in the chapter on syncope from evacuation.
But he who wishes to evacuate his arm, and to release his blood secondly: it is necessary for him whose phlebotomy is for much evacuation, and whose strength is weak, that he release blood gradually according to the quantity of strength in continuous days.
But he who wishes to evacuate his arm, and to release his blood secondly, and his body is strong: then let him do it after seven hours, or nine, from his first phlebotomy.
But he who wishes to draw blood from his body to the opposite of the part to which it inclines: it is necessary that he be evacuated on the second day, or on the third.
But in whose body there is much blood already heated, and sharpened, and causes fever to happen: it is necessary that blood go out from it at one time, and a large quantity be drawn from it: and the opening be widened until syncope happens: after you have observed all the conditions of phlebotomy: and that you put your hand upon his pulse during the flow of blood, so that death does not happen instead of syncope.
For often that happens when the phlebotomist is ignorant, and negligence falls.
And it is not necessary, when you wish to release the arm, and release blood a second time, when the opening of the vein is already closed, and the exit of blood becomes difficult, that it be pressed upon forcefully, and bent and extended quickly.
For that is very bad, indeed leave it until it is bled a second time.
Or remove with the sharpness of the phlebotomy instrument that which is coagulated from the blood in the opening of the vein, and put upon it something of salt dissolved in water.
And place upon it something of theriac alfarob or sagapenum, and apply it with gentle compression, until the blood flows out.
But if the vein is already inflamed, then leave it, and do not cover it, until the inflammation subsides.
But if necessity requires bleeding a second time, and there is no excuse, then either phlebotomize it above that place, or in another vein in the other arm.

Concerning cupping, and the manner of its use.
Chapter XCVIII.
Cups are made from horns, and from bronze, and from glass.
And cupping is done in two ways.
With scarification and the extraction of blood: and the other is cupping without scarification. And this cupping which is without scarification, is according to two methods.
Or it is with fire.
Or it is without fire.
And there are fourteen places on the body where cuppings which are administered with scarification and bloodletting are applied.
One of which is the cupping of Alnocrati, and it is at the back of the head: and Alkehel, and it is in the middle of the neck: and the cuppings of Alhagdahim, and they are the two sides of the neck on both sides together: and the cuppings of the chin, and it is under the jaw in the lower part of the mouth: and the cuppings of the two shoulder blades: and the cuppings of Alachosos above the end of the spine: and the cuppings of the two elbows, and they are the middle of the two arms: and the cuppings of the two legs: and the cuppings of the two ankles.
And cupping indeed does not draw blood from you from the small veins scattered in the flesh, and because of that, strength does not fall from them, as is the case with phlebotomy.
Nor is it necessary that cupping, whether with fire or without fire, be administered in any illnesses which occur from repletion, until the whole body is evacuated.
But if necessity prompts cupping because of illness or habit, we administer it at any time at the beginning of the month, at the end of it, and in the middle of it, and at whatever time it may be.
That is because there are men in whom blood multiplies until it needs extraction by cupping; they find heaviness and headache in their head.
And some of them find fullness and redness in their face, head, and neck.
And some of them find itching in their face and forehead, and darkness and dullness in their eyes.
And some of them scratch the place of their cupping. And some of them have increased laughter.
And some of them find the taste of blood in their mouth; or the tongue is abscessed, and they spit blood.
And some of them have increased sleep.
And some of them see in their sleep blood and redness, and slain men, and wounds, and things similar to them. Therefore, when we see something of these things, and especially if it is in the middle third of the month, we then order venesection to be performed, after two or three hours of the day have passed. The benefit of cupping the nape of the neck is that it helps with heaviness in the head and that which flows down to the eyes.
