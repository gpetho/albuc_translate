and if anything comes at the end of the healing, it is necessary to administer softening and soothing medicines, with the splint that we have mentioned above; and sometimes one administers a piece of lead, and sometimes the appearance comes in the posterior spine of the back, and one thinks that it is a dislocation; and it is the bone which appears, wherefore one should not be presumptuous: by chance it would bring death.
Know that at the joints of the hip and at the joints of the shoulder, only dislocations occur, not as in other joints;
and the hip joint is dislocated according to four parts: inwards and outwards, and forwards and backwards;
and more inwards than elsewhere, and slightly dislocated forwards and backwards.
And the recognition of its inward dislocation is that when one joins the diseased leg with the healthy one, it is longer, and the diseased knee appears more prominent than the healthy one, and the patient cannot bend his knee to the fire; and the place which is nearby is swollen with a hot abscess, because the head of the thigh comes there.
And the sign of that which comes outwards, is that these symptoms are contrary to those above.
And the signs of that which happens in front, are that he extends his leg well according to its length; however, he does not bend his knee without pain, and if one wants to make it go, it cannot go forward, and he retains his urine, and the groin swells, and when he walks, he steps on his heel.
And the signs of him to whom it happens behind, are that he cannot extend his knee, nor bend it, and that leg is more twisted than the other, and the groin is softened, and the head of the thigh is in the place under the ribs.
The treatment of this type of dislocation is that you observe if the dislocation is old, and if it remains according to its disposition, then there is no cure; and it is not necessary that there be presumption.
And in one in whom the dislocation is fresh, and this is of the four types of dislocation, hasten that you turn the joint, and extend it inwards and outwards, and move it to the right and left; by chance it will return, and there is no need for other treatment.
And if it does not return in this manner, then you must prepare a strong assistant, and extend the leg strongly with two hands, or with a linen cloth tied above the knee; and another assistant extends his hand above in such a way that he puts his hands under his armpits, then binds him with a soft cloth above the thigh; and another holds the head of the linen;
and they extend upwards and downwards all at once, until the patient is lifted from the ground at once, and remains hanging.
This method of extension is common to the four similar cases, and if the dislocation has returned, as we have said, it is well.
If not, then it is necessary to apply treatment to its likeness.
When the dislocation is inward, you must place the patient on his healthy side, then place the linen on the root of the thigh, and the place which is below the groin, then extend the linen upwards towards the groin, and towards the hip joint;
then let another assistant take his arms, and embrace the large part of the affected thigh, and extend it outwards strongly; then it will return to its place immediately;
and this type of dislocation is easier than others.
And if you cannot set it in the manner we have described, you must bind the patient's two feet together with a strong, soft linen, above the two heels and above the two knees, and let the affected leg be extended two fingers more than the other;
then let it be hung from the top of a beam, and let it be two arm-lengths above the ground;
then instruct an assistant to embrace the head of the thigh, and let another assistant hang with the patient, who firmly embraces the thigh, for the joint returns immediately and quickly.
And when the dislocation is reduced, let the patient lie down, as we have said for someone with gibbosity, and tie a linen bandage tightly around the affected leg and also the foot;
then place two firebrands, one at the head and another at the feet, then place a firebrand between the patient's thighs in the middle of the groin; and have moistened cloths placed around so that it does not burn the patient, then let each assistant extend towards him, and the physician should equally set the dislocation with his hand.
If it returns, it is good.
If not, place it on a table, and press it as we have said in the case of gibbosity, and let the patient lie on the healthy side.
And when the dislocation is in front, it is necessary to extend the affected leg quickly, and as I have said; and let the physician place his effort on the affected angle, then press with the other hand, and extend it downwards towards the knee.
And if the dislocation is behind, it is not necessary to extend the affected limb downwards, nor to raise it from the ground, but it should be as if the hip were dislocated behind, as we have said concerning lying on the two hips; and let him be on his face, and the limb extended as we have said above, and one must press on the joint at the table; and it will be right when the patient extends his legs, and they are even; then join the thighs, and apply the plaster, and bind it with a bandage, so that the hip does not move; and let it rest for three or four days, then unbind it, and apply the plaster again;
and if you see that they are even, then bind it, as before, and leave it for three days;
then unbind it, and have him walk gently, until the limb is strengthened.
Of the dislocation of the knee
The knee is dislocated in three ways: that is to say, inwards and outwards, and backwards;
and it does not dislocate forwards.
And the sign is that you instruct the patient to bend his leg to his thigh, and if it does not bend to his thigh, know that it is dislocated;
and the restoration of all is that you make the patient sit upright, and extend his leg if he can, and put your back against his face, and put his foot on your feet;
then take his knee in your palms, and pull it between your fingers, then bend the two sides of the knee strongly; and another assistant extends his foot, until the knee returns to its place, and the sign is that he bends his leg easily to his thigh;
and put the plaster on, and bind the leg to the thigh, and bind them together tightly for three or four days, then unbind it;
and let the patient not work or walk, until the limb is strengthened.
And if the limb is not straight, administer strong extension, as we have said in the treatment of the hip, until it is healed.
of the dislocation of the heel
The heel is sometimes moved, and sometimes completely dislocated, both inwards and outwards.
And the sign is that the ankle is swollen, and tense towards the part where it is dislocated:
The reduction is easy, and one should extend it gently with one's hands when it is only moved.
And when it is completely dislocated, you must make the patient sit upright, and hold him firmly by the leg, above the ankle;
then pull the foot towards you with your right hand, and then with the left twice, then extend it upwards, and gently place the foot upwards;
and do this twice, as we have said, by pushing and pulling the foot;
If it returns, it is well;
If not, then begin the process again, for it returns;
and if it cannot return thus, then have the patient lie on his back on the ground, and firmly stick a stake in the ground between his thighs, and tie cloths around it, so that it does not injure the patient;
then let the assistant hold the thigh, and another assistant extend the foot with his hands or with a rope, which he ties above the ankle; then let each assistant extend equally, and the stake should always be between the patient's thighs to restrain the patient when he is extended;
then let the physician set the dislocation with his hands, and another assistant hold the healthy leg underneath, and the dislocation will return quickly.
And when it is in place, apply the plaster and lint, and bind it firmly, and tie the foot to the leg, and guard the nerves that are on the ankle behind, that the binding not be too tight;
then leave it two or three days, and if the binding loosens, tighten it;
then unbind it on the third or fourth day, and forbid the patient from walking for four days; and if he walks before that time, it is not certain that it will not dislocate again.
And if hot abscesses occur there, you must soothe them, as we have said elsewhere, until they are healed.
of the fingers When the fingers are dislocated, they must be gently extended without force, and that is an easy thing.
And if the dislocation is in any of the bones of the back of the foot, you must have the patient sit on a flat surface or on a completely straight table, and place your foot on the exposed part of the joints, then press firmly with your foot until it returns;
and when it is even, and nothing appears there, place a two-legged stool under the sole of the foot, then bind it tightly, and leave it for three days, then unbind it; and make him rest until he is healed.
of wound and dislocation together
When a wound occurs with the dislocation, and one wants to restore it, many times it happens that the patient dies:
therefore do not be presumptuous in the treatment, if you are not wise in the art and have not practiced it for a long time, and if you are not patient and merciful and slow, not rash;
and use at the beginning soothing things for a hot abscess, and leave the patient in God's hands, if you see no hope of healing; and when you see hope, then immediately restore the limb to its place, before the hot abscesses come there;
and when the limb is in place, administer things soothing to a hot abscess, and treat the wound, as is necessary, with drying ointments;
and if with the dislocation there is a fracture, and small bones separated, then remove them, and do what we have said in simple illnesses;
and guard yourself from entering the path of fear, according to what I have said, for it is worth more to you according to your Creator, and for the salvation of your soul and your body.
