And that which comes from a fall or from blows or similar things, the ancients prescribed methods of cure with great words, where there is not much profit in all;
but I have shortened it according to my intention, and have formed instruments in diverse ways.
I say that in the gibbosity that comes to the breast in front, there is no device or health;
and also that which comes straight on two sides, and one cannot cure except that which comes properly to the back, as I say;
and it is that the patient be stretched on his face in a level place, and have a bolster underneath him, that he not be injured;
then fix one firebrand firmly to the head in the ground, and another to the feet, and tie one rope across the armpits of the patient, and another at the hips, and at the knees, and at the ankles;
and two assistants hold the ropes, one at the head and the other at the feet, between the two firebrands;
and they should be well secured, the firebrands as we have said, and the physician place his palms firmly on the spine until it returns.
And if it does not return by this treatment, then take a board three arms long, and place it under the wall, and let the patient be on his side, and place the end of the board under the wall, then place the middle of the board on the spine; and let the physician place his feet on the other end of the board, and press firmly, until it returns to its place;
and if you wish, do it with a lathe that is hand-turned. And it is that you place two firebrands on the head of the patient at a distance of one arm's length, and towards the feet two others, and there should be between each firebrand, above and below, a hand's breadth of space in length;
and let there be in each firebrand one hole, where the lathe runs, and let the firebrands be firmly in the ground, so that they do not move;
and between each two firebrands let there be one turning firebrand, on which the cords turn, and let there be on the two turning ones two small firebrands of a hand's breadth in length, to turn the lathe;
and then tie the cord which is through the armpits to the lathe towards the head, and the other cord towards the feet to the other lathe, and each of the assistants turns his lathe with all the cord, and the master places the spine evenly as we have said.
And when it is even, then apply a drying plaster with egg white, and then the tow, and then the splint of a tablet three fingers long, which should cover the area of the gibbosity and the healthy part of the spine, then bind it with a cord, as we have said, and let the patient use a light diet, until he is healed;
and if anything comes at the end of the healing, then it is necessary to administer softening and soothing medicines, with the splint that we have mentioned above; and sometimes one administers a piece of lead, and sometimes the appearance comes in the posterior spine of the back, and one thinks that it is a dislocation; and it is the bone which appears, wherefore one should not be presumptuous: by chance it would bring about death.
Know that at the joints of the hip and at the joints of the shoulder there comes forth only dislocations, and it does not come there as with the other joints;
and the hip joint is dislocated according to four parts: inward and outward, and forward and backward;
and more inward than elsewhere, and little dislocated forward and backward.
And the recognition of its inward dislocation is that when one joins the diseased leg with the healthy one, it is longer, and the diseased knee appears more than the healthy one, and the patient cannot bend his knee to the fire; and the place which is nearby is swollen with a hot abscess, because the head of the thigh comes there.
And the sign of that which comes outwardly, is that these accidents are contrary to those above.
And the signs of that which happens in front, is that he extends his leg well according to the fulfillment; however, he does not bend the knee without pain, and if one wants to make it go, it cannot forward, and he retains his urine, and the groin swells, and when he walks, he steps on the heel.
And the signs of that which happens behind, is that he cannot extend his knee, nor bend it, and that leg is more twisted than the other, and the groin is softened, and the head of the thigh is in the place beneath the ribs.
The treatment of this type of dislocation is that you observe if the dislocation is old, and it remains according to its disposition, then there is no cure; and there is no need for presumption.
And in that one where the dislocation is fresh, and it is of the four manners of dislocation, hasten that you turn the joint, and extend it inwards and outwards, and move it to the right and left; by chance it will return, and needs no other cure.
And if it does not return in this manner, then you must prepare a strong assistant, and extend the leg strongly with two hands, or with a rope tied above the knee; and another assistant extends his hand above in such a manner that he puts his hands under his armpits, then binds him softly with a rope above the thigh; and another holds the head of the rope;
and they extend upwards and downwards all at once, until the patient is lifted from the ground at once, and remains hanging.
This method of extension is common to the four similar [cases], and if the dislocation has returned, as we have said, it is well.
If not, then it is necessary to treat it similarly.
When the dislocation is inward, it is necessary that you place the patient on his healthy side, then place the rope on the root of the thigh, and the place which is under the groin, then extend the rope upwards towards the groin, and towards the clavicle;
then let another assistant take his arms, and embrace the large part of the affected thigh, and extend it outwards strongly; then it will return to its place immediately;
and this type of dislocation is easier than others.
And if you cannot set it in the manner we have described, then you must bind the patient's two feet together with a strong, soft rope, above the two heels, and above the two knees, and let the affected leg be extended two fingers more than the other;
then let it be suspended from the top of a beam, and let it be two arm-lengths above the ground;
then instruct an assistant to embrace the head of the thigh, and let another assistant hang with the patient, who firmly embraces the thigh, for the joint returns immediately and quickly.
And when the dislocation is in the hip, let the patient lie down, as we have said in the case of one with gibbosity, and tightly bind a rope around the affected leg properly, and also around the foot;
then place two burning coals, one at the head and another at the feet, then insert a burning coal between the patient's thighs in the middle of the groin; and have moistened cloths placed around, so that it does not burn the patient, then let each assistant extend towards him, and let the physician put the dislocation back in place with his hand.
If it returns, it is good.
If not, put it on a table, and press it as we have said in the case of gibbosity, and let the patient lie on his healthy side.
And when the dislocation is in front, it is necessary to extend the affected leg forcefully, and according to what I have said; and let the physician put his effort on the affected angle, then press with the other hand, and extend it downwards towards the knee.
And if the dislocation is behind, it is not necessary to extend the affected limb downwards, or lift it from the ground, but it should be as if the hip were dislocated behind, as we have said about lying on the two hips; and let him be on his face, and the limb extended as we have said before, and one must press on the joint at the table; and it will be right when the patient extends his legs, and they are equal; then bring the thighs together, and apply the plaster, and bind it with a bandage, so that the hip does not move; and let it rest for three or four days, then unbind it, and apply the plaster again;
and if you see that they are equal, then bind it, as before, and leave it for three days;
then unbind it, and have him walk gently, until the limb is strengthened.
Of the dislocation of the knee
The knee is dislocated in three ways: that is to say, inwards and outwards, and backwards;
and it does not dislocate forward.
And the sign is that you instruct the patient to bend his leg to his thigh, and if it does not bend to his thigh, know that it is dislocated;
and the restoration of all is that you make the patient sit upright, and extend his leg if he can, and put your back against his face, and put his foot on your feet;
then take his knee in your palms, and pull it between your fingers, then bend the two sides of the knee strongly; and another assistant extends his foot, until the knee returns to its place, and the sign is that he bends his leg easily to his thigh;
and apply the plaster, and bind the leg to the thigh, and bind them together tightly for three or four days, then unbind it;
and the patient should not exert himself to walk, until the limb is strengthened.
And if the limb is not set, the assistant should strongly extend it, as we have said in the treatment of the hip, until it is healed.
of the dislocation of the heel
The heel is sometimes sprained, and sometimes completely dislocated, both inwards and outwards.
And the sign is that the ankle is swollen, and more so on the side where it is dislocated:
The reduction is easy, and one should gently extend it with one's hands when it is only sprained.
And when it is completely dislocated, you must make the patient sit upright, and hold him firmly by the leg, above the ankle;
then pull the foot towards you with your right hand, and then with your left twice, then extend it upwards, and gently place the foot upwards;
and do this twice, as we have said, by pushing and pulling the foot;
If it returns, it is well;
if not, then begin the procedure again, for it will return;
and if it cannot return thus, then make the patient lie on his back on the ground, and firmly fix a stake in the ground between his thighs, and tie cloths around it, so that it does not injure the patient;
then let the assistant hold the thigh, and another assistant extend the foot with his hands or with a rope, which he ties above the ankle; then let each assistant extend equally, and the stake should always be between the patient's thighs to restrain the patient when he is extended;
then let the physician carefully reset the dislocation with his hands, and another assistant hold the healthy leg underneath, and the dislocation will return quickly.
And when it is in place, apply the plaster and lint, and bind it firmly, and tie the foot to the leg, and watch the nerves that are on the ankle behind, that the bandage is not too tight;
then leave it for two or three days, and if the bandage loosens, tighten it;
then untie it on the third or fourth day, and forbid the patient from walking for four days; and if he walks before that time, it is not certain that it will not dislocate again.
And if a hot abscess develops, you must soothe it, as we have said elsewhere, until it is healed.
of the fingers When the fingers are dislocated, they must be gently extended without force, and this is an easy thing.
And if the dislocation is in any joint of the back of the foot, you must have the patient sit on a level surface or on a completely straight table, and place your foot on the exposed part of the joint, then press firmly with your foot until it returns;
and when it is even, and nothing shows, then place a two-legged table under the sole of the foot, then bind it firmly, and leave it for three days, then unbind it; and let it rest until it is healed.
of wound and dislocation together
When a wound occurs with the dislocation, and one wants to restore it, it often happens that the patient dies:
so do not be presumptuous in the treatment, if you are not wise in the art and have not practiced it for a long time, and if you are not patient and merciful and slow, not rash;
and use at the beginning soothing things for a hot abscess, and leave the patient in God's hands, if you see no hope of healing; and when you see hope, then immediately restore the limb to its place, before the hot abscesses come;
and when the limb is in place, administer things soothing to a hot abscess, and treat the wound, as is necessary, with drying ointments;
and if with the dislocation there is a fracture, and small bones separated, remove them, and do what we have said in simple illnesses;
and guard yourself from entering the path of fear, according to what I have said, for it benefits you according to your Creator, and for the safety of your soul and your body.
