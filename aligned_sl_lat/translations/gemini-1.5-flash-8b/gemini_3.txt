Part I.
Concerning the benefit and harm of the cautery; and with what complexions it is suitable, and at what time; and if with iron, or gold.
Chapter 1.
But before I recount the operation with it, it is necessary that I speak of the quality of its benefits and harms, and in what complexion it is to be employed.
I say therefore, that discourse on the quality of the cautery's benefit and harm is a lengthy and subtle discourse, a matter of knowledge and hidden secret.
Many wise men have also spoken on this, and disagreed on it; and for my part, a little from their sayings suffices me, fearing prolixity.
I say therefore, that the cautery benefits every complexion, whether with matter or without matter, except for the two complexions which are hot without matter and dry without matter.
However, they disagreed on the hot and dry complexion with matter.
For some said that the cautery is beneficial in it.
And others said the opposite, namely that the cautery is not suitable in the illness which is from heat and dryness, because the nature of fire is heat, and it is unsuitable for healing from a hot and dry illness with hot and dry [means].
And he who says the opposite said: that through the cautery help is given in every hot and dry illness that occurs in human bodies, because when you compare the human body and its humours to the complexion of fire, you find the human body cold.
And I myself say, through his statement: because experience has already revealed that to me many times.
However, it is not necessary that it come upon that, unless one who is already proven and exercised in the last exercise of the cautery chapter, and has known the diversity of human complexions and the disposition of illnesses within themselves, and their causes, and their accidents, and their duration of time.
However, there is no fear from the remaining complexions; and especially in cold and humid illnesses. For now all physicians have agreed upon them, and they have not disagreed in the help provided by the cautery in those cases.
And know, children, that the secret of healing through cautery with fire, and its goodness over cautery with burning medicine, is that fire is simple, whose action does not reach except the member that is cauterized; and does not harm any other member joined to it, except by a small harm.
However, the action of burning medicine reaches what is separated from the members; and perhaps causes illness of difficult healing in the member, and perhaps kills it.
But fire, because of its subtlety and goodness of its substance, does not do that, unless it overflows.
Now, however, this has become known to us through experience, because of the length of service and study with the art, and our standing upon the truth of things; and because of that I am excused from the length of speech.
And know, children, that they disagree on the time in which the cautery should be performed, and they set a better time for the verus.
And I say that the cautery is suitable at all times, because the harm which is incidental to time is submerged in the benefit which is attracted with the cautery.
And especially if the cautery is for pains from necessary, strong, urgent pains, which do not tolerate slowness, because it is feared from them that a greater evil will happen, than the small harm which enters because of time.
And do not let it enter your minds, O children, that which the common people and foolish physicians do not fear, namely that the illness which is cured by cautery does not always return.  And they put this as a consequence, and the matter is not as they think. For the cautery is not, except in the place of medicine, which alters the complexion and dries the humours that are the causes of the pains that occur.
However, the cautery excels over medicine in the speed of its cure, and the virtue of its operation, and the vehemence of its dominion. And it is possible that the illness may return at some time according to the complexion of the infirm person, and the location of his illness, and its strength, and that which is prepared in his body from the aggregation of superfluities in him, and that which arrives at him in his acquisition from foods and similar causes, unless there is a disease in which the cautery is administered. A subtle illness in a limb of few superfluities and humours, like a toothache, because of the pain, and things similar to it, it is possible that that pain will not return in it.
But that happens in few cases.
But truly the common people's statement, that cautery is the last resort of medicine, is a correct statement, not in the sense they intend by it. For they believe that there is no cure which can compare with medicine, nor with anything else after the application of cautery.
However, the matter is the opposite of that. The idea that cautery is the last resort of medicine is not the case unless when we apply methods of treatment in some illnesses, and those treatments do not work; then, at the end of the matter, we administer cautery, and it works.  Then, it follows that cautery is the last resort of medicine, not in the sense understood by the common people and many foolish physicians.
However, the ancients said that cautery with gold is better than cautery with iron.  And they did not say this except because of the equal weight and the high quality of its substance.  And they said that the site of the cautery does not produce pus. But that is not absolutely so, for I have already tested it, and found that it does not do so in some bodies without others.
And cautery with it [gold] is good, and better than with iron, as they said.
However, when you apply a cautery of gold in fire, it is not clear to you when it is heated to the desired degree because of the redness of the gold.  And when it approaches the cold, and if you add heat to it, it melts in the fire and turns into sheets.
Therefore, the craftsman falls away from that practice.  And because of this, cautery with iron is faster and more readily available for us, from the straightforwardness of the operation.
And I indeed have already arranged this chapter on cautery, according to chapters, and have arranged them from head to foot, so that they may alleviate the one seeking what they want from it. Concerning the method of cauterizing the head at the coronal suture.
