and it is that the patient be stretched on his face in one even place, and have a cushion beneath him, that he not be injured;
then stick one firebrand firmly in the ground at the head, and another at the feet, and tie one rope across the armpits of the patient, and another at the hips, and at the knees, and at the ankles;
and two assistants hold the ropes, one at the head and the other at the feet, between the two firebrands;
and let them be firmly fixed, the firebrands as we have said, and the physician place his palms on the spine firmly, until it returns.
And if it does not return by this treatment, then take a board three arms long, and place it under the wall, and let the patient be on his side, and place the head of the board under the wall, then place the middle of the board on the spine; and let the physician place his feet on the other end of the board, and press well, until it returns to its place;
and if you wish, do it with a lathe that turns by hand. And it is that you insert two firebrands at the head of the patient at a distance of one arm's length, and towards the feet two others, and there should be between each firebrand, above and below, a palm's breadth of space in length;
and let there be in each firebrand one hole, where the lathe runs, and let the firebrands be firmly in the earth, so that they do not move;
and between each two firebrands let there be one turning firebrand, on which the ropes turn, and let there be on two turning firebrands two small firebrands of a palm's length, to turn the lathe;
and then tie the rope which is through the armpits to the lathe towards the head, and the other rope towards the feet to the other lathe, and each of the assistants turns his lathe with all the rope, and the master sets the spine straight as we have said.
And when it is straight, then apply a desiccant plaster with egg white, and then the lint, and then a splint of a tablet three fingers long, which covers the place of the gibbosity and the part of the healthy vertebra, then bind it with a rope, as we have said, and let the patient use subtle nourishment, until he is healed;
and if anything comes at the end of the healing, then it is necessary to administer softening and soothing medicines, with the splint that we have mentioned above; and sometimes one administers a piece of lead, and sometimes the appearance comes in the last vertebra of the back, and one thinks that it is a dislocation; and it is the bone which appears, wherefore one should not presume there: by chance it would bring death.
Know that at the joints of the hip and at the joints of the shoulder there comes forth only dislocations, not as in other joints;
and the hip joint is dislocated according to four parts: inwards and outwards, and forwards and backwards;
and more inwards than elsewhere, and little dislocated forwards and backwards.
And the recognition of its inward dislocation is that when one joins the diseased leg with the healthy one, it is longer, and the diseased knee is more open than the healthy one, and the patient cannot bend his knee to the fire; and the place which is nearby is swollen with a hot abscess, because the head of the thigh comes there.
And the sign of that which comes outwards, is that these accidents are contrary to those above.
And the signs of that which happens in front, is that he straightens his leg according to its length; nevertheless, he does not bend his knee without pain, and if one wants to make him walk, he cannot go forward, and he retains his urine, and his groin swells, and when he walks, he steps on his heel.
And the signs of that which happens behind, is that he cannot extend his knee, nor bend it, and that leg is more twisted than the other, and the groin is softened, and the head of the thigh is below the ribs.
The treatment of this kind of dislocation is that you consider if the dislocation is old, and if it remains according to its disposition, then there is no cure; and there is no need for presumption.
And in him whose dislocation is fresh, and this is of the four kinds of dislocation, hasten that you turn the joint, and extend it inwards and outwards, and move it to the right and left; by chance it will return, and needs no other cure.
And if it does not return in this manner, then you must prepare a strong assistant, and extend the leg strongly with two hands, or with a linen cloth tied above the knee; and another assistant extends his hand above in such a way that he puts his hands under his armpits, then binds him with a soft linen cloth above the thigh; and another holds the head of the linen;
and they extend up and down all at once, until the patient is lifted from the ground at once, and remains hanging.
This method of extension is common to the four similar cases, and if the dislocation has returned, as we have said, it is well.
If not, then it is necessary to apply a cure to its likeness.
When the dislocation is inward, you must place the patient on his sound side, then place the linen on the root of the thigh, and the place which is under the groin, then extend the linen upward toward the groin, and toward the collarbone;
then let another assistant take his arms, and embrace the thick part of the affected thigh, and extend it outwards forcefully; then it will return to its place immediately;
and this type of dislocation is easier than others.
And if you cannot set it in this manner that we have described, then you must bind the patient's two feet together with a strong, soft linen above the two heels, and above the two knees, and let the affected leg be extended two fingers more than the other;
then let it be hung from the top of a beam, and let it be two arm-lengths above the ground;
then instruct an assistant to embrace the head of the thigh, and let another assistant hang with the patient, who firmly embraces the thigh, for the joint returns immediately quickly.
And when the dislocation is outward, let the patient lie down, as we have said in the case of those with gibbosity, and tighten a linen bandage on the affected leg properly, and on the foot as well;
then place two firebrands, one at the head and another at the feet, then insert a firebrand between the patient's thighs in the middle of the groin; and have moistened cloths placed around, so that it does not burn the patient, then let each assistant extend towards him, and let the physician put the dislocation back in place with his hand.
If it returns, that is good.
If not, place him on a table, and press him as we have said in the case of gibbosity, and let the patient lie on his healthy side.
And when the dislocation is in front, it is necessary to extend the affected leg quickly, and according to what I have said; and let the physician put his effort on the affected angle, then press with the other hand, and extend it downwards towards the knee.
And if the dislocation is behind, it is not necessary to extend the affected limb downwards, nor to lift it from the ground, but it should be as if the hip were dislocated behind, as we have said about lying on the two hips; and he should be on his face, and the limb extended as we have said before, and one must press on the joint at the table; and it will be perfect when the patient extends his legs, and they are equal; then bring the thighs together, and apply the plaster, and bind it with a bandage, so that the hip does not move; and let it rest for three or four days, then unbind it, and apply the plaster again;
and if you see that they are equal, then bind it, as before, and leave it for three days;
then unbind it, and have him walk gently, until the limb is strengthened.
Of the dislocation of the knee
The knee is dislocated in three ways: that is to say, inwards and outwards, and backwards;
and it does not dislocate forwards.
And the sign is that you instruct the patient to bend his leg to his thigh, and if it does not bend to his thigh, know that it is dislocated;
and the restoration of all is that you make the patient sit upright, and extend his leg if he can, and put your back against his face, and put his foot on your feet;
then take his knee in your palms, and pull it between your fingers, then bend the two sides of the knee strongly; and another assistant extends his foot, until the knee returns to its place, and the sign is that he bends his leg easily to his thigh;
and apply the plaster, and make the leg bend to the thigh, and bind them together tightly for three or four days, then unbind it;
and let the patient not exert himself to walk, until the limb is strengthened.
And if the limb is not straight, administer strong extension, as we have said in the treatment of the hip, until it is healed.
of the dislocation of the heel
The heel is sometimes displaced, and sometimes completely dislocated, both inward and outward.
And the sign is that the ankle is swollen, and tense towards the part where it is dislocated:
The reduction is easy, and one should gently extend it with one's hands when it is only displaced.
And when it is completely dislocated, you must make the patient sit upright, and hold him firmly by the leg, above the ankle;
then pull the foot towards you with your right hand, and then with your left hand twice, then extend it upwards, and gently put the foot upwards;
and do this twice, as we have said, by pushing and pulling the foot;
If it returns, it is good;
If not, repeat the process, for it will return;
and if it cannot return thus, make the patient lie on his back on the ground, and firmly stick a stake in the ground between his thighs, and tie cloths around it, so that it does not injure the patient;
then let the assistant hold the thigh, and another assistant extend the foot with his hands or with a rope, which he ties above the ankle; then let each assistant extend equally, and the stake should always be between the patient's thighs to restrain the patient when he is extended;
then let the physician equally set the dislocation with his hands, and another assistant hold the healthy leg underneath, and the dislocation will return quickly.
And when it is in place, put the plaster and lint on, and bind it firmly, and tie the foot to the leg, and guard the nerves which are on the ankle behind, that the binding is not too tight;
then leave it two or three days, and if the binding loosens, tighten it;
then unbind it on the third or fourth day, and forbid the patient from walking for four days; and if he walks before that time, it is not certain that it will not dislocate again.
And if hot abscesses appear there, then you must soothe them, as we have said elsewhere, until they are healed.
of the fingers When the fingers are dislocated, they must be gently extended without effort, and this is an easy thing.
And if the dislocation is in any of the knots of the back of the foot, then you must have the patient sit on a level place or on a perfectly straight table, and place your foot on what is visible of those joints, then press firmly with your foot until it returns;
and when it is even, and nothing is visible there, then place a two-legged table under the sole of the foot, then bind it firmly, and leave it for three days, then unbind it; and make him rest until he is healed.
of wound and dislocation together
When a wound occurs with the dislocation, and one wants to restore it, many times it happens that the patient dies:
so do not be presumptuous in the treatment, if you are not wise in the art and have not practiced it for a long time, and if you are not patient and merciful and slow, not rash;
and use at the beginning soothing things for a hot abscess, and leave the patient in God's hands, if you see no hope of healing; and when you see hope, then immediately restore the limb to its place, before the hot abscesses come there;
and when the limb is in place, then administer things that soothe a hot abscess, and treat the wound, as is necessary, with drying ointments;
and if with the dislocation there is a fracture, and small bones separated, then remove them, and do what we have said in simple illnesses;
and guard yourself from entering a path of fear, according to what I have said, for this is worth more to you according to your Creator, and for the salvation of your soul and your body.
