Chapter I.
But before I recall the operation with it, it is necessary that I speak of the quality of its benefits and harms: and in which complexion it should be exhibited.
Therefore I say that the discussion of the quality of the benefit of cautery, and of its harm, is a lengthy and subtle discussion, a science, and a hidden secret.
Many wise men have also spoken on it, and they have disagreed: and indeed a little from their discussions suffices me, fearing prolixity.
Therefore I say that cautery universally benefits every complexion which is with matter and without matter, except for two complexions which are a hot complexion without matter and a dry complexion without matter.
However, in a hot and dry complexion with matter, they disagreed.
For some have said that cautery is beneficial in it.
And others said the contrary, namely that cautery is not suitable in an illness which is from heat and dryness; because the nature of fire is heat: and it is unsuitable that a hot and dry illness be cured with something hot and dry.
And he who says the contrary said: that through cautery benefit is done in every hot and dry illness which happens in the bodies of men; because when you compare the body of man and its humors to the complexion of fire, you find the body of man cold.
And I indeed say through his speech: because experience has already revealed that to me several times.
However, it is not fitting that he come to it unless he is already proven and practiced in the chapter of cautery exercise lastly, and has known the diversity of the complexions of men, and the disposition of illnesses in themselves, and their causes, and their accidents, and the space of their time.
From the other complexions however there is no fear for you; and especially in cold and moist illnesses. For now all physicians have agreed on them: and they did not disagree in benefit with cautery in them.
And know, sons, that from the secret of healing through cautery with fire, and its goodness over cautery with burning medicine, is this: because fire is simple, whose action does not arrive except to the member which is cauterized: and it does not harm another member joined with it, except with little harm.
The action of burning medicines however arrives at that which is distant from the members: and perhaps it causes illness in a member difficult of healing, and perhaps it kills.
Fire however, because of its subtlety and the goodness of its substance, does not do that, unless it overflows.
Now indeed that has become known to us through experiment, because of the length of service and study with the art, and standing upon the truth of things: and because of that I excuse myself from length of speech.
And know, sons, that they disagree in the time in which it is fitting to perform cautery, and they place the better time in spring.
And I say that cautery is suitable at all times: therefore, because harm happening because of time is submerged in the help which is drawn with cautery.
And especially if cautery is because of pains from necessary, strong, urgent pains which do not tolerate slowness: because it is feared from them that harm which is greater than the small harm entering because of time may happen.
And let not that which the common people and foolish physicians only fear fall into your minds, O sons, namely that that illness which is healed by cautery does not always return: and they posit that as a consequence, and the matter is not as they think: because cautery is nothing but a kind of medicine which alters the complexion, and dries up the humors which are the causes why pains happen.

Concerning the usefulness of phlebotomy, and the precautions to be observed in it.
Moreover, the quality of phlebotomy, and its consequences arising from it, in whose rectification it behooves you to think.
First therefore of that it is necessary that you know, that phlebotomy is not administered except in the preservation of health, and its continuance, and precaution against the coming of illnesses.
And that phlebotomy be done in one of the three veins which are in the elbow, namely the Cephalic, and the Median, and the Basilic.
And that phlebotomy be done at the beginning of Spring upon fasting, in spring and summer: and in autumn and winter after eating, when signs of repletion appear.
And let phlebotomy be done on the first day, or on the third, after three hours have passed from the day.
Phlebotomy however which is administered in illnesses, does not have a determined hour: but when convenience and necessity prompt to it, in the night, or day, and at every hour, and at every time.
And it is not fitting that boys be bled until fourteen years come upon them.
Nor let a pregnant woman be bled, except from the fourth month to the seventh.
Nor let old men be bled, who have already passed sixty years.

Nor let a patient suffering from nausea, that is, a sickening fullness, be bled; nor one who is intoxicated, nor one recovering from drunkenness, until that is removed from them.
Nor one ill with dropsy.
And let phlebotomy be avoided again after colic, vomiting, diarrhoea, excessive intercourse, labor, exercise, wakefulness, fasting, and everything that weakens the bodily or vital strength.
Then consider the subtlety of the humors beforehand, if the blood is thick with food and drink and medicines: if that is possible for him.

Concerning cupping, and the quality of its use.
Chapter XCVIII.
Cups are made from horns, and from metal, and from glass.
And cupping is done in two ways.
With scarification and the extraction of blood: and the other is cupping without scarification. And this cupping which is without scarification, is according to two methods.
Or it is with fire.
Or it is without fire.
And there are fourteen places on the body for cuppings which are administered with scarification and bloodletting.
One of which is the cupping of Alnocrati, and it is at the back of the head: and Alkehel, and it is the middle of the neck: and the cuppings of Alhagdahim, and they are two plates of the neck on both sides together: and the cuppings of the chin, and it is under the jaw in the lower part of the mouth: and the cuppings of the two shoulder blades: and the cuppings of Alachosos on the end of the spine: and the cuppings of the two elbows, and they are the middle of the two arms: and the cuppings of the two thighs: and the cuppings of the two calves.
And cupping indeed does not draw blood from you from the small veins scattered in the flesh, and because of that strength does not fall from them, as is the case with phlebotomy.
Nor should cupping be administered, whether with fire or without fire, in any illnesses which arise from repletion, until the whole body is evacuated.
But if necessity demands cupping because of illness or habit, we administer it at any time at the beginning of the month, at the end of it, and in the middle of it, and at whatever time it may be.
That is because there are men in whom blood multiplies until it needs extraction by cupping, and they find heaviness and headache in their head.
And there are those who find fullness and redness in their face, head, and neck.
And there are those who find itching in their face and forehead, and darkness and dullness in their eyes.
And there are those who scratch the place of their cupping. And there are those whose laughter increases.
And there are those who find the taste of blood in their mouth; or their tongue is ulcerated, and they spit blood.
And there are those whose sleep increases.
And there are those who see in their sleep blood and redness, and the slain, and wounds, and things similar to them. Therefore, when we see something of these things, and especially if it is in the middle third of the month, we then order that bloodletting be done, after two or three hours of the day have passed. Now, the benefit of cupping the nape of the neck is that it helps with heaviness in the head and that which spreads to the eyes.
