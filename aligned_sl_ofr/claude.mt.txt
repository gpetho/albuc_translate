and this type of dislocation is easier than the others.
And if you cannot secure it in this way that we have described, it is necessary that you tie the two feet of the patient together with a strong gentle bandage above the two heels, and above the two knees, and let the diseased leg be extended two fingers longer than the other;
then let him be hung from the head of a beam, and be raised from the ground two arms' length;
then command an assistant to embrace the head of the thigh, and let another assistant hang with the patient, who strongly embraces the thigh, for the joint returns immediately hastily.
And when the dislocation is outward, let the patient lie, as we have said for the one who has a hump, and tighten a bandage on the diseased leg specifically, and on the chest also;
then put two poles, one at the head and another at the feet, then fix a pole between the patient's thighs in the middle of the form; and have plenty of moist cloths around, so it doesn't hurt the patient, then let each assistant pull toward himself, and let the physician put the dislocation back in place with his hand.
If it returns, that is good.
If not, put it on a table, and press it as we have said for the hump, and let the patient lie on the healthy side.
And when the dislocation is forward, it is necessary to extend the diseased leg hastily, and according to what I have said; and let the physician make an effort to be above the diseased groin, then press with the other hand, and extend it downward toward the knee.
And if the dislocation is outward, it is not necessary to extend the patient downward, nor raise him from the ground, rather it is necessary that he be as if the hip was dislocated outward, as we have said about lying on the two hips; and let him be on his face, and the bandage extended as we have said before, and one must press on the joint with the table; and it will be right when the patient extends his legs, and they are equal; then join the thighs, and put the plaster, and bind it with a bandage, so that the hip does not move; and let him rest three or four days, then unbind it, and put the plaster on;
and if you see that they are equal, bind it, as before, and leave it three days;
then unbind it, and make him walk gently, until the limb is strengthened.
On the dislocation of the knee
The knee is dislocated in three ways: that is to say, inward and outward, and backward;
and does not dislocate forward.
And the sign is that you command the patient to bend his leg to his thigh, and if it does not attach to his thigh, know that it is dislocated;
and the restoration of all is that you make the patient sit straight, and extend his leg if he can, and put your back on his face, and put his foot on your feet;
then take his knee in your palms, and pull back between your fingers, then bend the two sides of the knee strongly; and another assistant extends his foot, until the knee returns to its place, and the sign is that it attaches his leg to his thigh easily;
and put the plaster on, and make the leg attach to the thigh, and bind them together strongly for three or four days, then unbind it;
and let the patient not trouble himself with walking, until the limb is strengthened.
And if the limb is not right, then administer strong extension, as we have said in the treatment of the hip, until it is healed
of the dislocation of the heel
The heel is sometimes moved, and sometimes completely dislocated, both inward and outward.
And the sign is that the ankle is swollen, and tends forward to the part where it is dislocated:
the reduction is easy, and one should extend it gently with one's hands when it is only moved.
And when it is completely dislocated, it is necessary that you make the patient sit upright, and a strong assistant should hold them by the leg, above the ankle;
then pull the foot toward you with your right hand, and then with the left hand two times, then extend it upward, and gently push the foot upward;
and do this twice, as we have said, pushing and pulling the foot;
if it returns, that is good;
if not, then begin the work again, for it will return;
and if it cannot return this way, make the patient lie on their back on the ground, and firmly plant a stake in the ground between their thighs, and tie cloths around it, so it doesn't hurt the patient;
then let the assistant hold the thigh, and another assistant extend the foot with his hands or with a band, which he ties over the top of the foot; then let each assistant pull equally, and let the stake remain between the patient's thighs to hold the patient when they pull;
then let the physician align the dislocation with his hands, and another assistant should hold the healthy leg below, and the dislocation will return quickly.
And when it is in place, put the plaster and tow on it, and bind it firmly, and tie the foot to the leg, and watch the nerves that are behind the ankle, that the binding is not too tight;
then leave it for two or three days, and if the binding loosens, tighten it;
then unbind it on the third or fourth day, and forbid the patient from walking for four days; and if they walk before this term, there is no certainty that it will not dislocate again.
And if hot swelling comes, it is necessary that you calm it, as we have said elsewhere, until it is healed.
of the toes When the toes are dislocated, it is necessary to gently extend them without effort, and this is an easy thing.
And if the dislocation is in any joint of the top of the foot, it is necessary that you make the patient sit on a flat place or on a table completely straight, and put your foot on what appears of these joints, then press firmly with your foot until it returns;
and when it is aligned, and nothing appears, put a board with two ends under the sole of the foot, then bind it firmly, and leave it for three days, then unbind it; and make them rest until it is healed.
of wound and dislocation together
When a wound comes with the dislocation, and one wants to restore it, many times it happens that the patient dies:
so do not be presumptuous in the treatment, if you are not wise in the art and have not practiced it for a long time, and if you are not compassionate and merciful and slow, not bold;
and use at the beginning things that calm hot swelling, and leave the patient in God's hands, if you see no hope of health; and when you see hope, then return the limb to its place immediately, before the hot swelling comes;
and when the limb is in place, administer things that calm hot swelling, and treat the wound, as necessary, with drying ointments;
and if with the dislocation there is breakage, and small separated bones, then remove them, and do what we have said in simple diseases;
and beware of entering the path of fear, according to what I have said, for this serves you better according to your Creator, and for the safety of your soul and body.
