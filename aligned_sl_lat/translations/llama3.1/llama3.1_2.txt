Part I.
On the application and harm of cautery: to what constitutions it is suited, at what time: and whether with iron or gold.
Chapter I.
But before that, as I recall the operation with it, it is necessary to say the quality of its applications and its harms, and in what complexion it is shown.
Therefore I say that the discourse about the quality of the application of cautery and its harm is a long and subtle one, requiring knowledge and a hidden secret.
And many wise men have spoken on the subject and disagreed about it, and for my part I find that a little from their words is enough for me, fearing its prolongation.
Therefore I say that cautery is applied universally to every constitution which has matter and lacks matter: excepting the two constitutions, which are a hot constitution lacking matter and a dry constitution lacking matter.
But in a hot and dry constitution with matter they disagreed.
For some said that cautery is beneficial in it.
And others said the opposite of that, namely that cautery is not suitable in a disease caused by heat and dryness, because the nature of fire is heat, and it is unseemly to heal from a hot and dry affliction with something hot and dry.
And he who said the opposite of that said that through cautery a benefit is achieved in every hot and dry affliction that occurs in human bodies: because when you compare the body of man and its humors to the temperament of fire, you find the body of man cool.
And I myself say through his words, because experience has already revealed it to me a few times.
However it is not necessary that he should come to that unless he has already been proved and practiced in the exercise of Cauterization, and knows the diversity of human complexions and the disposition of illnesses within themselves, and their causes, and accidents, and the space of time for them.
But as to the other complexions, you should have no fear over them; and especially in cold and moist illnesses. For indeed all physicians have come together on these, and have not disagreed with the use of a cautery in them.
And know, my sons, that from the secret of treatment by cautery with fire and its goodness in relation to a burning cautery with medicine, it is this: because fire is simple, whose action does not come except to the member being cauterized, and it does not harm another joined part to it, unless slight damage.
But the action of burning medicine reaches beyond the member that is extended from the body and perhaps causes an accident to happen in the afflicted member making its cure difficult and perhaps killing it.
But fire because of its subtlety and the goodness of its substance does not do that unless it overflows.
For indeed we have come to know that through experience, because of the length of service and study with art, and standing on the truth of things: and because of this I excuse myself from the length of speech.
And know, my sons, that they disagree among themselves as to the time when it is fitting to make a cautery, and set better the true time.
And I say that a cautery is fitting at any time, therefore the damage occurring because of the time is submerged in the benefit which is attracted with the cautery.
And especially if the cautery is for pains from necessary, strong, and frequent pains, which do not tolerate delay: therefore because it is feared that a greater evil may occur than the small damage that would pass through with time.
And do not let that thought fall into your minds, my sons, which the common people and foolish doctors suppose, namely that there is always a return of the illness which is cured by cauterization: and they put down as a consequence what is not so: therefore because cauterization is merely a substitute for medicine, which alters the complexion and dries up the humors which are the causes why pains occur.
However, cauterization is indeed an excess over medicine by the swiftness of its own nature and the power of its operation and the vehemence of its authority: and it is possible that the illness may return after some hour of time according to the complexion of the sick person and the location of his illness and the power of it: and what is prepared in his body from the aggregation of superfluities in him: and which comes to him in his acquisition from foods, and similar things from causes, unless an illness occurs in which cauterization is administered. The illness is a subtle one of few superfluities and humors in the member, as in a tooth cauterized because of pain, and similar ones: for it is possible that the pain will not return in it.
But this happens in few cases.
But the common people's talk again that cauterization is the last resort of medicine, is a true saying: not for what they intend by it themselves: because they believe that there is no cure which comes together with medicine, nor with another remedy after the occasion of the cautery has passed.
But the matter is contrary to that and the intent is not so, that cauterization is the last resort of medicine, is only when we administer modes of cure for some illnesses, and those medicines do not take effect: then we administer in the end of the thing cauterization and it takes effect: thence it follows that cauterization is the last resort of medicine, not according to the intention which the common people and many foolish doctors understand.
The ancients said that the cautery with gold is better than the cautery with iron: and they did not say this except on account of the equality of gold and the subtlety of its substance: and they said that a place of cautery does not create pus. In truth it is not that absolutely: for I have already tested this and found that it does not do so in certain bodies without doing so in others.
And the cautery with it is good, and better than with iron, as they said.
But indeed when you put the cautery made of gold into fire, it is not clear to you how much heat it has, because of the reddening of the gold: and when it starts to get cold and you add more fire on top of it, it melts in the fire and turns into sheets.
Therefore craftsmen fall back on it in their work: and because of this a cautery with iron is made among us more quickly and closer to the operation, in fairness.
