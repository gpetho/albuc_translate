Praefatio.	Preface.
Postquam compleui uobis o Filii, librum hunc, qui est postremus scientiae in medicina cum complemento ipsius, et consecutus sum finem in eo, ex expositionibus eius, et ipsius declarationibus, uisum est mihi, ut compleam ipsum uobis cum hoc Tractatu qui est pars operationis, id est, chirurgiae cum manu.	After I completed for you, O sons, this book, which is the final one in medical science, along with its supplements, and I reached its end with its explanations and clarifications, it seemed fitting to me to complete it for you with this Treatise, which is a part of practice, that is, surgery by hand.
Quoniam operatio cum manu prostrata est in regione nostra, et tempore nostro priuata omnino: donec fortasse periit scientia eius, et abscissum est uestigium eius: et non remanserunt ex ea, nisi descriptiones paruae in libris antiquorum, quas mutauerunt manus: et accidit eis error et haesitatio, donec clausae sunt intentiones eius, et elongata est uis ipsius: Visum est mihi, ut uiuificem eam cum ordinatione huius Tractatus in ea secundum uiam ex positionis, et declarationis, et abbreuiationis: et ut ueniam cum formis ferramentorum Cauterii, et reliquorum instrumentorum operis: cum fit ex declarationis additione, et ex properatione eius quod est necesse.	Since the practice of manual surgery has fallen in our region, and in our time has been almost entirely lost, until perhaps the knowledge of it perished, and its traces were cut off. Only small descriptions of it remain in the books of the ancients, altered by other hands, where errors and confusion have arisen, until its purposes became closed off and its efficacy weakened. It seemed to me fitting to bring it back to life with the organization of this Treatise, following the way of exposition, clarification, and abbreviation, and to include the forms of the cautery tools and other instruments of the practice, with explanations added and with the necessary urgency.
Caussa autem propter quam non inuenitur bonus artifex eius cum manu sua in hoc nostro tempore, est: quia ars medicinae est longa, et oportet auctorem eius exercitari ante illud in scientia anatomiae, quam narrauit Galenus, donec sciat iuuamenta membrorum, et ipsorum formas, et eorum coniunctionem, et separationem, et habeat cognitionem ossium, et neruorum, et lacertorum, et numerum ipsorum, et eorum egressionem, et uenarum pulsatilium, et quietarum, et locorum exitus earum.	The reason why a skilled practitioner in this art with his own hand is not found in our time is because the art of medicine is extensive, and its practitioner must first be trained in the science of anatomy, as described by Galen, until he knows the functions of the parts, their forms, and their connections and separations, and has knowledge of the bones, nerves, muscles, and their numbers and origins, as well as of the veins, both pulsating and still, and the places of their exits.
Et propter illud dixit Hippocrates, quod nomen cum nomine est multum, et cum opere est paucum, et praecipue in arte manus.	And because of this, Hippocrates said that name with name is plentiful, but with practice it is scarce, especially in the art of the hand.
Nos uero iam diximus de illo in introitu huius libri.	We have already spoken of this in the introduction of this book.
Quoniam qui non est sciens, quod diximus de anatomia, non euacuatur, quin iam cadat in errorem, quo interficiuntur homines: sicut ego uidi multos ex illis, qui formantur de hac scientia, et iactant se de ea absque scientia, et sine experimento.	For anyone who does not know what we have said about anatomy is not immune from error, an error by which people are killed, as I have seen many of those who are trained in this science and boast of it without knowledge and without experience.
Quod est, quia uidi medicum stolidum secuisse apostema scrophulosum in collo mulieris, et incidit quasdam arterias in collo: quare fluxit sanguis mulieris, donec cecidit mortua inter manus eius.	For instance, I saw a foolish physician who cut into a scrofulous abscess on a woman’s neck and severed certain arteries in her neck, causing her blood to flow until she died in his hands.
Et uidi medicum alium, qui praesumpsit ut extraheret lapidem uiro, qui processerat in aetate, et lapis erat magnus.	And I saw another physician who presumed to extract a stone from an older man, and the stone was large.
Peruenit ergo ad eum, et extraxit ipsum cum frusto uesicae.	He therefore reached the stone and extracted it along with a piece of the bladder.
Quare mortuus est uir in tertio die.	Thus the man died on the third day.
Et ego quidem iam uocatus fueram ad extrahendum ipsum: et uidi ex magnitudine lapidis, et dispositione infirmi, quod moriretur.	And I had been called to extract the stone myself, but I saw from the size of the stone and the patient’s condition that he would die.
Et uidi medicum alium, qui accipiebat stipem apud quendam Ducum terrae nostrae propter medicinam: et accidit eunucho nigro, qui erat apud ipsum, fractura in crure eius prope calcaneum: et properauit medicus cum ignorantia sua, et strinxit fracturam super uulnus cum puluinaribus, et astellis strictura forti, et non dimisit uulneri expirationem.	And I saw another physician who took payment from a certain Duke of our land for his practice, and a black eunuch under his care suffered a fracture in his leg near the heel. The physician, in his ignorance, rushed and bound the fracture over the wound with cushions and tight splints, not allowing the wound to breathe.
Deinde absoluit eum secundum desyderia sua.	Then he discharged him according to his own wishes.
Postea dimisit eum diebus aliquot, et praecepit ei, ne solueret ligamentum: et stetit sic donec apostematum est crus eius, et ipsius pes, et peruenit ad perditionem.	Afterwards, he left him for several days and instructed him not to loosen the bandage, and so it remained until his leg and foot became infected, resulting in ruin.
Vocatus sum ergo ad eum: et properaui ad soluendum ligamentum: et consecuta est tranquillitas, et euasit a doloribus suis.	I was therefore called to him, and I hurried to loosen the bandage, and peace followed, and he was freed from his pains.
Verumtamen corruptio iam confirmata erat in membro: et non potui refraenare ipsam.	Nevertheless, the decay in the limb was already set in, and I could not halt it.
Pon ergo cessauit corruptio perambulare in membro, donec periit.	Thus, the decay ceased to spread through the limb until it perished.
Et uidi medicum alium, qui perforauit apostema cancerosum: et ulceratum est post dies, donec magnificata est malicia eius cum domino ipsius.	And I saw another physician who pierced a cancerous abscess, and it became ulcerated after several days, until its malignancy grew along with the suffering of its owner.
Quod est, quia quando est cancer purus ex humore melancholico, tunc non oportet ut perueniatur ad eum cum ferro omnino, nisi sit in membro quod toleret, ut abscindatur totum eius.	This is because, when cancer is purely from a melancholic humor, it must never be treated with iron at all, unless it is in a limb that can endure having the entire affected area excised.
Et propter illud o Filii, necesse est uobis, ut sciatis, quod operatio cum manu diuiditur in duas diuisiones: scilicet, operationem cui associatur salus: et operationem, cum qua est periculum in pluribus dispositionibus.	And therefore, O sons, it is necessary for you to know that manual operation is divided into two categories: namely, operations associated with safety, and operations that involve danger in many conditions.
Et ego iam excitaui in omni loco huius libri, in quo uenit operatio, in qua est terror et timor.	And I have already pointed out in every place in this book where there is an operation involving terror and fear.
Quare necesse est uobis, ut caueatis illud, et dimitatis ipsum: ut non inueniant stolidi uiam ad loquendum, et dehonestandum.	Therefore, it is necessary for you to be cautious of such things and to avoid them, so that fools do not find a reason to speak and dishonor the profession.
Accipite ergo mensuram cum sollicitudine et protectione, et infirmos uestros cum facilitate et firmitudine, et utimini uia meliore perducente gratia Dei ad salutem et successionem laudabilem: et dimittite aegritudines terribiles difficilis sanationis: et tollite animas uestras ab eo quod facit uos timere, ut ingrediatur super uos haesitatio in fide uestra et commodo uestro.	Therefore, approach with care and protection, and treat your patients with gentleness and firmness, using the best method, guided by the grace of God toward health and praiseworthy outcomes; and avoid terrible illnesses that are difficult to heal; and keep your souls from things that cause you fear, lest hesitation should enter your faith and your practice.
Est enim magis remanens ad gloriam uestram, et sublimius in mundo isto, et postremo ad quantitates uestras.	For this will be of greater benefit to your glory, and will elevate you more in this world, and ultimately increase your honor.
Galenus enim iam dixit in quibusdam suis admonitionibus.	Galen has already said in certain of his teachings:
Non medicetis aegritudinem malam: ne nominemini medici mali.	Do not treat a malignant disease, lest you be called a poor physician.
Iam autem diuisi hunc Tractatum secundum tria Capitula. ¶	Now, I have divided this Treatise into three Chapters. ¶

De Cauterio cum igne, et Cauterio cum medicina acuta, capitulatum, et ordinatum a capite usque ad pedes, et formis instrumentorum, et ferramentis Cauterii: et omni quod necessarium est in operatione. ¶	On cauterization with fire, and cauterization with sharp medicines, arranged chapter by chapter, and organized from head to foot, with the forms of instruments and cautery tools, and everything necessary for practice. ¶

De restauratione, et dislocatione, et curatione torsionis, et similibus illius: capitulatum, ordinatum a capite usque ad pedes, et formis instrumentorum.	On restoration, dislocation, and treatment of twisting and similar injuries; arranged chapter by chapter from head to foot, with the forms of instruments.

Liber Chirurgici Albucasis.	The Book of Surgery by Albucasis.
Pars I.	Part I.
¶ De Iuuamento, et Nocumento Cauterii: et quibus complexionibus conueniat, et quo tempore: et si cum ferro, uel auro.	¶ On the Benefit and Harm of Cautery, and for which temperaments it is suitable, and at what time: and whether with iron or with gold.
Caput I.	Chapter I.
Sed Ante Quidem Quam rememorer operationis cum eo, oportet ut dicam qualitatem Iuuamentorum eius, et Nocumentorum ipsius: et in qua complexione exhibeatur.	But before I discuss the procedure with it, it is necessary to speak of its benefits and harms, and in what temperament it should be applied.
Dico ergo, quod sermo de qualitate iuuamenti Cauterii, et nocumenti eius, est sermo prolixus, et subtilis, scientia, et secretum occultum.	I say, then, that the discourse on the nature of the benefit and harm of cautery is a long and subtle matter, involving knowledge and hidden secrets.
Etiam locuta fuit in eo multitudo sapientum, et dissenserunt in eo: et mihi quidem sufficit parum ex sermonibus eorum, timens prolongationem.	Many wise men have spoken about it, and there has been disagreement on it; for me, a little of their discourse will suffice, as I wish to avoid length.
Dico ergo, quod Cauterium confert uniuersaliter omni complexioni, quae est cum materia, et absque materia: excaeptis duabus complexionibus, quae sunt complexio calida absque materia, et complexio sicca absque materia.	I say, then, that cautery is universally beneficial for all temperaments with or without matter, except for two temperaments: those that are hot without matter and dry without matter.
In complexione autem calida et sicca cum materia dissenserunt.	But in the case of the hot and dry temperament with matter, there is disagreement.
Quidam enim dixerunt, quod Cauterium iuuatiuum est in ea.	Some have said that cautery is beneficial in this case.
Et dixerunt alii contrarium illius, scilicet quod Cauterium non est conueniens in aegritudine, quae est ex caliditate et siccitate: quoniam natura ignis est caliditas: et inconueniens est, ut sanetur ex agritudine calida et sicca, cum calido et sicco.	Others have said the opposite, namely that cautery is not suitable for an illness arising from heat and dryness, because the nature of fire is heat, and it is unsuitable to treat a hot and dry illness with something that is also hot and dry.
Et dixit ille qui dicit contrarium illius: quod per Cauterium fit iuuamentum in omni aegritudine calida et sicca, quae accidit in corporibus hominum: quoniam quando tu comparas corpus hominis, et humiditates eius, ad complexionem ignis, inuenis corpus hominis frigidum.	And the one who claims the opposite says that cautery provides relief in any hot and dry illness occurring in human bodies, because when you compare the human body and its moistures to the temperament of fire, you find the human body to be cold.
Et ego quidem dico per sermonem eius: quia ex perimentum iam detexit mihi illud aliquotiens.	And I myself agree with his words because experience has already shown me this many times.
Verumtamen non oportet, ut ueniat super illud, nisi qui iam probatus est, et exercitatus in capitulo Cauterii exercitio ultimo, et cognouit diuersitatem complexionum hominum, et dispositionem aegritudinum in se ipsis, et caussas earum, et accidentia ipsarum, et spacium temporis earum.	Nevertheless, no one should apply it except one who has already been proven and trained in the chapter on cautery, through complete practice, and who understands the diversity of human temperaments, the disposition of illnesses within themselves, their causes, their symptoms, and their timing.
Ex reliquis autem complexionibus non est super uos timor: et praecipue in aegritudinibus frigidis et humidis.	As for the remaining temperaments, there is no fear upon you, especially in cold and moist illnesses.
Iam enim conuenerunt omnes medici super eas: et non dissenserunt in iuuamento cum Cauterio in eis.	Indeed, all physicians have agreed on these, and they have not disagreed on the benefit of cautery in such cases.
Et scitote filii, quod ex secreto curationis per Cauterium cum igne, et bonitate eius super Cauterium cum medicina comburente est: quoniam ignis est simplex, cuius actio non peruenit, nisi ad membrum quod cauterisatur: et non nocet membro alio coniuncto cum eo, nisi nocumento paruo.	And know, my sons, that one secret of curing with cautery by fire, and its superiority over cautery with burning medicine, is that fire is simple, and its effect reaches only the member being cauterized and does not harm other parts connected to it, except with minimal harm.
Medicinae uero comburentis actio peruenit ad illud quod elongatur ex membris: et fortasse facit accidere in membro aegritudinem difficilis sanationis, et fortasse interficit.	The action of burning medicines, however, spreads to the surrounding parts, and it may even cause a difficult-to-heal illness in the affected member, or it may even kill.
Ignis autem propter subtilitatem suam et bonitatem substantiae suae, non facit illud, nisi superfluat.	But fire, because of its subtlety and the quality of its substance, does not cause this unless it is excessive.
Iam uero innotuit nobis illud per experimentum, propter longitudinem seruitutis et studii cum arte, et stationem super ueritatem rerum: et propter illud excusor a longitudine sermonis.	And indeed, this has become known to us through experience, due to prolonged service and study in the art, and dedication to the truth of things; because of this, I excuse myself from prolonging the discussion.
Et si non esset, quia non licet huic libro meo, afferrem uobis secretum occultum in igne, et qualitatem operationis eius in corporibus expulsionis suae, qua expellit aegritudines sermone philosophico demonstratiuo, qui acueretur a mentibus uestris.	And if it were not that my book does not permit it, I would bring to you the hidden secret of fire, and the quality of its action in bodies for expelling sicknesses, demonstrated through philosophical reasoning, which would sharpen your minds.
Et scitote filii, quod ipsi dissentiunt in tempore, in quo conuenit fieri cauterium, et ponunt melius tempus ueris.	And know, my sons, that they disagree on the time when cautery is best performed, and they consider spring to be the best time.
Et ego dico, quod cauterium conuenit in omni tempore: propterea, quod nocumentum accidens propter tempus submergitur in iuuamento, quod attrahitur cum cauterio.	But I say that cautery is suitable at any time because the harm occurring due to timing is outweighed by the benefit drawn from cautery.
Et praecipue si cauterium est propter dolores ex doloribus necessariis, fortibus, festinis, qui non tolerant tarditatem: propterea quod timetur ex eis, quod accidat malicia quae est maior, quam paruum nocumentum intrans propter tempus.	Especially when cautery is needed for pains among the necessary pains—severe, sudden, and not tolerating delay—because it is feared that a greater harm may arise than the small harm brought by the timing.
Et non cadat in mentibus uestris o filii illud, quod non extimant, nisi uulgus et stolidi medici, scilicet quod illi aegritudini quae sanatur per cauterium, non est reditio semper: et ponunt illud consequens, et res non est sicut putant: propterea quod cauterium non est, nisi loco medicinae, quae alterat complexionem, et exiccat humiditates quae sunt caussae, quare accidunt dolores.	And do not let it enter your minds, O sons, what only the common people and ignorant physicians believe, namely, that the illness cured by cautery never returns; they take this as certain, but it is not as they think. Cautery is no more than a substitute for medicine, which changes the temperament and dries the moistures that are the causes of pain.
Verumtamen cauterium superfluit super medicinam per uelocitatem ualetudinis suae, et uirtutem suae operationis, et uehementiam dominii sui: et possibile est, ut redeat aegritudo hora aliqua temporis secundum complexionem infirmi, et locationem aegritudinis suae, et uirtutem eius: et quod praeparatur in corpore eius ex aggregatione superfluitatum in eo: et quod peruenit ad ipsum in acquisitione sua ex cibis, et similibus illis ex caussis, nisi fit aegritudo, in qua administratur cauterium.	Nevertheless, cautery surpasses medicine through the speed of its healing power, the strength of its action, and the intensity of its efficacy. And it is possible that the illness may return at some time, depending on the patient’s temperament, the location of his illness, and its strength, as well as what accumulates in his body from an aggregation of superfluities within it, and from what it acquires from foods and other similar causes—unless it is an illness for which cautery is applied.
Aegritudo subtilis in membro paucarum superfluitatum, et humiditatum, sicut cauterium dentis, propter dolorem, et similia illi, possibile est enim, ut non redeat in eo ille dolor.	A subtle illness in a part of the body with few superfluities and humidities, as in the case of cautery of a tooth for pain, and similar instances, may indeed not return in that area with the same pain.
Sed illud fit in paucis.	But this happens in few cases.
At uero sermo uulgi iterum, quod cauterium est postremum medicinae, est sermo rectus: non ad illud quod ipsi intendunt in illo: quoniam ipsi credunt, quod non est curatio quae conferat cum medicina, neque cum alio post occasum cauterii.	However, the common saying, again, that cautery is the final remedy, is a correct saying—not for the reason they assume in it, because they believe that there is no treatment that matches medicine, nor any other remedy after cautery has failed.
Res autem est econtrario illius, et intentio, quod cauterium est postremum medicinae, non est, nisi quod quando nos administramus modos curationis in aliqua aegritudinum, et non conferunt illae medicinae: deinde administramus in fine rei cauterium, et confert: tunc inde cadit, quod cauterium est postremum mediciuae, non secundum intentionem quam intelligunt uulgus et multistolidorum medicorum.	But the reality is the opposite of that, and the meaning of cautery as the final remedy is only that when we have applied all methods of treatment to some illness, and those medicines do not succeed, then we finally apply cautery, and it is effective; hence it is said that cautery is the final remedy—not with the intention understood by the common people and the multitude of ignorant physicians.
Antiqui autem dixerunt, quod cauterium cum auro est melius, quam cauterium cum ferro: et non dixerunt illud, nisi propter aequalitatem auri, et sublimitatem substantiae suae: et dixerunt, quod non facit pus locus cauterii.	The ancients said that cautery with gold is better than cautery with iron; and they said this only because of the uniformity of gold and the excellence of its substance; and they said that the cauterized place does not produce pus.
Verum non est illud absolute: quoniam ego iam probaui illud, et inueni, quod ipsum non facit illud in quibusdam corporibus absque aliis.	But this is not absolutely true, for I have already tested it and found that it does not do this in some bodies, while it does in others.
Et cauterium cum ipso est bonum, et melius quam cum ferro, sicut dixerunt.	And cautery with gold is good and better than with iron, as they said.
Verumtamen quando tu ignis cauterium in igne ex auro, non declaratur tibi, quando ignitur secundum quantitatem quam uis, propter rubedinem auri: et quando properat ad ipsum frigus, et si addis super ipsum ignitionem, liquescit in igne, et uertitur in laminas.	Nevertheless, when you heat a gold cautery in the fire, it is not clear to you when it reaches the desired degree of heat because of the redness of the gold; and when it quickly cools, and if you add more heat, it melts in the fire and turns into thin layers.
Quare cadit artifex ex illo in occupationem: et propter illud fit Cauterium cum ferro apud nos uelocius et propinquius, ex rectitudine ad operationem.	Thus, the practitioner is inconvenienced by it, and for this reason, cautery with iron is faster and more accessible for us, being more straightforward in operation.
Et ego quidem iam ordinaui hoc Capitulum in Cauterio, secundum capitula: et ordinaui ea a capite usque ad pedes: ut alleuient quaerenti illud quod uult ex eo.	And I have indeed arranged this chapter on cautery according to topics, and I have ordered them from head to foot, so as to make it easier for the one who seeks what he wants from it.
¶ De modo cauterizandi Caput in commissura coronali.	On the method of cauterizing the head at the coronal suture.
Caput II.	Chapter II.
Confert hic cauterizatio ad dominium humiditatis et frigiditatis super cerebrum: quae duae sunt caussae sodae, et multitudinis fluxus humorum ex capite ad partes oculorum et aurium, et multitudinis somni, et doloris dentium, et doloris gutturis: et uniuersaliter omnis aegritudinis, quae accidit ex frigiditate, sicut paralisis, et sodae, epilepsiae, et apoplexiae, et similia illis ex aegritudinibus.	This cauterization helps to control moisture and coldness over the brain: which are two causes of headaches, and excessive flow of humors from the head to the parts of the eyes and ears, and excess sleep, and tooth pain, and throat pain; and generally all illnesses that arise from cold, such as paralysis, headaches, epilepsy, apoplexy, and similar illnesses.
Modus cauterizationis est, ut praecipias infirmo: ut euacuetur in primis cum medicina solutiua mundificante caput tribus noctibus, aut quatuor, secundum quod exigit uirtus infirmi, et eius aetas, et consuetudo eius.	The method of cauterization is to instruct the patient: first to be purged with a cleansing medicine for the head over three or four nights, according to the strength, age, and usual condition of the patient.
Deinde praecipe ei, ut radi faciat caput suum cum nouacula.	Then instruct him to have his head shaved with a razor.
Deinde fac eum sedere inter manus tuas quadratum, ita quod iam posuit manus suas super pectus suum.	Then make him sit upright between your hands, so that he has already placed his hands on his chest.
Deinde pone radicem palmae tuae super radicem nasi sui inter oculos suos.	Then place the base of your palm on the base of his nose between his eyes.
Vbicumque ergo peruenerit digitus tuus medius, signa locum illum cum incausto.	Wherever your middle finger reaches, mark that place with ink.
Deinde igni Cauterium Oliuare: cuius haec est forma.	Then heat the olive-shaped cautery tool: which has this form.
Deinde pone ipsum super locum signatum cum incausto, descensione, qua constringatur manus tua paulatim, et tu reuolues ipsum.	Then place it on the spot marked with ink, with a downward motion, gradually applying pressure with your hand, and rotate it.
Deinde leua manum tuam uelociter, et aspicias locum.	Then lift your hand quickly, and examine the spot.
Quod si uides iam detegi ex osse quantitatem capitis alkiel, aut quantitatem zeuherbi, tunc eleua manum tuam.	If you see that an area the size of an alkiel seed or a zeuherb seed has been exposed to the bone, then lift your hand.
Et si non, itera manum tuam ferro ipso, aut cum alio ab ipso si infrigidatur: donec uideantur ossa, quae dixi tibi.	If not, repeat the process with the same tool, or another one if it has cooled, until the bones I told you of are visible.
Deinde sume aliquid ex sale, et solue illud in aqua, et imbibe in ipso cotum, et pone super locum: et dimitte ipsum tribus diebus.	Then take some salt, dissolve it in water, soak a piece of cloth in it, and place it on the spot, and leave it there for three days.
Deinde pone super ipsum cotum imbibitum in butyro, et dimitte illum super locum, donec destruatur eschara ignis.	Then place over it a cloth soaked in butter, and leave it on the spot until the burn scab is removed.
Deinde cura illud cum unguento tetrapharmacon, usquequo sanetur.	Then treat it with tetrapharmacon ointment until it heals.
Iam dixerunt, quod quanto uulnus plus remanet apertum, emittit pus: quare est melius et magis iuuatiuum.	They have said that the longer the wound remains open, the more pus it discharges: thus it is better and more beneficial.
Et dixerunt quidam eorum, ut cauterizetur cutis usque ad os: et teneatur Cauterium, donec aduratur quiddam spissitudinis ossis.	Some of them said to cauterize the skin down to the bone: and hold the cautery until some thickness of the bone is burnt.
Deinde radatur post illud quod adustum est ex osse, deinde curetur.	Then shave off the part of the bone that has been burnt, then treat it.
Et dicunt alii, quod oportet ut cum cauterio perueniatur ad ultimum, donec imprimat in os impressionem for tem, ita quod cadat os, quod fit sicut forma kirate: aut instrumentum quod mulieres ponunt infuso alphabeti parui: et extimant, quod exspirantur ex hoc loco uapores capitis, et dimittatur uulnus apertum tempore longo.	Others say that it is necessary to go to the end with the cautery, so that it makes a strong impression on the bone, so that the bone falls away, forming something like the shape of a kirate, or like the instrument women use with small carved letters, believing that the head vapors escape from this spot, and the wound is left open for a long time.
Deinde curetur, donec consolidetur.	Then treat it until it heals.
Mihi autem non uidentur hae duae species Cauterii omnino nisi in quibusdam hominibus secundum uiam timoris.	To me, however, these two forms of cautery do not seem advisable, except in certain people as a last resort.
Et dimittere ipsum, apud me est melius cum salute ipsius.	In my view, leaving it as it is, is better for his well-being.
Debilitatur enim caput ex solutione continuitatis ipsius naturalis: sicut testificati sumus in reliquis membris: et praecipue quando caput infirmi debile est natura: et species quidem Cauterii prima est magis salua et melior apud me, et ea utor: operare ergo cum ea in salute. Et nominatur Clauilis: quoniam forma capitis eius est sicut forma claui, in quo est quiddam curuitatis, et in medio eius est quaedam eminentia parua.	It is called the "Clauilis" because the shape of its head resembles a nail, with a slight curve, and in the middle of it is a small protrusion.
Deinde pone ipsum super locum, et retine manum tuam: et tu reuolue ferrum paulatim cum uelocitate: et sit quantitas quae aduritur ex spissitudine cutis, sicut medietas eius. Et eleua manum tuam, ut non aduratur arteria, quae est inferius, et accidat fluxus sanguinis.	And lift your hand so that the artery beneath is not burned, causing a flow of blood.
Deinde imbibe cotum in aqua salis, et pone super locum, et dimitte ipsum tribus diebus: deinde pone cotum cum butyro: postea cura cum unguento usquequo sanetur. Et ego ueniam cum rememoratione regiminis fluxus sanguinis accidentis ex arteria secundum modum suum, et in loco suo huius libri.	And I will address the regulation of blood flow that may occur from the artery in its own proper method and place in this book.
Quod si uides ex aegritudine, quod non sufficit ei hoc Cauterium, et uides corpus infirmi tolerans cauterium in medio capitis, sicut narrauimus, cura uulnus, donec sanetur.	But if you see from the illness that this cautery is insufficient for him, and you see that the patient’s body can tolerate the cautery in the middle of the head, as we have described, treat the wound until it heals.
Et si uis, administra Cauterium, quod diximus in capitulo extractionis arteriae cum Cauterio habente duos cultellos.	And if you wish, apply the cautery we described in the chapter on artery extraction, using a cautery with two blades.

Cauterium in eo est secundum duos modos: aut cauterium cum igne: aut cauterium cum medicina comburente.	The cautery for this is of two types: either cautery with fire or cautery with a burning medicine.
In cauterio autem cum igne, praecipe infirmo ante illud, ut dimittat pilos suos, si est ex illis qui mundant eos, donec prolongentur et aequentur.	In cauterizing with fire, instruct the patient beforehand to let his hairs grow out, if he is one who trims them, until they are long and even.
Si autem pungunt eum apud ortum suum: tunc constringe oculos eius cum uitta, ut non moueantur, donec nascantur.	But if they prick him at their root, then bind his eyes with a band so that they do not move, until they grow.
Cum ergo nascuntur et aequantur, tunc pone caput infirmi in sinu tuo: deinde signa super Palpebram oculorum eius cum encaustro, signo quod sit secundum figuram folii myrti: et sit principium signi prope pilos.	When they grow out and are even, then place the patient’s head in your lap, then mark on his eyelid with ink in the shape of a myrtle leaf, starting the mark near the hairs.
Deinde pone cotum imbibitum in albumine oui: aut in mucilagine psilii super oculum.	Then place a cloth soaked in egg white or in psyllium mucilage over the eye.
Deinde igni cauterium, cuius forma est haec.	Then heat the cautery, the form of which is this.
Deinde cauteriza super figuram, quam signasti paulatim in uicibus multis, donec comburatur superficies cutis, quae est sicut figura folii myrti, totam apparentem proprie.	Then cauterize along the shape you marked gradually in many strokes, until the surface of the skin, shaped like the myrtle leaf, is properly burned entirely.
Et signum sanitatis operis tui est: ut si uideas Palpebram oculi iam contrahi, et pilos eleuari apud puncturam oculi.	And the sign of success in your work is if you see the eyelid of the eye begin to contract and the hairs lift away from the puncture of the eye.
Eleua ergo manum tuam tunc: et dimitte ipsum tribus diebus.	Then lift your hand and leave it alone for three days.
Deinde pone super ipsum Cotum cum butyro, donec eradicetur eschara.	Then place a cloth with butter on it until the scab is removed.
Deinde cura ipsum cum unguento, donec sanetur.	Then treat it with ointment until it is healed.
Quod si redeat aliquod ex pilis post horam, et mollificetur palpebra: tunc itera Cauterium super illum locum, sicut fecisti in primis.	But if some hairs return after a while, and the eyelid softens, then repeat the cauterization on that spot as you did the first time.
Si autem pili sunt in inferiori: tunc cauteriza ipsam, donec eleuetur ad locum suum naturalem: et aequetur ut non pungant pili oculum.	But if the hairs are in the lower part, then cauterize it until it is lifted to its natural position and leveled so that the hairs no longer prick the eye.
¶ De modo cauterizandi cum medicina comburente in eodem casu.	On the method of cauterizing with a burning medicine in the same case.
Caput XVIII.	Chapter 18.
Est ut praecipias infirmo, ut dimittat pilos, donec prolongentur et aequentur.	Instruct the patient to let his hairs grow until they are long and even.
Fac ex charta de panno formam folii myrti.	Make a myrtle-leaf shape from a cloth paper.
Deinde accipe ex sapone cognito, et ex calce non imbibita ex unoquoque pondus uncia I aut quasi illud: deinde tere ea simul contritione bona, et fac illud cum festinatione, ut non infrigidetur.	Then take a known soap and unslaked lime, each in a weight of one ounce or close to that; then grind them together thoroughly, and do this quickly so it does not cool.
Deinde ex pande ex eo super chartam quam fecisti sicut formam folii myrti, et pone ipsum super palpebram oculi unius, aut duorum.	Then spread this mixture over the paper you made in the shape of a myrtle leaf, and place it on the eyelid of one or both eyes.
Et pone sub oculo cotum imbibitum in albumine oui: et caput infirmi sit in sinu tuo.	And place a cloth soaked in egg white under the eye, and the patient's head should rest in your lap.
Et pone digitum tuum indicem super medicinam, et preme ipsum parumper.	Place your index finger on the medicine and press it gently.
Et tu moueas ipsum quotiens sentit infirmus mordicationem medicinae: quoniam inuenit eius mordicatio sicut ignis.	Move it each time the patient feels the medicine’s stinging, as he perceives its sting like fire.
Dum ergo perseuerat hoc quod ipse inuenit mordicatio, tunc dimitte medicinam, et moue ipsam cum digito tuo.	As long as he continues to feel the sting, leave the medicine in place and move it with your finger.
Cum ergo quiescit mordicatio, tunc remoue medicinam, et ablue oculum cum aqua.	When the stinging stops, remove the medicine and wash the eye with water.
Et considera tunc, si uides palpebram eleuari, sicut eleuatur apud contractionem cum igne, aut cum incisione, est bene: et si non, tunc itera super ipsam ex medicina super locum, in quo non imprimit medicina, et non denigrat, donec aequetur operatio tua, et contrahatur oculus.	Then observe if the eyelid rises, as it would from contraction by fire or incision; if so, it is good. If not, reapply the medicine to the area where it did not take effect and did not darken, until your work is balanced and the eye contracts.
Deinde pone super ipsum Cotum cum butyro, donec eradicetur cutis adustionis.	Then place a cloth with butter over it until the burned skin is removed.
Deinde cura ipsum cum unguento diaphinicon, aut alio, donec sanetur.	Then treat it with diaphinicon ointment or another ointment until it heals.
Et necesse est tibi apud operationem, ut custodias ultima custodia, ne in oculum cadat aliquid ex medicina.	During the procedure, you must exercise utmost care to ensure that none of the medicine falls into the eye.
Quod si mollificatur palpebra post dies, et pugnat ex capillis aliquid in oculo, tunc itera medicinam super illum locum, qui mollificatur ex palpebra proprie, sicut fecisti in primis.	If the eyelid softens after a few days and any hairs prick the eye, then repeat the medicine on the specific part of the eyelid that softened, as you did at first.
Deinde cura ipsum, donec sanetur.	Then treat it until it heals.
Et haec est forma folii myrti.	And this is the shape of the myrtle leaf.
Et scias, quod oculi hominum diuersificantur in magnitudine, et paruitate.	Know that human eyes vary in size and smallness.
Ergo secundum illud sit consideratio tua: et non occultatur uia rectitudinis illi, cui est studium de hac arte.	Therefore, let your consideration be according to this, for the way of righteousness is not hidden from one dedicated to this art.
¶ De modo cauterizandi in Fistula lachrymali.	On the method of cauterizing in the case of a tear duct fistula.
Caput XIX.	Chapter 19.
Quando curas Fistulam, cum eo quod diximus in diuisionibus aegritudinum, et non confert curatio tua: tunc, oportet ut cauterizes ipsam secundum hunc modum.	When you treat a fistula using what we discussed in the divisions of diseases and your treatment is ineffective, then you should cauterize it according to this method.
Praecipe infirmo, ut ponat caput suum in sinu tuo, et teneat caput eius minister inter manus suas taliter, ut non moueatur, neque agitetur cum capite suo.	Instruct the patient to place his head in your lap, and have an assistant hold his head in his hands so that it remains still and does not move.
Deinde pone Cotum infusum in albugine oui, aut in mucilagine psilii super oculum eius: deinde igni Cauterium, cuius forma est haec.	Then place a cloth soaked in egg white or in a psillium mucilage over his eye; next, heat the cautery, which has this form.
Sit concauum, sicut forma cannae pennae uulturis ab extremitate una, cum qua fit Cauterium: et si uis sit penetrans usque ad extremitatem aliam, et si uis sit sursum sic tenta.	It should be concave, like the shape of the tip of a vulture’s feather on one end, with which the cauterization is performed; and if you wish, it can be penetrating to the other end, and, if you prefer, it should be raised in this way.
Verumtamen hoc concauum est melius ad opus tuum.	However, this concave form is better for your work.
Deinde exprime fistulam, si est aperta, et extrahe ex ea saniem, et exicca ipsam: et si non est aperta, tunc aperi eam, et extrahe pus eius.	Then squeeze the fistula, if it is open, to drain the fluid, and dry it; if it is not open, then open it and extract the pus.
Deinde pone tunc super eam Cauterium, quod fit ignitum ualde.	Then place on it the cautery, which has been heated very much.
Et tene cum eo manum tuam parumper apud Cauterium ab oculo, ad partem nasi, ut non erret manus tua, aut inquietetur infirmus: et ponas Cauterium in adipe oculi, et corrumpas ipsum.	And hold your hand steady on the cautery, positioned away from the eye towards the nose, so that your hand does not slip or the patient become disturbed; place the cautery on the fatty tissue of the eye and destroy it.
Si ergo peruenis in principio Cauterii tui usque ad os, erit bene.	If with the beginning of your cauterization you reach the bone, it will be good.
Et si non: tunc itera Cauterium semel, aut bis, si indiges illo: et dimitte illud tribus diebus.	If not, then repeat the cauterization once or twice as needed, and leave it for three days.
Deinde pone Cotum cum butyro, et cura ipsum cum unguento exiccatiuo, donec sanetur.	Then apply a cloth with butter and treat it with a drying ointment until it heals.
Quod si praeterierint ei quadraginta dies, et non sanatur: tunc pone super ipsum medicinam acutam corrosiuam, donec detegatur os: et rade ipsum secundum quod uenit in capitulo suo.	If forty days pass and it does not heal, then apply a sharp, corrosive medicine until the bone is exposed, and scrape it as described in the relevant chapter.
Et est modus alius Cauterii fistulae, quem quidam primi dixerunt.	There is another method of cauterizing a fistula that some early practitioners have described.
Intende ad locum fistulae, et finde ipsum: deinde pone in ipsam fissuram camahan subtile, id est, Cannulam subtilem, cuius forma haec est.	Direct your attention to the location of the fistula, split it open, and place a fine rod, that is, a thin tube, into the fissure, shaped like this.
Et funde in ipsum quantitatem drachmae ex plumbo liquefacto, et tene manum tuam cum Canna retentione bona, et non moueatur infirmus omnino, ut non currat, uel perueniat plumbum liquefactum ad oculum eius.	Pour into it an amount of a drachma of molten lead, holding your hand steady on the tube with a good grip, and ensure the patient does not move at all, so that the lead does not flow or reach his eye.
Et oportet ut ponas super oculum infirmi cotum infusum in albumine oui, aut in aqua.	It is essential to place a cloth soaked in egg white or water over the patient’s eye.
Plumbum enim comburit locum fistulae, et sanat ipsam sanatione mirabili.	The lead burns the site of the fistula and heals it with remarkable efficacy.
Quod si sanatur fistula, cum eo quod diximus ex Cauterio, et curatione, est bonum.	If the fistula heals with the cautery and treatment as we described, it is good.
Et si non, tunc necesse est administrare foramen nasi, et reducere fistulam ad cursum nasi, secundum quod ueniet in loco suo sibi proprio.	If not, then it is necessary to create an opening in the nose and redirect the fistula towards the nasal passage, as it naturally should be.

Fac illud aliquotiens, nam dolor remouebitur, aut in illo eodem die, aut die alio.	Repeat this several times; the pain will dissipate either that day or the next.
Et oportet in impressione illius Cauterii, ut impleat infirmus os suum buyro bono, et teneat ipsum hora una: deinde exspuat ipsum.	After applying the cautery, have the patient fill his mouth with good butter, hold it there for an hour, and then spit it out.

Et ciba infirmum omni cibo sicco paucae humiditatis, usquequo confortetur membrum, et sanetur.	And feed the patient only dry food with little moisture, until the part is strengthened and healed.
Et modus alius incisionis est, ut consideres ubi apparet magnitudo apostematis, et collectio aquae.	And another method of incision is to observe where the swelling is most prominent and where the water has collected.
Quoniam quandoque est in occipitio plus, aut in sincipite, aut in dextra, aut in sinistra.	For sometimes it is more in the back of the head, or in the forehead, or on the right side, or on the left side.
Ascende ergo cum incisione ubi apparet tibi apostema et impletio aquae: et scinde illud secundum quod tibi possibile est.	Then make the incision where the swelling and fullness of water appear to you: and cut it as far as is possible for you.
Et obserua, ne incidas arteriam, et accidat fluxus sanguinis, et moriatur infirmus ex illo fluxu cum euacuatione humiditatis.	And be careful not to cut an artery, and a flow of blood occurs, and the patient dies from that flow along with the evacuation of moisture.
¶ De incisione Arteriarum quae sunt post aures.	On the incision of the Arteries which are behind the ears.
Caput II.	Chapter II.
Quando accidunt alicui catarri ad oculos, aut ad pectus, et antiquatur illud, et non confert curatio medicinarum: tunc magis ultima curatio est in illo, incisio harum duarum arteriarum.	When a person suffers from cataracts affecting the eyes or chest, and it becomes chronic, and medical treatment does not help: then the ultimate treatment is the incision of these two arteries.
Oportet ergo quando uis incidere eas, ut radat infirmus caput suum.	Therefore, when you wish to incise them, the patient must shave his head.
Deinde frica locum cum panno aspero, ut appareant arteriae.	Then rub the area with a rough cloth, so that the arteries become visible.
Deinde stringat infirmus collum suum, cum quolibet panno suo.	Then the patient should bind his neck with any cloth he has.
Deinde considera, ubi pulset uena.	Then observe where the vein pulses.
Et loca utriusque sunt loca quae sunt post aures depressa: et raro occultatur, nisi in quibusdam hominibus.	And the locations of each are the depressed areas behind the ears: and it is rarely hidden, except in certain people.
Deinde signa cum encaustro super utraque: et incide utramque cum spatumili utrimque acuro, incisione perueniente ad os: et fiat illud per latitudinem capitis.	Then mark both with ink: and incise each one with a spatula sharpened on both sides, cutting down to the bone: and let it be done across the width of the head.
Et si uis, intromitte spatumile sub arteria, et incide eam ad superiora cum incisione.	And if you wish, insert the spatula under the artery, and cut it upward with the incision.
Et sit longitudo incisionis quasi duo digiti iuncti.	And let the length of the incision be about two fingers joined together.
Nam quando inciditur uena, egreditur sanguis egressione pulsatili, qui salit ad interiora saltu frequenti.	For when the vein is cut, blood exits in a pulsating flow, which leaps inward with frequent spurting.
Si autem arteria non apparet sensui, tunc oportet ut mensures ab aure quantitatem spacii trium digitorum.	But if the artery is not perceptible to the touch, then you must measure from the ear the space of three fingers.
Deinde signa cum encaustro, et finde usque ad os.	Then mark with ink, and cut down to the bone.
Et illud quod oportet emittere ex sanguine, est VI uncia et fortasse emittis ex eo plus, aut minus.	And the amount of blood that should be let out is six ounces, though perhaps you may release more or less of it.
Totum illud secundum quantitatem quae apparet tibi ex uirute infirmi et repletione arteriae eius.	All of this is according to the quantity you perceive from the strength of the patient and the fullness of his artery.
Deinde considera ad uulnus.	Then examine the wound.
Tunc si remanet ad os ex sifac aliquid, tunc incide illud, ut non accidat apostema calidum: tunc stringe uulnus cum liciniis ex panno lini.	Then if any membrane remains on the bone, cut it so that a hot swelling does not occur: then bind the wound with strips of linen cloth.
Deinde cura ipsum cum unguento, donec sanetur.	Then treat it with ointment until it heals.
¶ De extractione arteriarum, quae sunt in timporibus.	On the extraction of the arteries that are in the temples.
Caput III.	Chapter III.
Quando accidit homini hemicranea antiqua, aut catarri acuti propter humiditates acrimoniales, et caliditate in lacertis timporum, aut soda antiqua, uehemens, et similia illis: et medicatur cum modis curationis medicinae, et non confert illud: tunc experti sumus in his aegritudinibus extractionem arteriarum ex timporibus, aut cauterizationem earum, sicut narrauimus.	When a person suffers from chronic hemicrania (migraine), or acute catarrh due to sharp humors, and there is warmth in the muscles of the temples, or they have old and severe conditions like this, and medical treatments do not bring relief: then we have tried in such cases either the extraction of the arteries from the temples or their cauterization, as we have previously described.
Et modus operationis in extractione earum, est, ut radat infirmus pilos qui sunt in timporibus, et intendat ad arteriam apparentem in timpore.	The procedure for extracting them is as follows: the patient should shave the hair on the temples, then focus on the artery that appears in the temple.
Ipsa namque ostenditur tibi ex pulsu suo, et raro occultatur nisi in paucis hominibus, aut uehementia frigoris.	This artery will show itself to you by its pulse, and it is rarely hidden except in a few people or due to extreme cold.
Quod si occultatur tibi, tunc stringat infirmus caput suum cum quolibet panno suo: deinde frica locum cum panno aspero.	If it is hidden, then the patient should bind their head with any cloth available, and then rub the area with a rough cloth.
Aut uapora locum cum aqua calida, donec appareat tibi apparitione manifesta.	Or, steam the area with warm water until it becomes clearly visible.
Deinde accipe spatumile, quod paulo ante formaui.	Then take the spatula that I have previously described.
Deinde excoria cum eo cutem cum facilitate, donec peruenias ad arteriam.	Then peel back the skin gently with it until you reach the artery.
Deinde proiice in eam uncinum, et extrahe eam ad superiora, donec extrahas eam ex cute, et facias eam euadere ex tunicis quae sunt sub ea ab omni parte.	Next, insert a hook into it and pull it upwards, lifting it out of the skin, and free it from the membranes surrounding it on all sides.
Si autem arteria est subtilis: tunc inuolue eam cum extremitate uncini.	If the artery is thin, then wrap it with the end of the hook.
Deinde incide ex ea partem secundum quantitatem qua elongentur extremitates eius, et contrahantur, et non accidat fluxus sanguinis.	Then cut off a portion of it to the extent that the ends will retract and contract, and blood flow does not occur.
Nam si non inciditur, et si non abscinditur, non ascendit sanguis penitus.	For if it is not incised and if it is not cut off, blood will not fully drain.
Deinde euacua ex sanguine ana VI unci, usque ad .III.	Then evacuate from it about six ounces of blood, down to three.
Quod si arteria est magna: tunc oportet ut liges eam in duobus locis ex filo duplo, forti, ex serico: aut ex chordis alhohod, ut non properet ad ipsam putrefactio ante consolidationem uulneris, et accidat fluxus sanguinis.	If the artery is large, then you should tie it in two places with a strong double thread, either silk or cotton threads, so that decay does not reach it before the wound heals, and blood does not flow.
Deinde incide superfluitatem, quae est inter duo ligamenta.	Then cut the excess portion that lies between the two ligatures.
Fac illud in illa hora, aut post horam aliam.	Do this at that moment, or an hour later.
Et si uis, cauteriza eam cauterio usque ad os, cum cauterio cultellari: donec incidantur extremitates eius, et stabit loco eiusdem operis.	And if you wish, cauterize it down to the bone with a knife-shaped cautery iron: until the ends are seared, which will hold in place by the same procedure.
Et est melius opus, nisi si infirmus habet febrem, aut est calefactae complexionis.	This is a better procedure, unless the patient has a fever or a hot temperament.
Quoniam cauterium est ex eis, quae iuuant ad finiendas humiditates.	For the cautery is among those things that help to stop moistures.
Ergo est melius iuuamentum.	Therefore, it is the better assistance.
Et oportet post extractionem arteriarum, ut impleatur locus cotto antiquo: et ponantur super ipsum puluilli decenter facti.	And after the extraction of the arteries, the site should be filled with old cotton, and neatly made compresses should be placed over it.
Et post solutionem curetur cum medicinis siccis, quae faciunt nasci carnem, et cum liciniis, donec sanetur.	And after the dressing is removed, it should be treated with dry medicines that promote flesh growth, and with linen cloths, until it heals.
Quod si interim dum operaris accidit fluxus sanguinis ex arteria, tunc propera ad abscindendum eum cum cauterio, aut ut impleas locum cum zegi: et stringe manum tuam, donec abscindatur sanguis.	If during the operation a flow of blood occurs from the artery, quickly stop it with a cautery or fill the area with lint; press your hand until the bleeding stops.
Quod si nihil ex illis est tibi praesens, tunc pone super locum digitum tuum, donec congeletur sanguis.	If none of these are at hand, place your finger over the area until the blood clots.
Et embroca locum cum aqua uehementis frigoris, donec quiescat acuitas, et stringatur secundum quod oportet.	Then bathe the area with very cold water until the intensity subsides, and bind it as needed.
Et ex eo quod est leuius et facilius extractione arteriae, est, ut cauterizes uenam cum hoc cauterio habente duos cultellos: sitque acuitas duorum cultellorum similis almagdatem.	And a simpler and easier alternative to artery extraction is to cauterize the vein with this cautery, which has two blades: the sharpness of both blades should be like almagdatem (almagda stone).
Verumtamen sunt multo minoris acuitatis, quam duo cultelli.	However, they are far less sharp than two knives.
Quoniam si sunt acutae, sicut duo cultelli, properat utrisque frigus: et non incidunt carnem cum uelocitate.	For if they are as sharp as two knives, both become cold quickly and do not cut flesh swiftly.
Cum ergo in eis est aliquantulum grossitudinis, tenetur in eis calor ignis, et abscinditur caro uelociter: cum hoc ergo opus est melius omni opere, et leuius, et facilius.	Since they have some thickness, they retain the heat of the fire and cut flesh swiftly; therefore, this method is better, lighter, and easier than any other.
Et sit spacium quod est inter duos cultellos quantitas grossitudinis digiti, postquam tu signas duo loca cum encaustro: et depone utrosque ignitos ualde, donec perueniant ad os, et incidant uenam in duobus locis: ut elongetur quod est inter extremitates eius.	Let the space between the two blades be about the thickness of a finger, after marking two spots with ink: then place both very hot blades until they reach the bone, cutting the vein in two places, so that it lengthens between the ends.
Nam non consolidantur cum hoc cauterio penitus.	For they do not heal completely with this cautery.
Et haec est forma cauterii.	And this is the shape of the cautery.
¶ De cura cursus lachrymarum acutarum assiduarum ab oculis.	On the treatment of continuous, sharp tears from the eyes.
Caput IIII.	Chapter IV.
Quando sunt lachrymae assiduae, et est cursus earum ex uenis, quae sunt super craneum capitis exterius: et non confert in eis aliquid curationis medicinae cum medicinis, et uides faciem infirmi iam rubere, et sentit in fronte sua deambulationem, sicut est deambulatio formicarum: et oculi eius sunt extenuati, humidi, ita quod iam corrosi sunt pili eorum cum corrosione: et calefactae sunt palpebrae eius ex acuitate lachrymarum: tunc antiqui indiguerunt in curatione earum hac operatione, et denominauerunt eam curationem, cum Ense.	When there are continuous tears, and their flow comes from the veins that are on the outside of the skull of the head: and no medical treatment is effective with medicines, and you see the face of the patient already reddened, and he feels movement in his forehead, like the movement of ants: and his eyes are weakened, moist, so that the hairs of them are already corroded with erosion: and his eyelids are heated from the sharpness of the tears: then the ancients used this procedure in their treatment, and they called it the treatment "with the Knife."
Et est, ut praecipias infirmo radere pilos qui sunt in fronte sua.	And it is, that you instruct the patient to shave the hairs that are on his forehead.
Deinde incide in fronte tres fissuras aequedistantes secundum longitudinem frontis: et sit longitudo fissurae quasi duo digiti, et fissura una sit aequedistans longitudini nasi in medio frontis, et secunda secundum longitudinem parumper a motu lacertorum, et tertia a parte altera: et caue ab incisione arteriarum quae sunt in duobus lateribus: et elonga manum tuam a continuatione duarum mandibularum, et sit spacium omnis incisionis quantitas trium digitorum coniunctorum: et sit tibi frustum spongiae praeparatum, aut panni siccati multi, cum quibus exsicces sanguinem.	Then cut three parallel incisions in the forehead along the length of the forehead: and let the length of each incision be about two fingers, and let one incision be aligned with the length of the nose in the middle of the forehead, and the second along the length slightly away from the movement of the muscles, and the third on the other side: and beware of cutting the arteries that are on both sides: and keep your hand away from the connection of the two jaws, and let the space of each incision be the width of three joined fingers: and have a piece of sponge prepared for yourself, or many dry cloths, with which you may dry the blood.
Deinde intromitte spatumile duarum acutarum extremitatum, cuius mox sequens est forma.	Then insert the "spatumile" with two sharp ends, whose form follows immediately.
Ab incisione quae est a parte timporis, usque ad incisionem secundam: et excoria cum eo totam cutem, quae est inter duas incisiones, cum sifac, quod est super os.	From the incision that is on the side of the temple, up to the second incision: and strip with it all the skin that is between the two incisions, with a tool, which is above the bone.
Deinde fac illud etiam ab incisione media usque ad incisionem secundam.	Then do the same also from the middle incision up to the second incision.
Deinde extrahe hoc spatumile, et intromitte in incisionem primam iterum instrumentum aliud, quod nominatur cultellare acutum, ab una parte lene, non acutum ab altera parte.	Then remove this "spatumile," and insert into the first incision again another instrument, which is called the "sharp cutter," sharp on one side, not sharp on the other side.
Et haec est forma eius.	And this is its form.
Et pone latus eius acutum uersus carnem annexam cum cute, et latus eius lene uersus os: et impelle ipsum, donec perueniat ad fissuram mediam: et abscinde cum eo omnia uasa, quae descendunt a capite ad oculos: praeter quod continuetur incisio usque ad illud quod apparet de cute.	And place its sharp side toward the flesh attached to the skin, and its smooth side toward the bone: and push it until it reaches the middle incision: and cut with it all the vessels that descend from the head to the eyes: except that the incision should continue up to what is visible of the skin.
Deinde fac illud in fissura media usque ad fissuram aliam.	Then do the same in the middle incision up to the other incision.
Et postquam currit ex sanguine quantitas aequalis, exprime loca ex frustis sanguinis coagulati.	And after an equal quantity of blood flows, squeeze the places of the clots of coagulated blood.
Deinde pone in omnem fissuram licinium ex cotto ueteri, et pone super eos puluillos imbibitos in uino, et oleo, aut aceto.	Then place in each incision a strip of old cotton, and place over them cushions soaked in wine, oil, or vinegar.
Et uide ut non accidat apostema calidum.	And see to it that no hot swelling occurs.
Et in die tertia solue ligamentum, et administra infusionem multam cum aqua tepida.	And on the third day, loosen the bandage, and apply a generous infusion with warm water.
Deinde cura illud ex unguento basilicon, et reliquis quibus curantur uulnera: usquequo sanetur.	Then treat it with basilicon ointment and the other things by which wounds are treated: until it heals.
¶ De cura lachrymarum acutarum et catarrorum ad oculos ex interioribus capitis.	On the treatment of sharp tears and catarrhs to the eyes from the inner parts of the head.
Caput V.	Chapter V.
Quando accidunt alicui catarri acuti, acres, assidui: et uides oculos eius extenuatos, paruos, et iam debilitatus est aspectus eorum: et palpebrae ulceratae, et cadunt pili ex eis, et in profundo capitis est dolor acutus, laedens, et sternutatio consequens: tunc sume ex istis accidentibus, quod illae materiae, et catarri non ueniunt, nisi ex uenis multis.	When sharp, intense, continuous catarrhs happen to someone: and you see his eyes weakened, small, and already their vision has been impaired: and the eyelids are ulcerated, and hairs fall from them, and in the depth of the head there is a sharp, damaging pain, and subsequent sneezing: then you may conclude from these symptoms, that these matters, and catarrhs, come only from many veins.
Et melior curatio in eis est haec.	And the best treatment for them is this.
Et est, ut praecipias infirmo radere frontem suam.	And it is, that you instruct the patient to shave his forehead.
Deinde incide in eam fissuram unam in medio frontis, aut alterius parumper per latitudinem, et incipe cum fissura a timpore sinistro ad timpus dextrum: et sit incisio usque ad os: et elonga manum tuam a lacertis timporum, qui mouentur apud masticationem: donec quando detegitur os: et exsiccabis totum sanguinem cum spongia.	Then make a single incision in the middle of the forehead, or slightly on one side, across the width, and begin the incision from the left temple to the right temple: and let the incision go down to the bone: and keep your hand away from the muscles of the temples, which move during chewing: until when the bone is revealed: and you will dry all the blood with a sponge.
Tunc separa inter duo labia fissurae cum cotto ueteri, aut cum liciniis ex lino.	Then separate the two edges of the incision with old cotton, or with linen strips.
Deinde stringe desuper cum puluillis: et imbibe puluillos cum uino et oleo, ut non accidat apostema calidum.	Then compress from above with cushions: and soak the cushions with wine and oil, so that hot swelling does not occur.
Et quando soluis illud, et uides quod apostema calidum iam imminuitur: tunc oportet ut frices os, donec incipiat in eo nasci caro.	And when you loosen it, and you see that the hot swelling is already reduced: then it is necessary to rub the bone, until flesh begins to grow on it.
