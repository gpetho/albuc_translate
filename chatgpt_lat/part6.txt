¶ De restauratione fracturae ossium, quando est cum vulnere.	On the Restoration of Bone Fractures When There is Also a Wound
Caput XX.	Chapter 20.
Conuenit ei cui accidit fractura cum uulnere, praecipue si est magnum, sicut os coxae, aut adiutorii, aut simile illi, ut properes, et phlebotomes ipsum statim: si attestantur tibi conditiones phlebotomiae, sicut praemisimus.	If someone experiences a fracture with a wound, especially if it is severe, like in the hip bone or its support, or something similar, you should act quickly and immediately perform bloodletting if the conditions for bloodletting are present, as previously mentioned.
Quod si uulnus effluit sanguinem, tunc oportet, ut properes ad abscisionem eius, ita ut puluerizes super ipsum zegi tritum: si non est tibi praesens aliud.	If the wound is bleeding, then you must proceed to cut it and apply powdered dried sage over it, if nothing else is available.
Deinde incipe restaurare fracturam in illo eodem die: et non postponas illud, si non accidit apostema.	Then begin to repair the fracture on the same day; do not delay unless an abscess develops.
Si uero accidit apostema calidum, tunc dimitte restaurationem eius usque ad diem nonum, donec sedetur apostema.	But if a hot abscess forms, then postpone the repair until the ninth day, until the abscess subsides.
Et non appropinques ei in die tertio, et quarto omnino, nam tu faceres ei accidere accidentia mala.	Do not approach it at all on the third and fourth days, for you could cause harmful complications.
Si autem os fractum est eminens super cutem detectum: tunc oportet, ut coneris reducere ipsum, et aequare ipsum manibus tuis cum facilitate et extensione parua.	If the broken bone is protruding from the exposed skin, then you must attempt to realign it and level it with your hands with gentle pressure and slight extension.
Si autem non praeparatur extensio eius, et ipsius aequatio manibus tuis: tunc reduc ipsum cum isto instrumento.	If alignment and extension with your hands cannot be prepared, then realign it with this instrument.
Et est instrumentum quod fit ex ferro, cuius longitudo est quantitas septem digitorum, aut octo: et eius latitudo est secundum quantitatem uulneris.	The instrument is made of iron, with a length of about seven or eight fingers, and its width corresponds to the size of the wound.
Et propter illud necesse est medico, ut accipiat ex eis tria, aut quatuor secundum quantitatem qua indiget ad illud ex curatione in omni specie fracturae.	Because of this, the physician needs to have three or four of them, depending on the amount required for treating any type of fracture.
Et sit rotundum: et sit in eo grossitudo parua, ut non duplicetur apud compressionem super ipsum in hora operationis: et sit acutae extremitatis, cui sit margo in extremitatibus suis.	It should be round, with a slight thickness, so that it does not compress under pressure during the procedure; it should have a sharp end, with an edge at its tip.
Et sit superius eius ad grossitudinem ex medietate eius usque ad inferiora.	The upper part should have thickness from the middle to the bottom.
Et haec est forma eius.	And this is its shape.
Oportet in Hac Operatione, ut ponatur extremitas eius acuta curuata super extremitatem ossis eminentis: et impelle ipsum cum eo uice una, donec quando redit os et aequatur quadam aequatione.	In this procedure, the sharp, curved tip should be placed over the protruding bone end and pushed once, until the bone returns and is somewhat aligned.
Conare ergo aequare extremitates fracturae adinuicem.	Try, therefore, to align the ends of the fracture with each other.
Quod si extremitates eius fracta est subtilis, et non accipit eam instrumentum accaeptione bona, tunc opotet, ut incidas extremitatem illius ossis, donec instrumentum potestatem habeat eius.	If the ends of the fracture are delicate and the instrument does not hold it well, then you should cut the end of that bone until the instrument can hold it.
Si uero non potes reducere eos cum eo quod narrauimus omnino, tunc incide ipsum cum eo quod conuenit ei ex Incisoriis quae diximus: aut serra ipsum cum una serrarum quocunque modo possibile est tibi.	If you cannot realign it completely with the method we described, then cut it with the appropriate tool we mentioned or saw it with one of the saws in whatever way is possible for you.
Deinde rade illud quod remanet in osse ex asperitate et corticibus minutis.	Then scrape off any roughness and small splinters remaining on the bone.
Cum ergo reducitur os, et inuenit infirmus post reductionem eius dolorem uehementem laedentem, tunc scias, quod os non rediit ad locum suum naturalem.	When the bone is realigned, and the patient experiences intense, piercing pain afterward, know that the bone has not returned to its natural position.
Fac ergo illud.	Do it, therefore.
Nam tu confers infirmo iuuamentum magnum, cum completur restauratio tua quam facis ossi.	For you provide great relief to the patient when the restoration you perform on the bone is complete.
Tunc submerge pannum in uino stiptico, nigro.	Then soak a cloth in black astringent wine.
Et proprie si est in aestate.	Especially if it is summer.
Et non ponas super uulnus caerotum, neque aliquid in quo sit oleum: ut non faciat accidere in ipso putredinem et corruptionem.	Do not place a poultice or anything containing oil on the wound, to avoid causing rot and decay in it.
Deinde administra astellas in hora complementi tui a restauratione ossis.	Then apply splints at the moment you finish the bone restoration.
Et dimitte uulnus detectum: ita, ut incidas cum forficibus foramen in inuolutionibus secundum quantitatem uulneris.	Leave the wound exposed, making an opening in the bandages with scissors to match the size of the wound.
Et caue omni cautela, ne stringas uulnus cum fractura.	Take every precaution not to tighten the wound with the fracture.
Multotiens enim fecerunt istud stolidi medici: et fecerunt accidere super infirmos suos aut mortem, aut corrosionem, aut zuchem, id est, saniem.	For foolish doctors have often done this and caused death, erosion, or “zuchem” (that is, discharge) in their patients.
Et sit strictura tua mollis, lenis, diuersa stricturae fracturarum reliquarum.	Let your binding be soft and gentle, different from the usual binding for fractures.
Quod si uulnus est malum, aut est uulnus magnum, et times super ipsum quaedam accidentia mala quae narrauimus, et inuenis dolorem in loco suspensum: tunc non oportet, ut ponas super ipsum astellas.	If the wound is severe or large and you fear certain bad complications we have described, and there is pain in the area, then you should not place splints over it.
Et fac eis inuolutiones ex pannis duris in loco astellarum, et stringe ipsum cum eis.	Instead, make bandages from firm cloth in place of splints, and bind it with them.
Cum autem est post diem unum, aut dies duos, et uides uulnus iam incipere, ut in eo generetur pus: tunc aufer desuper ipsum pannum quem posuisti super ipsum cum uino.	After one or two days, if you see that pus is starting to form in the wound, then remove the cloth you placed over it with the wine.
Deinde administra licinia, et unguenta cum quibus de consuetudine nostra est, ut medicemur uulnera: sicut unguentum tetrapharmacon, et similia illi.	Then apply linen bandages and ointments that we customarily use to treat wounds, such as tetrapharmacon ointment and similar ones.
Et conuenit tibi, ut soluas ligamentum.	And it is advisable to loosen the bandage.
Et considera uulnus in omni die mane et uespere, donec consolidetur, et sanetur.	Observe the wound every day, morning and evening, until it stabilizes and heals.
Et oportet, ut praeparetur membrum taliter, ut currat ex eo pus ad inferiora cum facilitate.	The limb should be positioned so that the pus can flow downward easily.
Quod si praetereunt uulneri dies multi, et non consolidatur, neque abscinditur pus ab eo: tunc scias, quod illic sunt satagie ossis parua.	If many days pass and the wound does not heal, nor does the pus cease, then you should know that there are small bone fragments within.
Quare oportet, ut inquiras uulnus cum tenta.	Therefore, it is necessary to probe the wound with a probe.
Quot uero ex illis alsatagie sunt separata, remoue ea, et extrahe ipsa.	Remove and extract any of those bone fragments that are loose.
Et quae ex eis sunt non separata, et pungunt membrum, et faciunt accidere dolorem, conare incidere ea, et auferre ea omni modo quo possibile est tibi illud.	For those that are not loose but pierce the tissue and cause pain, try to cut and remove them by any means possible.
Quod si accidat uulneri zuchen, aut ekileten, id est, estiomenus, aut species alia corruptionis et putrefactionis: tunc oportet, ut opponas unicuique ex eis cum eo quod conuenit ei ex curatione, cuius praecessit narratio in capitulo suo.	If the wound develops zuchen, ekileten (that is, necrosis), or any other form of rot or decay, then you must treat each of these conditions with the appropriate remedy, as previously discussed in its respective chapter.
Et ex eo in quo oportet ut stet sermo meus, et cui facias praesentem mentem tuam quando frangitur os, et eminet super membrum, sicut os coxae, et adiutorii, et similia eis ex membris magnis, est, ut non praesumas attractionem eius, multotiens enim accidit ex illo mors, imo dimitte ipsum, donec putrefiat.	What I must emphasize, and what you should bear in mind when a bone breaks and protrudes over a limb, such as the hip bone or its support, or other large bones, is not to attempt to pull it; often this causes death. Instead, leave it until it decays.
Fortasse enim cadet per se post uiginti dies, aut triginta.	It may indeed fall off by itself after twenty or thirty days.
Tunc ergo cura uulnus, si uidetur tibi: et sinon, dimitte ipsum.	Then treat the wound if you see fit; if not, leave it alone.
¶ De curatione Nodationis in uestigio fracturae.	On the treatment of knotting in the scar of a fracture.
Caput XXI.	Chapter 21.
Multoties accidit haec Nodatio in uestigio sanationis fracturae.	This knotting often occurs in the scar of a healed fracture.
Praecipue, quae est prope iuncturas: et foedatur ex ea figura membri.	Especially when near the joints, it distorts the shape of the limb.
Et fortasse prohibet membrum ab operatione sua naturali.	It may even prevent the limb from functioning naturally.
Considera ergo tunc, si nodatio est recens: administra in ea medicinas quae stipticitatem habent, sicut aloe, et olibanum, et myrrha, et sarcocolla, et acacia, et similia eis.	Therefore, if the knot is recent, apply medicines with astringent properties, such as aloe, frankincense, myrrh, sarcocolla, acacia, and similar substances.
Ita, ut accipias ex istis quasdam, aut omnes eas: et confice eas cum uino stiptico, aut cum albumine oui, aut cum aceto: et pone eas super nodationem in stuppa, et stringe eas super ipsam strictura bona.	Take some or all of these, mix them with astringent wine, egg white, or vinegar, and apply them over the knot in tow, binding them tightly.
Et dimitte stricturam, nec soluas eam diebus multis.	Leave the binding on and do not loosen it for many days.
Deinde solue eam, et itera aliam, donec remoueatur nodatio.	Then loosen it, reapply another, and repeat until the knot disappears.
Aut stringe super ipsam laminam plumbi decenter.	Or bind a lead plate over it firmly.
Plumbo enim est proprietas remouendi omne quod timetur ex membris.	Lead has the property of removing all such concerns from the limbs.
Si autem nodatio iam in lapidem conuersa est, et stricta est, et festine prouocat necessitas ad auferendum eam: tunc finde super eam ex superiori parte eius, et incide superfluitatem eminentem: aut rade eam cum quibusdam rasoriis, donec remoueatur: et cura ipsam, donec sanetur.	If the knot has hardened into stone and is constricting, and there is an urgent need to remove it, then cut it from above and remove the protruding excess, or shave it down with certain rasps until it is gone, and treat it until it heals.
¶ De curatione quando restauratur, et remanet membrum post illud subtile, non secundum naturam suam.	On the treatment when restoration leaves the limb thin and not in its natural state.
Caput XXII.	Chapter 22.
Quando restauratur fractura ossis, et remanet membrum post illud subtile, debile: tunc non fit illud nisi propter caussas multas.	When a bone fracture is restored and the limb remains thin and weak afterward, this happens only for several reasons.
Quarum una est, multitudo solutionis ligamenti et ligationis eius non secundum quod oportet: aut propter superfluitatem stricturae ligamentorum, donec prohibeatur nutrimentum currere ad membrum: aut propter multitudinem embrocationis superfluae in non sua hora: aut propter paucitatem sanguinis in corpore infirmi, et debilitatem eius.	One reason is the frequent loosening and improper binding of the ligament, or due to the excessive tightness of the bindings, which prevents nourishment from reaching the limb; or due to excessive embrocation at the wrong time, or due to a lack of blood in the patient's body and their weakness.
Et curatio illius est, cibare infirmum, et tenerum facere corpus eius, donec multiplicetur sanguis in eo.	The treatment is to feed the patient and to soften their body until blood increases in it.
Et administra balneum.	And administer baths.
Et intromitte gaudium et laeticiam super eum.	Introduce joy and cheerfulness to the patient.
Deinde pone picem super membrum: ut attrahat pix ad ipsum nutrimentum multum.	Then place pitch on the limb, so that the pitch draws much nourishment to it.
Et assiduet embrocationem eius cum aqua tepida, donec currat nutrimentum, et redeat ad figuram suam naturalem.	And continually apply embrocation with warm water until nourishment flows and the limb returns to its natural shape.
¶ De curatione ossium fractorum quando restaurantur tortuose.	On the treatment of broken bones when they heal crookedly.
Caput XXIII.	Chapter 23.
Quando accidit membro iam restaurato post sanationem eius tortuositas, et eminentia ossi fracto, aut nodatio: et foedatur per illud forma membri, uerumtamen membrum non prohibetur ab operatione sua naturali: tunc non oportet ut recipias sermonem eius, qui existimat, ut frangatur membrum denuo.	When, after healing, a crookedness, protrusion of the broken bone, or a knotting occurs in the limb and this distorts the shape of the limb, but does not prevent it from its natural function, then do not follow the advice of anyone who suggests breaking the limb again.
Et iam fuerunt multi stolidorum medicorum et restauratorum, qui fecerunt illud in terra nostra.	Many foolish doctors and restorers in our land have done this.
Et haec quidem operatio est uituperabilis ualde, perducens ad mortem, uel timorem magnum, ut scilicet sit mortalis.	This procedure is indeed highly blameworthy, leading to death or great fear, as it is potentially fatal.
Verum si claudicatio et nodatio sunt recentes: tunc oportet, ut embrocetur cum aqua tepida, in qua decoctae sint herbae mollificantes, sicut folia alteae, et radix eius, et mellilotum, et illis similia.	But if the limp and knotting are recent, then they should be massaged with warm water in which softening herbs, like marshmallow leaves and root, and sweet clover, and similar herbs, have been boiled.
Et fiat ei emplastrum cum emplastro mollificatiuo, sicut dyachilon decenter factum.	Apply a plaster made with a softening plaster, such as properly prepared dyachilon.
Aut sumatur mucilago radicis alteae, et concutiatur cum adipe gallinae, aut oleo sisamino: et emplastretur cum eo.	Or take marshmallow root mucilage, mix it with chicken fat or sesame oil, and apply it as a plaster.
Aut sumantur ficus pingues, et terantur cum fimo columbino, et similibus eis quae nominantur ex medicinis diminuentes consolidationem.	Or take fat figs, grind them with pigeon dung, and similar ingredients known among medicines that reduce hardening.
Et quandoque soluitur nodatio cum fricatione assidua, antiqua, et leni, quae fit cum manibus humidis.	Sometimes, the knotting loosens with gentle, prolonged, and careful rubbing done with wet hands.
Et administretur motus membri ad omnem partem in horis omnibus.	Move the limb in every direction at all times.
Si autem tortuositas iam antecessit, et fortis facta est, et conuersa est in lapidem, et prouocata est necessitas ad curatonem eius cum ferro: tunc oportet, ut secetur superius eius, et soluatur continuatio ossis, et incidatur quod superfluit ex nodatione aut osse cum Incisoriis subtilibus: et administretur facilitas in illo cum sollicitudine et studio.	If the crookedness is longstanding, has become strong, and has turned to stone, and there is a need to treat it with metal tools, then it is necessary to cut above it, to separate the bone union, and to remove the excess from the knot or bone with fine cutting tools, applying ease and care.
¶ De sermone in Dislocatione.	On the discussion of dislocation.
Caput XXIIII.	Chapter 24.
Dislocatio est exitus alicuius iuncturarum a loco suo.	Dislocation is the displacement of a joint from its natural position.
Quare prohibetur a motu suo, et foedatur figura membri.	Thus it is prevented from movement, and the limb’s form is disfigured.
Et facit accidere super infirmum dolores, et laesiones uehementes.	It causes the patient severe pain and injury.
Quando ergo accidit alicui dislocatio, tunc oportet festinare ad restaurationem eius: neque tardet eam omnino.	Therefore, when a dislocation occurs, it is necessary to quickly restore it and not delay at all.
Nam si tardatur, apostematur locus, et fortasse cum eo reductio dislocationis.	For if delayed, the area may become inflamed, and perhaps hinder the reduction of the dislocation.
Propter illud ergo non oportet, ut moueatur, neque extendatur in hora apostemationis eius.	For this reason, it should not be moved or extended when it is inflamed.
Quoniam multotiens facit accidere spasmum, et dolores laedentes.	Since often it causes spasms and harmful pains.
Verum quando accidit illud, tunc oportet, ut properes ad phlebotomiam infirmi.	But when this happens, you must promptly perform bloodletting on the patient.
Deinde dimittatur, donec quiescat apostema.	Then leave it until the inflammation subsides.
Deinde embrocetur membrum cum aqua calida et oleo.	Then rub the limb with warm water and oil.
Deinde reducatur cum facilitate.	Then it should be reduced with ease.
Et curetur omne membrum cum eo cuius rememoratio ueniet in loco suo.	And treat every limb with the appropriate remedy, which will be discussed in its proper place.
Ego quidem iam ordinaui capitula dislocationis, etiam secundum quod praecessit in fractura, a superiori parte corporis usque ad inferiorem eius.	Indeed, I have already organized the chapters on dislocation, just as in fractures, from the upper part of the body to the lower.
¶ De cura dislocationis Mandibulae inferioris.	On the treatment of dislocation of the lower jaw.
Caput XXV.	Chapter 25.
Parum dislocantur duae Mandibulae nisi raro.	The two sides of the jaw rarely dislocate.
Et dislocatio ambarum fit secundum unum duoru modorum.	When both are dislocated, it occurs in one of two ways.
Aut enim remouentur a locis suis remotione parua, et mollificantur parumper.	Either they are displaced slightly, becoming somewhat loosened,
Aut dislocantur dislocatione integra, completa, donec mollificentur ad partem pectoris, donec currat saliua infirmi, et non possit tenere eam, neque possit claudere os suum, et inuoluatur lingua eius cum sermone.	Or they are entirely dislocated, so that they fall toward the chest, causing saliva to flow uncontrollably, preventing the patient from closing the mouth, and causing the tongue to interfere with speech.
Quando autem dislocatio eius est parua, tunc redit in pluribus dispositionibus per se cum paucitate.	When the dislocation is minor, it often returns to its normal position on its own with minimal intervention.
Si uero est dislocatio completa, integra: tunc oportet, ut administretur reductio eius uelociter, et non tardetur penitus. Et est, ut teneat minister caput infirmi: et intromittat medicus pollicem manus suae unius in radicem Mandibulae dislocationis inter os eius, si dislocatio est a parte una.	But if the dislocation is complete, it is necessary to reposition it quickly, without any delay. To do this, an assistant should hold the patient’s head still, while the physician inserts the thumb of one hand along the root of the jaw’s dislocation, on one side if only one side is dislocated.
Aut intromittat duos pollices suos simul, si dislocatio est a duabus partibus.	Or, if both sides are dislocated, the physician should insert both thumbs simultaneously.
Et reliqui digiti manuum eius sint extra, quibus aequet.	The other fingers should be placed outside to steady the movement.
Et praecipe infirmo, ut mundificet Mandibulas suas, et absoluat eas ad manducandum ad omnem partem.	Instruct the patient to relax the jaw, as if preparing to chew from side to side.
Et medicus aequet Mandibulam, et impellat Mandibulam, donec redeat ad locum suum.	Then the physician should gently push the jaw, guiding it back into its position.
Quod si difficilis sit reductio eius, praecipue si sunt ambae Mandibulae simul: tunc administra uaporationem cum aqua calida, et oleo, donec fiat facilis reductio ambarum: neque tardes reductionem ambarum omnino, sicut diximus.	If repositioning is difficult, especially when both sides are dislocated, then apply warm steam with water and oil until the jaw can be repositioned more easily. Do not delay the procedure.
Cum ergo redeunt et aequantur, et clauditur os infirmi, et non mollificantur: tunc pone super eas puluillos pannorum, cum ceroto quod sit factum ex cera: et oleo rosato.	Once the jaw is realigned and the patient can close the mouth without looseness, place small cloth pads over the area, with a plaster made of wax and rose oil.
Deinde ligetur cum facilitate cum ligamentis mollibus.	Then gently secure it with soft bindings.
Et sit somnus infirmi super dorsum suum: et caput eius sit stans inter duo puluinaria, ut non moueat ipsum ad dextram et sinistram: neque conetur, uel cogatur masticare aliquid: imo ponatur cibus eius sorbitio lenis, donec quando remouentur dolores, et nodatur Mandibula: tunc comedat quod uult, et administretur illud cum facilitate.	The patient should sleep on their back with the head held steady between two pillows to prevent any movement to the sides and should avoid any chewing motions. Food should be given in soft sips until pain subsides and the jaw stabilizes; then, the patient may eat freely but cautiously.
Non laboret super operationem oris sui apud comestionem et potum, et oscitationem, donec nodetur Mandibula, et sanetur.	The patient should avoid straining the jaw during eating, drinking, and yawning until the jaw is stable and healed.
Quod si difficilis fiat reductio duarum Mandibuiarum, quando dislocantur in hora aliqua, et non conuertuntur ad loca sua: tunc multotiens accidunt inde febres, et soda assidua: et fortasse soluitur uenter infirmi, et fortasse uomit choleram puram.	If both sides of the jaw are severely dislocated and cannot be repositioned, fevers, continuous sweating, diarrhea, and possibly vomiting of bile may occur.
Cum ergo uides illud: tunc scias, quia est perditio, et multotiens moritur ille cui accidit illud in decem diebus.	When these symptoms appear, know that it is likely fatal, and often the patient dies within ten days.
¶ De reducione dislocationis Furculae, et extremitatis humeri.	On the reduction of the dislocation of the clavicle and shoulder joint.
Caput XXVI.	Chapter 26.
Furcula quidem non dislocatur ex latere intrinseco, propter continuationem suam cum pectore.	The clavicle does not typically dislocate inward due to its connection with the chest.
Et dislocatur quandoque ad exteriora: et declaratur illud sensui.	Occasionally, it dislocates outward, which is evident to the touch.
Et restauratio eius est, ut iaceat infirmus super dorsum suum, et extendat brachia sua.	To restore it, have the patient lie on their back and extend their arms.
Deinde coarta locum cum palma tua coartatione forti, nam ipsa redibit.	Then press the area with your palm firmly, which should reposition it.
Deinde pone super ipsam emplastrum, et puluillos, et stringe eam.	Afterward, apply a plaster and cloth pads, and secure it.
Extremitates uero eius quae sequuntur cubitum, et continuantur cum eo, non dislocantur, nisi raro.	The ends of the bones extending toward the elbow, connected to it, rarely dislocate.
Quod si dislocatur die aliquo: tunc oportet, ut reducatur et aequetur secundum quod diximus, et quod praeparatur tibi.	But if it is dislocated on any day, then it should be reduced and aligned as we have said, and what is prepared for you.
Deinde pone super ipsam emplastrum, et puluillos, et stricturam.	Then place a plaster over it, along with cushions and a bandage.
Et praecipe infirmo, adhaerere tranquillitati et quieti, donec sanetur.	And instruct the patient to adhere to calm and rest until healed.
Et cum hac eadem curatione reducitur extremitas Humeri, quando separatur etiam a loco suo.	And with this same treatment, the shoulder extremity is reduced when it is also separated from its place.
¶ De reductione dislocationis Humeri.	On the reduction of shoulder dislocation.
Caput XXVII.	Chapter 27.
Scias, quod Humerus non dislocatur, nisi secundum tres modos.	Know that the shoulder is dislocated only in three ways.
Quorum unus est, ut dislocetur ad partem titillici ad inferiora.	One of these is that it dislocates downward toward the side of the armpit.
Secundus est, ut dislocetur ad partem pectoris.	The second is that it dislocates toward the chest.
Et fortasse dislocatur ad superiorem partem Humeri: et illud fit raro.	And sometimes it dislocates to the upper part of the shoulder: and this occurs rarely.
Et non dislocatur ad posteriora, propter spatulam.	And it does not dislocate backward, because of the shoulder blade.
Et non dislocatur ad anteriora, propter neruos.	And it does not dislocate forward, because of the nerves.
Et plurimum quidem dislocatur et egreditur ad inferiora uersus titillicum: praecipue in illis, quorum carnes sunt paucae.	And indeed, it most often dislocates and exits downward toward the armpit, especially in those who have little flesh.
Quoniam egrediur in eis uelociter, et ingreditur uelociter.	For it exits from them quickly and re-enters quickly.
In illis autem quorum carnes sunt multae, est econtrario illius, scilicet quia egreditur cum difficultate, et ingreditur cum difficultate.	However, in those with much flesh, the opposite is true, namely, it exits with difficulty and re-enters with difficulty.
Et fortasse accidit quibusdam hominibus percussio, aut casus, et apostematur Humerus apostemate calido, et putatur propter illud, quia iam dislocatus est.	And sometimes, a blow or fall happens to certain people, and the shoulder is inflamed with a hot abscess, and because of this it is thought that it has been dislocated.
Oportet ergo, ut experiaris illud, donec stes super certitudinem eius, et tunc procedas in curatione eius.	Therefore, you should examine it until you are certain of it, and then proceed with its treatment.
Et cognoscitur dislocatio, quando est ad inferiora uersus titillicum: et distinguas inter Humerum dislocatum, et humerum sanum.	And the dislocation is recognized when it is downward toward the armpit, and you can distinguish between a dislocated shoulder and a healthy shoulder.
Nam tu inuenis inter eos diuersitatem manifestam, et inuenis in capite Humeri concauitatem, et sub titillico apud tactum caput humeri quasi ipsum sit ouum: et non potest infirmus reducere manum ad aurem suam, neque mouere eam omnibus motibus.	For you will find a clear difference between them, and you will find a concavity at the head of the shoulder, and under the armpit, upon touch, the head of the shoulder feels almost like an egg: and the patient cannot bring his hand to his ear or move it in all motions.
Et similiter, si dislocatur uersus pectus, aut ad sursum: tunc inuenis manifestum tactum, neque occultatur.	And similarly, if it dislocates toward the chest or upward, then you find it clearly by touch, nor is it hidden.
Et huius quidem dislocationis fit facilis reductio, quando est recens, aut infirmus est puer.	And the reduction of this dislocation is indeed easy when it is recent, or the patient is a child.
Et reductio eius est, ut eleuet minister manum suam ad superiora.	And its reduction is achieved by the assistant raising his hand upward.
Deinde pone tu pollices tuos sub titillico eius, et eleua iuncturam cum fortitudine ad sursum ad locum suum: et minister eleuet manum suam, et extendat eam ad sursum.	Then place your thumbs under his armpit, and lift the joint forcefully upward to its place, and the assistant raises his hand and extends it upward.
Deinde ponat eam inferius, ipse enim redit uelociter.	Then place it downward, for it returns quickly.
Si autem non redit per illud quod diximus, et sunt a principio quo accidit dislocatio dies multi: tunc oportet, ut balneetur infirmus in aqua calida, et administretur embrocatio quae mollificat et lenit, sicut si decoquantur radices alteae, et foenugraecum, et mellilotum in aqua, et administretur.	But if it does not return by what we have said, and many days have passed since the dislocation occurred, then it is necessary for the patient to bathe in warm water, and to receive an ointment that softens and soothes, as if the roots of marshmallow, fenugreek, and melilot are boiled in water and applied.
Deinde resupinetur infirmus super dorsum suum: et ponatur sub titillico eius sphera et lana, quae sit aequalis inter lenitatem et stricturam.	Then the patient should lie on his back, and a sphere and wool, balanced between softness and firmness, should be placed under his armpit.
Deinde ponat medicus calcaneum suum super spheram, et eleuet caput humeri cum uirtute, et attrahat manum infirmi ad inferiora.	Then the physician places his heel on the sphere, lifts the head of the shoulder with force, and pulls the patient's hand downward.
Et minister alius teneat caput infirmi, ut non moueatur ad inferiora: ipse namque redit statim.	And another assistant holds the patient’s head so that it does not move downward, for it returns immediately.
Et si uis, reduc eum secundum modum hunc: et est, ut facias esse praesentem uirum qui sit longior infirmo, et stet a parte lateris, et intromittat humerum suum sub titillico infirmi: et eleuet titillicum eius ad superiora, donec sit infirmus suspensus in aere.	And if you wish, reduce it according to this method: have a taller man than the patient stand at the side, insert his shoulder under the patient's armpit, and lift his armpit upward until the patient is suspended in the air.
Et minister alius trahat manum infirmi ad inferiora uentris eius.	And another assistant should pull the patient’s hand downward toward his abdomen.
Si autem infirmus est leuis: tunc oportet, ut suspendatur cum eo aliquid aliud, ut grauet ipsum: nam redit dislocatio statim.	But if the patient is light, then something else should be suspended with him to weigh him down, for the dislocation will return immediately.
Et reducitur etiam secundum modum alium: et est, ut figatur in terra lignum longum, cuius caput sit rotundum figura: sicut manica mortarii: quod non sit grossum, neque subtile.	And it is also reduced according to another method: this is, a long piece of wood is fixed in the ground, with a rounded head in shape, like the handle of a mortar, which should be neither thick nor thin.
Deinde ponatur sub infirmo postquam ponuntur super caput ligni panni lenes: et infirmus sit stans secundum longitudinem ligni.	Then, after placing soft cloths over the head of the wood, it is positioned under the patient, who should stand along the length of the wood.
Deinde extendatur manus eius ad inferiora a parte altera, et extendatur corpus eius ab altera parte cum fortitudine: nam iunctura redit ad locum suum cum uelocitate.	Then his hand is stretched downward on one side, and his body is extended from the other side with strength, for the joint quickly returns to its place.
Quod si difficilis sit eius reductio per omnia quae diximus, tunc utere hac cura: et est, ut accipias lignum cuius longitudo sit quantitas duorum brachiorum, et eius latitudo quantitas quatuor digitorum, et grossitudo eius quantitas duorum digitorum: cui sit caput rotundum, ut alleuietur introitus eius in profundum titillici, secundum hanc formam.	But if its reduction is difficult with everything we have described, then use this method: take a piece of wood with a length equal to two forearms, a width of four fingers, and a thickness of two fingers, with a rounded head to ease its entry into the depth of the armpit, according to this shape.
Deinde ligentur super caput rotundum panni lenes, ut non laedat lignum infirmum.	Then tie soft cloths over the rounded head so that the wood does not harm the patient.
Deinde ponatur sub titillico infirmi, ut extendatur manus, aut brachium super lignum usque ad inferiora: et ligetur lignum super adiutorium et brachium et extremitatem manus.	Then place it under the patient’s armpit so that his hand or arm stretches downward over the wood, and the wood is tied over the support, arm, and the end of the hand.
Deinde ponatur brachium super gradum scalae per latitudinem, et extendatur manus ad inferiora, et dimittatur reliquum corpus suspensum a parte altera.	Then place the arm over the width of a step of a ladder, extend the hand downward, and let the rest of the body be suspended from the other side.
Iunctura enim ingreditur statim.	For the joint re-enters immediately.
Cum ergo completur introitus eius secundum quemcunque modum possibile est: tunc oportet, ut ponas sub titillico spheram aequalis quantitatis ex lana.	When its re-entry is thus completed by whatever means possible, then it is necessary to place a sphere of equal size made of wool under the armpit.
Deinde pone emplastrum praeparatum ex puluere molendini cum olibano, et albumine oui super humerum totum, sicut reuoluitur desuper.	Then apply a plaster prepared from mill dust with frankincense and egg white over the whole shoulder, as it is wrapped above.
Deinde stringe sub titillico strictura decenti: et reuoluatur ligamentum super emplastrum desuper: et annectatur manus eius ad collum ipsius, et dimittatur, neque moueatur manus eius septem diebus.	Then secure it under the armpit with an appropriate bandage, wrap the ligament over the plaster, and fasten his hand to his neck, leaving it unmoved for seven days.
Et oportet, ut ponatur cibus infirmi paucus, donec confortetur membrum: quoniam est uelocius ad sanationem eius.	And it is necessary to keep the patient’s food light until the limb is strengthened, for it aids its healing more quickly.
Deinde ponatur amplior post septem dies, aut quinque, et exerceatur cum motu.	Then after seven or five days, a larger amount may be given, and it should be exercised with motion.
Si ergo firmatur et non mollificatur, tunc iam sanatus est.	If it thus becomes firm and does not soften, then it is healed.
Si uero iunctura dislocatur multotiens, propter humiditatem quae accidit ei, aut propter caussam aliam: tunc oportet, ut administretur in ea cauterium cum tribus foraminibus, secundum quod praemissum est in capitulo de cauterio.	But if the joint dislocates many times, due to humidity affecting it or for another reason, then it is necessary to apply cautery with three openings, as mentioned previously in the chapter on cautery.
Quod si fiat hoc totum, et soluatur ligamentum post septem dies, et non confirmetur iunctura, et reducatur emplastrum et strictura super ipsum aliquotiens, et non firmetur, et cadat, et non possit eleuare ipsum ad superiora: tunc scias, quod nerui eius qui sunt ad caput humeri, iam incisi sunt, aut extensi, aut mollificati: et scias, quod iunctura nunquam figetur in loco suo.	If all this is done, and the ligament is loosened after seven days, and the joint does not become stable, and the plaster and bandage are reapplied over it multiple times, and it does not stabilize, and it falls and cannot be lifted upward, then you should know that the nerves attached to the head of the shoulder are now either severed, stretched, or softened, and know that the joint will never be fixed in its place.
Dislocationis autem quae fit uersus pectus et mammillam, aut ad posteriora reductio, fit cum impulsione et extensione cum manibus: donec redeat, et administretur in ea reliqua strictura et cura, donec sanetur.	The reduction of a dislocation that occurs toward the chest and breast or to the back is done with pressure and extension with the hands until it returns, and the remaining bandage and care is applied to it until it heals.
Quod si accidat post sanationem grauitas in membro, et tarditas in motu eius, tunc utatur infirmus balneo multotiens, donec leniatur illa grauitas, et redeat ad naturam eius primam.	If, after healing, heaviness in the limb occurs and slowness in its movement, then the patient should use the bath frequently, until that heaviness is lessened and it returns to its original nature.
¶ De curatione dislocationis Cubiti.	On the treatment of elbow dislocation.
Caput XXVIII.	Chapter 28.
Scias, quod iunctura Cubiti dislocatur cum difficultate, et similiter redit cum difficultate etiam: et ipsa quidem dislocatur ad omnes partes, et praecipue ad anteriorem, aut ad posteriorem.	Know that the elbow joint dislocates with difficulty and likewise returns with difficulty as well; and it indeed dislocates in all directions, especially to the front or to the back.
Et eius dislocatio non occultatur tibi: quoniam est cadens sub uisu, et sub tactu, secundum quamcunque figuram dislocetur.	And its dislocation is not hidden from you, for it falls within sight and touch, however it may be dislocated.
Et quando coniungis cubitum dislocatum cum sano, declaratur tibi illud manifeste.	And when you compare the dislocated elbow with the healthy one, it becomes clearly apparent to you.
Concauatur enim iunctura, et non potest duplicare brachium, neque tangere cum eo humerum suum.	For the joint is concave, and he cannot bend the arm or touch his shoulder with it.
Et oportet, ut properes, et reducas dislocationem statim, antequam accidat ei apostema calidum.	And it is necessary to act quickly and reduce the dislocation immediately, before a hot swelling occurs.
Nam si accidit ei apostema calidum, difficilis fit eius reductio: et fortasse non sanatur omnino, praecipue quando est ad posteriora.	For if a hot swelling occurs, its reduction becomes difficult, and perhaps it will not heal completely, especially when it is displaced backward.
Est enim deterior omnibus speciebus dislocationis, et uehementioris doloris eis: et multotiens descendit cum ea mors.	For it is worse than all types of dislocation, and causes more severe pain; and often death follows it.
Et restauratio eius quando est ex eis quae possibile est reducere, est, ut extendatur manus eius cum duabus manibus: et brachium eius sit expansum, et manus medici sint supra cubitum et sub eo, et ipse impellat iuncturam cum pollicibus manuum suarum simul, aut cum radice palmae suae, donec redeat ad locum suum.	And its restoration, when it is among those that can be reduced, is done by extending his hand with both hands, with his arm stretched out, and the physician’s hands placed above and below the elbow, and he presses the joint with his thumbs together or with the base of his palm until it returns to its place.
Aut uero dislocatio est ad anteriora: tunc redit ita, ut duplicet manum cum manu sua, donec percutiat cum radice palmae suae cubitum qui est dislocatus.	Or if the dislocation is to the front, then it is reduced by bending the hand with his hand, until he strikes the dislocated elbow with the base of his palm.
Quod si non obedit dislocatio ad reductionem, tunc administra extensionem uehementem, fortem ualde: et est, ut extendant brachium duo ministri: et teneant ipsum duo ministri etiam, ut non remoueatur apud extensionem.	But if the dislocation does not yield to the reduction, then apply strong, forceful traction; two assistants should extend the arm, while two others hold him in place so he does not move during the extension.
Deinde reuoluatur brachium ad omnem partem postquam inuoluitur super manum eius pannus duplicatus longus, aut ligamentum latum.	Then the arm is rotated in every direction, after a long folded cloth or wide bandage is wrapped over his hand.
Et quando tangit medicus manu sua iuncturam, inungat ipsam cum oleo: ut sit illud ex eis quae adiuuant in lubricitate iuncturae cum facilitate.	And when the physician touches the joint with his hand, he should anoint it with oil, as this aids in the lubrication of the joint, making it easier.
Deinde impellat iuncturam impulsione uehementi, donec redeat.	Then he presses the joint with strong force until it returns.
Et post reductionem eius oportet, ut ponatur super ipsam emplastrum, in quo sit stipticitas, et exiccatio cum albugine oui: et stringatur strictura decenti.	And after its reduction, it is necessary to apply a plaster to it, containing astringency and drying agents with egg white, and bind it with an appropriate bandage.
Et suspendatur brachium a collo infirmi: et dimitte diebus aliquot.	And suspend the arm from the patient's neck, and leave it for several days.
Deinde soluatur.	Then loosen it.
Si ergo firmatur iunctura in loco suo, tunc solue ligamentum ab ea, et dimitte ipsam.	If the joint is thus stabilized in its place, then release the bandage from it and leave it.
Et si uides iuncturam non bene stringi, tunc itera emplastrum, et ligamentum, et dimitte ipsam diebus aliquot, donec stringatur.	And if you see that the joint is not properly tightened, then repeat the plaster and bandage, and leave it for several days until it is secure.
Deinde solue ipsam.	Then release it.
Si autem accidit ei grauitas post reductionem iuncturae, et dispositio sit in motu: tunc administra humectationem in balneatione, et fricationem subtilem, et compressionem, donec liniatur, aut pone super iuncturam iliati, id est, natem arietis pinguem.	But if heaviness occurs after the joint reduction and there is stiffness in movement, then apply moistening in the bath, gentle rubbing, and compression until it is softened, or place the fat of a ram’s buttock over the joint.
Deinde ligetur, et dimitte ipsam super eam die et nocte.	Then bind it and leave it on there for a day and a night.
Deinde aufer eam, et intromitte ipsum in balneum.	Then remove it, and immerse him in the bath.
Cum ergo sudat, tunc frica iuncturam fricatione temperata.	When he begins to sweat, then rub the joint with moderate friction.
Deinde itera super ipsam aleliati uice una, et secunda, et tertia cum introitu balnei, donec leniatur.	Then apply the fat to it again, once, twice, and a third time with each bath entry until it is softened.
Et si uis, pone super ipsam stercus uaccinum, humidum, calefactum cum butyro, et stringe super ipsum.	And if you wish, place fresh cow dung, heated with butter, over it and bind it.
Fac illud aliquotiens.	Do this a few times.
Ipsa namque lenitur, et redit ad dispositionem suam.	For it will then be softened, and return to its natural state.
¶ De curatione dislocationis Almahassem.	On the treatment of the dislocation of the wrist.
Caput XXIX.	Chapter 29.
Mahassem manus multotiens dislocatur, id est, iunctura quae est inter brachium et manum.	The wrist is often dislocated, that is, the joint between the arm and the hand.
Et reductio dislocationis eius est facilis.	And the reduction of its dislocation is easy.
Econtrario reliquarum iuncturarum.	Unlike other joints.
Verumtamen oportet, ut uelocitas sit in reductione dislocationis eius in hora in qua dislocatur, antequam apostemetur locus: aut accidat in eo apostema calidum.	However, speed is necessary in the reduction of its dislocation at the hour it becomes dislocated, before the area swells or a warm swelling occurs there.
Et reductio dislocationis in eo est, ut ponas Mahassem manus infirmae super tabulam, et extendat minister manum eius: et ponat medicus palmam suam super eminentiam iuncturae, et impellat eam, donec redeat.	And the reduction of the dislocation is achieved by placing the wrist of the injured hand on a table, with an assistant extending the hand; and the doctor places his palm over the prominence of the joint and pushes it until it returns to position.
Verumtamen oportet, ut consyderetur, si palma iam recessit ad uentrem manus: tunc ponat infirmus dorsum manus suae super tabulam apud extensionem et reductionem: et si dislocatio est procedens ad dorsum manus, tunc sit positio manus eius intrinsece super tabulam, id est, interior pars manus eius super tabulam ponatur, ut cadat manus medici super ipsam eminentiam iuncturae.	However, it must be observed if the palm has already shifted toward the inner side of the hand: in that case, the patient places the back of his hand on the table during the extension and reduction; and if the dislocation moves toward the back of the hand, then the hand is placed with its inner side on the table, so that the doctor's hand falls on the prominence of the joint.
Si ergo redit statim, bonum est: et si non, tunc stringe eam cum emplastro sedante apostema: et dimitte ipsum, neque iteres illud.	If it immediately returns, this is good; if not, then bind it with a plaster that soothes swelling, and leave it without repeating the process.
Nam si non tolerat, neque potes super reductionem eius, postquam praetereunt ei dies, uerumtamen iunctura remanet secundum tortuositatem suam: et non nocet infirmo aliquid, nisi quod mollificatur manus, et non potest stringere aliquid: tunc scias, quod nerui sunt abscissi, aut attriti.	For if it cannot tolerate it, and you cannot complete the reduction after several days have passed, the joint will remain twisted; it does not harm the patient except that the hand becomes weak and cannot grip anything. Then you know that the nerves are severed or damaged.
Non est ergo ingenium in ea, nisi ut stringatur cum cauterio prope casum eius.	There is thus no treatment except to cauterize it near the affected area.
Et fortasse non confert illud aliquid.	And perhaps this will not help at all.
Cum ergo non redit Almahassem, tunc pone super ipsum emplastrum, quod narrauimus.	Therefore, if the wrist does not return, then place upon it the plaster we have described.
Deinde stringatur, et dimittatur quinque diebus: postea soluatur et dimittatur, et exercitetur manus.	Then bind it, and leave it for five days; afterward, unbind it and leave it, and exercise the hand.
Si autem priuatur motus eius, et accidat in ea aliquid grauitatis: tunc leni eam cum aqua calefacta, et fricatione aliquotiens, donec leniatur.	If, however, it is deprived of motion, and any heaviness occurs in it, then soften it with warm water and rub it occasionally until it softens.
¶ De curatione dislocationis Digitorum.	On the treatment of the dislocation of the fingers.
Caput XXX.	Chapter 30.
Quando dislocatur ex eis Digitus ad omnem partem, uel ex eis Digitus ad dorsum palmae, aut uentrem eius: tunc extende Digitum, et impelle dislocationem pollicibus tuis, donec redeat.	When one of the fingers is dislocated in any direction, or one of the fingers toward the back or the inner side of the hand, then extend the finger and push the dislocation with your thumbs until it returns.
Deinde sustenta caput Digiti, et suspende ipsum uersus partem ad quam dislocatur, et dimitte ipsum duobus diebus.	Then support the head of the finger, suspend it toward the side to which it was dislocated, and leave it for two days.
Deinde suspende eum, et extende ipsum, donec aequetur rectus in illo die suo.	Then suspend it, and extend it until it aligns straight on that day.
Cum ergo fuerit nox, liga ipsum secundum narrationem ipsam: et non cesses soluere ipsum in die: et exerce eum cum motu: et liga ipsum in nocte.	Then at night, bind it according to the instructions; during the day, keep unbinding it, exercise it with movement, and bind it at night.
Fac illud diebus aliquot, donec stringatur.	Do this for several days until it becomes firm.
Et similiter fac cum eo, si dislocatur ad uentrem manus: et liga eum ad partem ipsam, et fac cum eo operationem tuam primam, donec sanetur.	Similarly, if it dislocates toward the inner side of the hand, bind it toward that side, and perform the initial procedure until it heals.
Et similiter fac cum eo quando dislocatur ad reliquas partes.	And do likewise when it is dislocated toward other parts.
¶ De curatione dislocationis Spondilium dorsi.	On the treatment of dislocation of the vertebrae of the back.
Caput XXXI.	Chapter 31.
Quando accidit alicui Spondilium dorsi, aut colli dislocatio completa, aut remouentur Spondiles multae a locis suis: tunc non est in ea curatio, quoniam mors properat infirmo.	When someone experiences a complete dislocation of the vertebrae of the back or neck, or if many vertebrae are displaced from their places, then there is no treatment, as death quickly approaches the patient.
Et signum illius est, quod egestio infirmi egreditur absque uoluntate, neque potest tenere eam: et multotiens mollificantur ex eo quaedam membra eius, aut duo crura eius, aut duo brachia ipsius, aut unum eorum: aut remouetur Spondilis una de loco suo.	A sign of this is that the patient’s stool passes involuntarily, and they cannot control it; often, certain limbs are weakened as a result, or both of their legs, or both of their arms, or one of these; or a single vertebra is displaced from its place.
Multotiens enim remouetur, quod multotiens fit remotio earum ad quatuor partes.	Often it is displaced, and displacement frequently occurs in four directions.
Illa ergo quae remouetur ad posteriora, nominatur Gibbositas.	Thus, that which is displaced backward is called a hump.
Curatio uero eius est, ut consyderes tunc, si Gibbositas accidit a puericia: tunc non est in ea curatio, neque ex ea est sanatio omnino.	The treatment for this is to consider, if the hump appeared in childhood, then there is no cure, and no recovery is possible at all.
In illa autem quae accidit a casu, aut percussione, aut similibus illis, iam dixerunt antiqui modos curationis sermone prolixo.	But in cases that occur due to an accident, blow, or similar causes, the ancients have already described lengthy methods of treatment.
Cuius plurimum non redit cum proficuo.	Most often, this does not return with success.
Ego uero iam abbreuiaui ex illo, cuius parum excusat a multo eius ex propinquitate mea ad intentionem: et expositione mea qua exposui illud, et formaui instrumentum eius diuerso modo ab eo quo ostenderunt illud, et exposuerunt ipsum.	However, I have now shortened that, whose small portion excuses much of it due to my closeness to the aim; and in my explanation in which I set it forth, I have crafted its instrument differently from the way they presented and explained it.
Dico ergo, quod in gibbositate quae accidit ab anteriori parte, non est ingenium, neque sanatio ex ea.	Thus, I say that in a hump which occurs at the front, there is no treatment or recovery from it.
Et similiter in illa quae fit ad duo latera etiam.	And likewise, for one that occurs on both sides as well.
Et non curatur ex eis, nisi illa quae accidit in dorso proprie, cum eo quod ego narro: et est, ut extendatur infirmus super faciem suam super podium aequale prope parietem: et expandatur sub eo stramentum humidum, ut non laedatur pectus eius.	Of these, only the one occurring on the back itself can be treated by the method I describe: the patient is stretched face down on a level platform near a wall, with damp padding spread beneath him, so his chest is not injured.
Deinde ponatur lignum rectum fixum in fouea in terra, in extremitate podii, circiter caput eius: et lignum aliud circiter pedes eius in extremitate altera podii: et minister teneat lignum, et sit non firmatum in fouea.	Then place a straight piece of wood fixed in a hole in the ground at the end of the platform near his head, and another piece near his feet at the other end of the platform, with an assistant holding the wood without fixing it in the hole.
Et minister alter teneat alterum.	And another assistant holds the other.
Deinde inuolue super caput infirmi, et sub titillicis eius ligamentum lene, firmum: et extende extremitatem ligamenti ad lignum quod est apud caput eius: et liga ipsum in eo.	Then wrap a soft, strong bandage over the patient's head and under his chin; extend the end of the bandage to the wood near his head and tie it there.
Deinde stringe ligamentum aliud supra anchas eius, et supra genua ipsius, et apud cauillas eius.	Then fasten another bandage above his hips, above his knees, and at his ankles.
Deinde aggrega ligamenta omnia, et liga ea in ligno altero quod est apud pedes eius.	Then gather all the bandages and tie them to the other piece of wood near his feet.
Deinde extendat omnis minister ad partem ligni ligamentum, et non remoueantur duo ligna ex locis suis in quibus sunt fixa: uerumtamen non sint firmata, sicut diximus.	Then let each assistant pull their respective bandages toward the wood, and the two pieces of wood should not move from their places where they are set, although they should not be fixed, as we mentioned.
Et medicus ponat palmas suas super Spondilem cum fortitudine, donec redeat: aut ponat super eam tabulam.	And the physician should place his palms on the vertebra with strength until it returns, or place a board on it.
Deinde annectat super tabulam pedes suos, donec redeat.	Then he should press down on the board with his feet until it returns.
Si autem non redit per hanc curationem, tunc accipe tabulam cuius longitudo sit circiter tria brachia.	If it does not return with this treatment, then take a board about three arm-lengths long.
Et caua in pariete de quo diximus quod est prope infirmum, locum in quem ingrediatur extremitas tabulae.	And carve a spot in the wall, which we mentioned is near the patient, for the end of the board to fit into.
Deinde pone medium tabulae super Spondilem.	Then place the middle of the board over the vertebra.
Et ponat pedes suos medicus super extremitatem alteram.	And the physician places his feet on the other end.
Et stringat bene, donec coartetur Spondilis, et redeat ad locum suum.	And press firmly until the vertebra is compressed and returns to its place.
Deinde si uis, operare cum torno quod inuoluitur cum manu tua: et est, ut figas in terram apud caput infirmi in postremo podii duo ligna, quorum longitudo sit brachii unius: et apud caput eius in postremo podii duo ligna.	Then, if you wish, work with a winch that turns by hand: and set two pieces of wood in the ground near the patient's head at the end of the platform, each one arm-length tall; and place two pieces near his head at the end of the platform.
Et sit longitudo quae est inter omne lignum palmus unus: et iam factum est in omni loco foramen, in quo currit tornum: et firmetur lignum omne in terra multum, ne moueatur omnino.	And let the distance between each piece of wood be one palm; and a hole has been made in each place, through which the winch operates; each piece of wood should be firmly fixed in the ground so that it does not move at all.
Et intromittatur lignum rotundum: et est tornum in quo reuoluitur ligamentum in duobus foraminibus duorum lignorum.	Then insert a round rod; it is the winch on which the bandage is wound through the two holes in the two pieces of wood.
Et in extremitate eius est foramen, in quo firmatur lignum, cuius longitudo est palmus cum eo quod reuoluitur: et in duobus lignis aliis est similiter illius.	And at its end is a hole in which a piece of wood is fixed, one palm-length, and likewise in the other two pieces of wood.
Deinde stringe ligamenta quae strinxisti in pectore infirmi torno quod est apud caput eius: et ligamenta quae strinxisti in cruribus eius in torno quod est apud pedes eius.	Then tighten the bandages you fastened on the patient’s chest with the winch located near his head, and the bandages on his legs with the winch near his feet.
Deinde stet apud omne tornum minister, qui torqueat manum suam cum licinio, cum quo reuoluitur tornum: et medicus aequet Spondilem, secundum quod diximus.	Then let an assistant stand by each winch, turning it with the handle that rotates the winch, while the physician aligns the vertebra, as we have described.
Haec est forma torni, quae sequitur.	This is the structure of the winch, as follows.
Deinde postquam redit Spondilis, et aequatur locus, tunc oportet, ut ponas emplastrum exiccatiuum cum albumine oui.	Then, after the vertebra returns to place and the area is aligned, apply a drying plaster with egg white.
Deinde stuppam.	Then apply wool.
Postea pone supra emplastrum astellam ex tabula, cuius latitudo sit trium digitorum, aut circiter eos.	Afterward, place a splint of wood on top of the plaster, about three fingers in width, or around that.
Et longitudo eius quantitas quae capiat locum Gibbositatis, et super partem Spondilis sanae.	Its length should cover the area of the hump and extend over the healthy part of the vertebra.
Deinde ligetur cum ligamento quod diximus.	Then fasten it with the bandage we mentioned.
Et utatur infirmus nutrimento subtili, donec sanetur.	And let the patient consume a light diet until he is healed.
Quod si remaneat quiddam eminentiae in loco in fine sanationis: tunc oportet, ut administrentur medicinae quae mollificant, et leniunt, cum administratione tabulae quam narrauimus tempore longo.	If some prominence remains in the area at the end of healing, then softening and soothing medicines should be applied, along with the use of the board we described, for an extended period.
Et iam administratur in illo lamina plumbi.	At this point, a lead sheet is applied there.
Et quandoque accidit eminentia in postremo Spondilium dorsi: et putatur per eam dislocatio, et est illud os quod iam eminet.	Sometimes a prominence occurs at the back of the vertebrae of the spine, and it is thought to be a dislocation, but it is actually just a bone that now protrudes.
Quare non oportet, ut praesumptio fiat ei cum hac medicina: fortasse enim accidere faciet mortem.	Therefore, treatment should not be attempted with this remedy, as it may cause death.
¶ De curatione Anchae dislocatae.	On the treatment of a dislocated hip.
Caput XXXII.	Chapter 32.
Scias, quod iuncturae Anchae, et iuncturae humeri, non accidit, nisi dislocatio tantum: neque accidit eis quod accidit reliquis iuncturis ex remotione parua, et concauitate.	Know that in the joints of the hip and the shoulder, only dislocation occurs; they are not subject to the minor displacements and depressions that happen in other joints.
Et iunctura quidem Anchae dislocatur secundum quatuor partes.	And the hip joint can be dislocated in four directions.
Quod est, quia dislocatur ad anteriora, et ad posteriora.	That is, it may be dislocated forward and backward.
Et plurimum quidem dislocatur ad interiora: et parum dislocatur ad anteriora, et ad posteriora.	Most frequently, it dislocates inward; and it rarely dislocates forward or backward.
Et curatio dislocationis eius ad interiora, est: quia quando tu coniungis crus infirmi sanum cum infirmo, est longius: et est genu eius eminens plus quam sani, et non potest infirmus duplicare pedem suum apud inguen: et est locus qui sequitur inguen apostematus apostemate calido, propterea, quod caput coxae iam peruenit illic.	The treatment for an inward dislocation is this: when you place the patient's healthy leg together with the affected one, it appears longer, and the knee protrudes more than that of the healthy leg. The patient cannot bend his foot near the groin, and the area near the groin is swollen with a hot abscess because the head of the hip has already reached there.
Et signum eius cui accidit dislocatio ad exteriora est, quod sunt accidentia eius contraria istis accidentibus.	The sign of an outward dislocation is that its symptoms are the opposite of these.
Et signum eius cui accidit dislocatio ad anteriora, est, quia expandit crus sanum secundum complementum: uerumtamen non duplicat ipsum absque dolore qui est in genu: et si conatur ad ambulandum, non potest super illud ad anteriora: et retinetur urina eius, et apostematur inguen eius, et apud ambulationem est calcatio eius super calcaneum.	The sign of a forward dislocation is that the patient extends his healthy leg completely; however, he cannot bend it without pain in the knee. If he tries to walk, he cannot step forward on it; he retains urine, his groin is swollen, and while walking, he steps on his heel.
Et signum eius cui accidit dislocatio ad posteriora est, ut non expandat genu, neque possit duplicare ipsum antequam duplicet inguen: et est crus eius etiam breuius altero: et inguen est mollificatum, et est caput coxae apud locum ilii.	The sign of a backward dislocation is that he cannot extend the knee, nor bend it before bending the groin. His leg is also shorter than the other, the groin is softened, and the head of the hip is near the place of the hip bone.
Reductio uero huius dislocationis specierum est, ut consyderes tunc, si dislocatio est uetusta, quae iam antiquatur cum habente ipsam.	The reduction of each type of dislocation is to be considered if the dislocation is old and has become chronic in the patient.
Deinde si tu administres reductionem eius, et remanet secundum dispositionem suam: tunc non est in ea curatio omnino.	Then if you attempt the reduction and it stays in place, it is not in need of further treatment.
Non ergo oportet, ut praesumptio fiat ei penitus.	Therefore, it should not be presumed to require treatment at all.
In illo autem cuius dislocatio est recens, et est ex una specierum quatuor dislocationis: tunc propera ad hoc, ut uoluas iuncturam, et extendas ipsam ad interiora et ad exteriora, et moueas eam dextra et sinistra: fortasse enim redit, et non indiget alia cura.	In the case of a recent dislocation of one of the four types, proceed by rotating the joint, extending it inward and outward, and moving it right and left; it may return to place and need no further treatment.
Si autem non redit hoc modo, tunc oportet, ut praepares ministrum fortem, et extendat crus eius ab inferiori parte: aut cum manibus suis, aut cum ligamento, quod ligetur super crus eius supra genu: et minister alius extendat manum eius desuper, ita ut intromittat manus suas sub titillicis eius.	If it does not return in this way, then a strong assistant should be prepared to extend the patient's leg from below, either with his hands or with a band placed above the knee on his leg. Another assistant should extend the arm from above, inserting his hands under the patient's armpits.
Deinde stringat cum ligamento leni super radicem coxae, et teneat extremitatem ligamenti alterius.	Then he should tighten a soft band over the base of the hip and hold the other end of the band.
Et sit extensio eius aut ab anteriori parte inguinis ad partem furculae, aut a posteriori ad partem dorsi.	The extension should be either from the front part of the groin to the upper chest or from the back to the spine.
Et sit extensio eorum omnium uice una, donec eleuetur infirmus cum corpore suo a terra, et remaneat pendens.	All extensions should happen at once until the patient is lifted from the ground with his body hanging.
Et haec enim species extensionis est species communis specierum quatuor.	This type of extension is the general method for all four types.
Si ergo redit dislocatio cum eo quod diximus, est bonum: et si non, tunc necesse est omni speciei illud quod dicam de curatione propria.	If the dislocation returns in the manner described, it is good; if not, then each type will need its own specific treatment, as I will now explain.
In reductione quidem eius propria quando dislocatio est ad interiora, oportet, ut resupinetur infirmus super latus suum sanum.	In the specific reduction for an inward dislocation, the patient should lie on his healthy side.
Deinde ponat ligamentum super radicem coxae, in eo quod est inter caput coxae, et locum qui est sub inguine.	Then place a band over the base of the hip, between the head of the hip and the place under the groin.
Deinde extende ligamentum ad sursum a parte inguinis ad altiorem partem corporis, ad partem furculae.	Then extend the band upward from the groin area to the upper part of the body, toward the chest.
Deinde accipiat minister alius fortis brachia eius, et minuat locum grossum coxae infirmae, et extendat ad exteriora extensione uehementi.	Next, another strong assistant should take hold of his arms and press the thick area of the affected hip, extending it forcefully outward.
Iam enim redit ad locum suum.	For now, it returns to its place.
Et haec species est facilioris reliquis speciebus curationis, qua reducitur hoc membrum.	And this method is the easiest of the other types of treatments for returning this limb to its place.
Si autem excusatur super te, et non obedit tibi ad introitum cum hac specie curationis omnino: tunc oportet, ut ligentur duo pedes infirmi simul cum ligamento forti, leni, super duos calcaneos, et super duo genua: et sit longitudo cuiusque a compare suo quantitatis quatuor digitorum: et sit crus infirmum extensum plus altero quantitate duorum digitorum.	But if it resists you and does not yield in this way of treatment at all, then the patient's two feet must be bound together with a strong, soft band over both heels and both knees; each should be separated from the other by a length of four fingers, and the affected leg should be extended two fingers' length longer than the other.
Deinde suspendatur infirmus super caput ligni, quod sit in domo: et sit longitudo eius a terra quantitas duorum brachiorum.	Then suspend the patient over the top of a beam that is within the house, at a height of about two arm-lengths from the ground.
Deinde praecipe iuueni forti, ut muniat caput coxae: et suspendatur cum infirmo inuenis alius: et impellat iuuenis alius, muniens coxam cum fortitudine, nam iunctura redit ad locum suum cum uelocitate.	Then instruct a strong young man to support the head of the hip; another youth should be suspended with the patient, and another youth should push, pressing the hip with strength, for the joint returns to its place quickly.
In reditione autem eius propria, quando est dislocatio ad exteriora, oportet, ut iaceat infirmus super podium, secundum quod narrauimus in habente gibbositatem.	For its specific reduction when the dislocation is outward, the patient should lie on a platform, as we described for someone with a hunch.
Et stringatur ligamentum super crus eius, infirmum proprie, et super pectus eius.	And let a band be bound over his affected leg and across his chest.
Deinde ponantur duo ligna, unum apud pedes eius, et alterum apud caput ipsius.	Then place two pieces of wood, one by his feet and the other by his head.
Deinde ponatur lignum additum in medio podii, fixum bene: super quod inuoluti sint panni humidi, ut non laedat infirmum: et sit lignum inter coxas eius, ut non attrahatur ad inferiora apud extensionem.	Then place an additional wooden piece securely fixed in the middle of the platform, over which wet cloths are wrapped so it does not harm the patient; and place the wood between his thighs so that he is not pulled downward during the extension.
Deinde extendat omnis minister ad partem suam, et medicus aequet reductionem manu sua.	Then let each assistant extend in their respective direction, and the physician aligns the reduction with his hand.
Si ergo obedit ad reuersionem, erit bonum.	If it responds to the reduction, that will be good.
Et si non, pone super ipsam tabulam, et preme ipsam, sicut diximus in gibbositate, aequaliter.	And if not, place him on the board and press it, as we described for a hunch, evenly.
Verumtamen oportet, ut iaceat infirmus super latus suum sanum.	However, the patient should lie on his healthy side.
Et quando dislocatio est ad anteriora, tunc oportet, ut extendatur crus infirmi uelociter: et est secundum hanc eandem narrationem super podium: et ponat medicus palmam manus suae dextrae super inguen infirmum.	And when the dislocation is forward, the patient’s leg should be quickly extended; this follows the same procedure on the platform: and the physician places his right palm over the affected groin.
Deinde coartet ipsum cum manu altera, et cum illa coartatione pone ipsum extensum ad inferiora ad partem genu.	Then, with the other hand, compress it, and with that compression, push it downwards toward the knee.
Et si dislocatio est ad posteriora: tunc non oportet, ut extendatur infirmus ad inferiora, et sit eleuatus super terram: imo oportet, ut sit positus super rem duram, sicut oportet, ut fit etiam si dislocatur ancha eius ad exteriora, secundum illud quod diximus de accubitu eius super duas anchas: et est super faciem eius, et ligamenta extensa: secundum quod diximus nuper.	And if the dislocation is backward, then the patient should not be extended downward, but instead lifted above the ground. In this case, he should be placed on a hard surface, as should also be done if his hip dislocates outward, according to what we described about his lying on both hips: he lies face down, with the bands extended, as we recently described.
Et oportet, ut administretur compressio cum tabula etiam super locum ad quem egreditur iunctura, cum completur reductio secundum quod oportet.	And it is necessary to apply compression with a board over the area where the joint is displaced, once the reduction is completed as it should be.
Et signum reditionis eius non occultatur tibi: et est, ut extendas duo crura infirmi: et quando uides ea aequalia, et infirmus stringit crus suum, et expandit ipsum absque excusatione: tunc scias, quia iam redit membrum, secundum quod oportet: tunc coniunge duas coxas, et pone emplastrum, et stringe cum imemati, id est, rota: ita, ut non moueatur ancha ad aliquam partium: et adhaereat infirmus quieti tribus diebus, aut quatuor.	The sign that it has returned will not escape you: it is when you extend both of the patient’s legs and observe them to be equal, and the patient moves his leg and extends it without difficulty. Then you will know that the limb has returned as it should. Then join the two hips, apply a plaster, and secure it with a support, called a "wheel," so that the hip does not move in any direction. The patient should remain at rest for three or four days.
Deinde solue ligamentum, et emplastrum, et compara crus cum altero: tunc si uides ea aequalia in statu, tunc scias, quod dislocatio iam firmata est: absolue ergo infirmum ad ambulandum.	Then loosen the band and the plaster, and compare the leg with the other; if you see them equal in position, then you will know that the dislocation has been fixed: release the patient to walk.
Et si uides aliquid mollificationis: tunc redi, et emplastra illud, et stringe illud secundum stricturam tuam primam: et dimitte illud etiam tribus diebus.	And if you observe any softening, then go back, apply the plaster again, and bind it with the same tightness as before, and leave it again for three days.
Deinde solue illud et fac ipsum tardare in ambulatione super utrumque aliquot diebus, donec confortetur bene.	Then loosen it and have him walk slowly on both legs for a few days until he regains full strength.
¶ De dislocationis Genu.	On the Dislocation of the Knee.
Caput XXXIII.	Chapter 33.
Genu dislocatur secundum tres modos.	The knee is dislocated in three ways.
Dislocatur ad exteriora, ad interiora, et ad inferiora, scilicet ad posteriora.	It dislocates outward, inward, and downward, specifically backward.
Et non dislocatur ad anteriora.	And it does not dislocate forward.
Et signum dislocationis eius, est, ut praecipias infirmo, ut applicet crus suum ad coxam suam.	The sign of its dislocation is that you instruct the patient to bring his leg toward his hip.
Si autem non adhaeret cum coxa: tunc scias, quod Genu est dislocatum.	If it does not adhere to the hip, then know that the knee is dislocated.
Et restauratio omnium modorum dislocationis eius est, ut facias sedere infirmum rectum, ita ut iam extendat crus suum si potest super illud, et adhaeret dorsum tuum ad faciem eius: et pone pedem eius inter pedes tuos.	The method of restoring all types of its dislocation is to have the patient sit upright so that he can extend his leg over it if he can, and press your back against his face, placing his foot between your feet.
Deinde adhaerere fac Genu eius cum palmis tuis, et recta eas inter digitos tuos super Genu eius.	Then clasp his knee with your palms and straighten them between your fingers over his knee.
Deinde applica cum palma tua duo latera Genu eius cum fortitudine.	Then press both sides of his knee with your palm with strength.
Et minister alius extendat pedem eius, donec redeat Genu ad locum suum.	And another assistant extends his foot until the knee returns to its place.
Et signum reditionis est, ut adhaereat crus cum coxa in lenitate, non laboriose.	The sign of its return is that the leg joins with the hip smoothly, not with difficulty.
Deinde emplastra ipsum, et adhaerere fac crus cum coxa.	Then apply a plaster, and make the leg adhere to the hip.
Deinde liga ea simul cum uitta tribus diebus, aut quatuor.	Then bind them together with a band for three or four days.
Deinde solue ea.	Then loosen them.
Et non utatur infirmus ambulatione aliquot diebus, donec sanetur.	And the patient should not walk for a few days until it heals.
Si autem recusatur tibi reductio eius, tunc administra extensionem fortem cum ligamentis, quorum praecessit narratio in curatione anchae, donec redeat.	But if its reduction is resisted, then perform a strong extension with bands, as previously described in the treatment of the hip, until it returns.
¶ De curatione dislocationis Calcanei.	On the Treatment of the Dislocation of the Heel.
Caput XXXIIII.	Chapter 34.
Calcaneus quandoque remouetur remotione parua, et quandoque dislocatur secundum complementum.	The heel sometimes shifts slightly, and sometimes dislocates fully.
Et dislocatio eius fit, aut ad interiora, aut ad exteriora.	Its dislocation occurs either inward or outward.
Et signum dislocationis eius est, quod uides Calcaneum inflatum, procedentem ad partem ad quam dislocatur.	The sign of its dislocation is that you see the heel swollen, protruding toward the direction in which it is dislocated.
Curatio autem remotionis eius, est facilis reditionis: et est, ut extendatur cum facilitate cum manibus, et aequetur, donec redeat.	The treatment of its slight displacement is an easy reduction: it involves gentle extension with the hands, aligning it until it returns.
In curatione autem eius quando dislocatur secundum complementum, oportet, ut facias sedere infirmum rectum: et teneat ipsum minister fortis ualde a posteriori parte dorsi eius, in medio ipsius.	For treating a full dislocation, have the patient sit upright, and a very strong assistant should hold him from behind at the middle of his back.
Deinde tene tu cum manu tua dextra pedem eius a superiori parte eius, et cum manu tua sinistra ab inferiori parte pedis eius loco cauillae.	Then grasp his foot with your right hand from the top, and with your left hand from the lower part of the foot at the ankle.
Deinde trahe pedem ad tecum manu tua dextra.	Then pull the foot toward you with your right hand.
Deinde cum sinistra duabus uicibus.	Then do the same with the left hand twice.
Deinde extende cum sinistra, et impelle pectus pedis cum dextra uersus crus absque labore.	Then extend with the left hand, and push the instep of the foot with the right hand toward the leg without strain.
Fac illud bis, sicut narrauimus.	Do this twice, as we described.
Deinde impelle pectus pedis ad crus in uice tertia, et tu trahis cauillam.	Then, on the third time, push the instep of the foot toward the leg, while you pull the ankle.
Si ergo redit in uice una, aut in duabus secundum hunc modum, et uides pedem aequatum, est bonum: et si non, tunc itera operationem super ipsum: quoniam ipse redit.	If it returns within one or two attempts in this way, and you see the foot aligned, that is good; if not, then repeat the procedure, for it will return.
Si autem prohibetur tibi reductio eius cum eo quod narrauimus: tunc fac iacere infirmum super dorsum suum super terram, et fige paxillum in terra firmatum ualde, ut cadat inter coxas eius: et tu iam inuoluisti super ipsum pannum, ut non laedat infirmum.	But if the reduction resists with the method we described, then have the patient lie on his back on the ground, and firmly fix a stake in the ground so that it falls between his hips, having already wrapped it in cloth so as not to harm the patient.
Deinde accipiat minister coxam eius: postea extendat minister alter pedem, aut cum manibus suis, aut cum ligamento quod liget super collum pedis.	Then one assistant should hold his hip, and another assistant should extend the foot, either with his hands or with a band tied around the ankle.
Deinde extendat omnis minister contra extensionem comparis sui: et paxillus sit stans inter duas coxas infirmi retinens eum, ut non attrahatur corpus eius ad inferiora apud extensionem.	Then let each assistant pull against the extension of his counterpart; and let the stake stand between the patient’s hips, holding him so that his body is not drawn downward during the extension.
Deinde aequet medicus dislocationem manibus suis, et minister alter teneat crus sanum ad inferiora.	Then the physician adjusts the dislocation with his hands, and another assistant holds the healthy leg downward.
Nam dislocatio redit cum uelocitate.	For the dislocation returns quickly.
Quando ergo redit dislocatio, et declaratur tibi sanitas reditionis eius: tunc pone emplastrum et stuppam, et stringe ipsam cum ligamentis firmiter, et liga pedem cum ligamento ad crus.	When the dislocation returns and its proper alignment is evident to you, then apply a plaster and cotton, bind it firmly with bandages, and tie the foot with a band to the leg.
Et oportet, ut caueas uel timeas de neruis, qui sunt supra calcaneum posterius, ut non sit ligamentum super eos uehemens, et laedat eos.	It is necessary to be cautious regarding the nerves above the heel from the back, to ensure the bandage is not overly tight, which could injure them.
Deinde dimitte ipsum duobus diebus, aut tribus.	Then leave it undisturbed for two or three days.
Quod si mollificatur ligamentum, stringe ipsum: deinde solue ipsum in die tertio, aut quarto: et prohibe infirmum ab ambulatione quadraginta diebus.	If the bandage loosens, tighten it again; then loosen it on the third or fourth day. Restrict the patient from walking for forty days.
Si uero conatur ambulare ante illud spacium, non fit securitas quin dissoluatur super ipsum dislocatio, et corrumpatur, et non recipiat post illud curationem.	If he tries to walk before this time has passed, there is no assurance that the dislocation will not recur and worsen, making it beyond healing.
Quod si accidat ei apostema calidum: tunc oportet, ut administres in sedatione eius illud, in quo praecessit narratio nostra in alio loco ex curatione, et embrocationem, donec remoueatur.	If a hot abscess forms, apply the treatment previously described in another section for cooling the inflammation, and apply embrocation until it is reduced.
¶ De dislocatione Digitorum pedis, et cura.	On the Dislocation of the Toes and their Treatment.
Caput XXXV.	Chapter 35.
Oportet, ut aequetur quod dislocatur ex eis cum extensione pauca absque labore: et illud quidem non est difficile, imo est facile.	The dislocated toe must be aligned with gentle extension without strain, which is not difficult but rather easy.
Si autem dislocatio est in quibusdam nodis dorsi pedis: tunc oportet, ut faciat sedere infirmus pedem suum super locum aequalem terrae, aut super tabulam: et est stans sicut ambulans.	If the dislocation is in one of the joints of the top of the foot, have the patient place his foot on a flat surface, such as the ground or a board, as though he were standing.
Deinde tu sta, et pone pedem tuum super illud quod eminet ex illis iuncturis.	Then, stand and place your foot over the protruding part of the joint.
Deinde calca illud cum pede tuo cum uirtute, donec redeat, et uideas ipsum iam aequatum, et non appareat in loco eminentia.	Press down with strength until the joint returns to place, and you see it aligned, without protrusion.
Deinde pone sub uentre pedis eius tabulam quae capiat pedem totum, cui sint duo capita.	Then place a board beneath his foot that covers the entire sole, with two ends.
Deinde stringe ipsum strictura decenti tribus diebus.	Then bind it with a moderate binding for three days.
Deinde solue ipsum, et fac quiescere eum ab ambulatione diebus multis, donec stringatur et securetur a reductione.	Then release it and have him refrain from walking for several days until it strengthens and stabilizes to prevent re-dislocation.
¶ De speciebus dislocationis cum uulnere uel fractura.	On Types of Dislocation with Wounds or Fractures.
Caput XXXVI.	Chapter 36.
Quando accidit aliquid ex illo, et conaris curationem eius, et ipsius restaurationem, multotiens succedit mors: et propter illud non oportet, ut praesumat super curationem eius, quod est huiusmodi, nisi qui est prudens in arte longi usus, misericors, pius, tardus, non audax, neque praesumptuosus: et ut utatur in incaeptione medicinis quae sedant apostemata calida tantum: et dimittat infirmum diuino iudicio.	When such an injury occurs, and one attempts to treat and restore it, often death results. Thus, only those who are wise in the art, experienced, compassionate, patient, cautious, not reckless nor presumptuous, should undertake such a treatment. They should begin with medicines that reduce hot abscesses alone and leave the patient to divine judgment.
Nisi uideas, aut speras salutem a perditione cum lenitate aegritudinis, et in quo apparet quiddam spei.	Unless you see or hope for relief from suffering with mild symptoms and a sign of possible recovery.
Conare ergo reductionem eius statim in principio rei, antequam accidat apostema calidum.	Attempt the reduction immediately at the onset, before a hot abscess forms.
Si ergo redit membrum, secundum quod uolumus, tunc administra regimen quod sedat apostema: et cura uulnus cum eo quod conuenit ei ex unguentis desiccatiuis.	If the limb returns as desired, then apply treatment to calm the abscess, and treat the wound with suitable drying ointments.
Quod si dislocatio est cum fractura, et accidunt in osse satagiae separatae, tunc conare ablationem earum, et fac in illo quod diximus in aegritudinibus simplicibus ex eo quod praecessit in locis earum.	If the dislocation includes a fracture and small fragments of bone have separated, attempt to remove these fragments, and follow the previously stated instructions for simple ailments as discussed in their respective places.
Et caue secundum tuam uirtutem, et custodi teipsum ab introitu in uiam timoris: secundum quod praecessit praedicatio mea tibi.	Exercise caution according to your ability, and guard yourself from entering the path of fear, as my previous counsel advised.
Nam illud est melius tibi apud Creatorem tuum, et magis permanens ad gloriam tuam, et magis saluum tuo corpori apud illos in tuo tempore.	For this will be better for you before your Creator, more enduring to your honor, and safer for your body among your peers in your time.
