Part I.
Concerning the benefit and harm of cautery: and to which complexions it is suitable, and at what time: and if with iron or gold.
Chapter I.
But before I recall the operation with it, it is necessary that I speak of the quality of its benefits and harms: and in which complexion it should be exhibited.
Therefore I say that the discussion of the quality of the benefit of cautery and its harm is a lengthy and subtle discussion, a science, and a hidden secret.
Many wise men have also spoken on it, and they have disagreed: and indeed a little from their discussions suffices me, fearing prolixity.
Therefore I say that cautery universally benefits every complexion which is with matter and without matter, except for two complexions, which are the hot complexion without matter and the dry complexion without matter.
However, in the hot and dry complexion with matter, they disagreed.
For some have said that cautery is beneficial in it.
And others said the opposite of that, namely that cautery is not suitable in illness which is from heat and dryness: because the nature of fire is heat: and it is unsuitable that a hot and dry illness should be healed with hot and dry.
And he who says the opposite of that said: that through cautery help is given in every hot and dry illness which happens in the bodies of men: because when you compare the body of man and its humors to the complexion of fire, you find the body of man cold.
And I indeed say through his words: because experiment has already revealed that to me several times.
However, it is not fitting that he come to that, except he who is already proven and practiced in the chapter of cautery by ultimate exercise, and has known the diversity of the complexions of men, and the disposition of illnesses in themselves, and their causes, and their accidents, and their duration.
From the other complexions however, there is no fear for you: and especially in cold and moist illnesses. For already all physicians have agreed on them: and they did not disagree in helping with cautery in them.
And know, sons, that from the secret of healing through cautery with fire, and its goodness over cautery with burning medicine is: because fire is simple, whose action does not arrive except at the member which is cauterized: and it does not harm another member joined with it, except with small harm.
But the action of burning medicines arrives at that which is distant from the members: and perhaps it causes in the member an illness of difficult healing, and perhaps it kills.
Fire however, because of its subtlety and goodness of its substance, does not do that, unless it overflows.
Now indeed that has become known to us through experiment, because of the length of service and study with the art, and standing upon the truth of things: and because of that I excuse myself from length of speech.
And know, sons, that they disagree in the time in which it is fitting to make cautery, and they place the better time in spring.
And I say that cautery is suitable at all times: therefore, because harm happening because of time is submerged in the help which is drawn with cautery.
And especially if cautery is for pains from necessary, strong, urgent pains, which do not tolerate slowness: because it is feared from them that harm may happen which is greater than the small harm entering because of time.
And let not that fall into your minds, O sons, which only the common people and foolish physicians fear, namely that that illness which is healed by cautery does not always return: and they put that as a consequence, and the matter is not as they think: because cautery is nothing but a medicine in place which alters the complexion, and dries the humors which are the causes why pains happen.
However, cautery surpasses medicine in the speed of its health, and the strength of its operation, and the vehemence of its dominion: and it is possible that the illness may return at some time according to the complexion of the infirm, and the location of his illness, and its strength: and what is prepared in his body from the aggregation of superfluities in it: and what arrives at it in its acquisition from foods, and similar things from causes, unless the illness in which cautery is administered occurs. An illness subtle in a member of few superfluities and humors, such as cautery of a tooth, because of pain, and similar things to it, for it is possible that that pain may not return in it.
But that happens in few cases.
But truly the speech of the common people again, that cautery is the last of medicines, is right speech: not to that which they intend in it: because they believe that there is no cure which can be compared with medicine, nor with anything else after the setting of the cautery.
But the matter is contrary to that, and the intention that cautery is the last of medicine is not, except that when we administer methods of cure in some illnesses, and those medicines do not help: then we administer cautery at the end of the matter, and it helps: then it follows that cautery is the last of medicine, not according to the intention which the common people and many foolish physicians understand.
But the ancients said that cautery with gold is better than cautery with iron: and they did not say that, except because of the evenness of gold, and the excellence of its substance: and they said that the place of cautery does not produce pus. That is not true absolutely: because I have already proved it, and found that it does not do that in some bodies without others.
And cautery with it is good, and better than with iron, as they said.
Nevertheless, when you heat a cautery in the fire made of gold, it is not clear to you when it is heated to the amount you want, because of the redness of the gold: and when it approaches cold, and if you add more heating to it, it melts in the fire, and turns into sheets.
Therefore the craftsman falls from it into difficulty: and because of that, cautery with iron is quicker and more readily available to us, because of its suitability for the operation.
