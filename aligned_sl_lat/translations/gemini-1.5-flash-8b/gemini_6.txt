Part I.
Concerning the benefit and harm of the cautery, and to which temperaments it is suitable, and at what time, and if with iron or gold.
Chapter 1.
But before I recount the operations using it, it is necessary to state the nature of its benefits and harms, and in which temperament it should be applied.
I say therefore, that the discourse on the nature of the cautery's benefit and harm is a lengthy and subtle discourse, requiring knowledge and a hidden secret.
Many wise men have spoken on it, and disagreed; but for me, a little from their words suffices, fearing to prolong the discussion.
I say therefore, that the cautery benefits universally every temperament, whether with or without material, except for the two temperaments which are hot without material, and dry without material.
However, in a hot and dry temperament with matter, they disagreed.
For some said that the cautery is beneficial in it.
And others said the opposite, namely that the cautery is not suitable for an illness arising from heat and dryness, because the nature of fire is heat; and it is unsuitable for being healed from a hot and dry illness with heat and dryness.
And he who says the opposite stated that through the cautery benefit is achieved in every hot and dry illness that occurs in human bodies, because when you compare the human body and its humors to the temperament of fire, you find the human body to be cold.
And indeed I say, through his discourse, that experience has revealed this to me several times.
However, it should not be applied to it unless he is already proven and skilled in the final exercise of the cautery chapter, and knows the diversity of human temperaments and the disposition of illnesses within themselves, and their causes and their occurrences, and the duration of their time.
However, there is no fear concerning the remaining temperaments, and especially in illnesses that are cold and moist. For all physicians have now agreed upon them, and they have not disagreed in the benefit of cautery in them.
And know, children, that from the secret of treatment by cautery with fire, and its goodness upon cautery with burning medicine, is that fire is simple, whose action does not reach, unless to the limb that is cauterized; and does not harm another limb joined with it, except for small harm.
However, the action of burning medicine reaches that which is elongated from the limbs; and perhaps causes illness of difficult healing, and perhaps kills, in the limb.
But fire, because of its subtlety and the goodness of its substance, does not do that, unless it overflows.
Now, however, that has become known to us through experiment, because of the length of service and study with the art, and the standing upon the truth of things; and because of that, I am excused from the length of speech.
And know, children, that they disagree on the time in which the cautery should be performed, and they set a better time in the spring.
And I say that cautery is appropriate at any time, because the harm that happens because of the time is submerged in the benefit that is drawn with the cautery.
And especially if the cautery is for pains from necessary, strong, urgent pains, which do not tolerate delay, because it is feared from them that a greater harm may occur than the small harm entering because of the time.
And do not let it fall into your minds, O children, that the common people and foolish physicians fear that the illness cured by cautery does not always return, and they posit that as a consequence, and the matter is not as they think: for cautery is nothing but a place for medicine, which alters the complexion and dries the humours which are the cause why pains occur.
However, cautery excels medicine in the speed of its cure, and the power of its operation, and the vehemence of its dominion: and it is possible that the illness may return at some time, according to the patient's complexion, and the location of their illness, and its power, and what is prepared in their body from the aggregation of superfluities in it, and what arrives in them by their acquisition from foods, and things similar to these causes, unless it is an illness in which cautery is administered. A subtle illness in a limb, of few superfluities and humours, like the cautery of a tooth, because of pain, and things similar to it, for it is possible that that pain will not return in it.
But that happens in few.
But indeed the common people's saying, that cautery is the last resort in medicine, is a correct saying: not according to what they intend by it; because they believe that there is no cure that can compare to medicine, nor to anything else after the cautery has passed.
However, the reality is the opposite of that, and the intention that cautery is the last resort in medicine is not, except that when we administer methods of treatment for some illnesses, and those medicines do not help; then we administer cautery in the end, and it does help; then from this it follows that cautery is the last resort in medicine, not according to the intention that the common people and many simple-minded physicians understand.
However, the ancients said that cautery with gold is better than cautery with iron; and they did not say that unless because of the equal weight of gold, and the excellence of its substance; and they said that the area of the cautery does not form pus. But that is not absolutely so; because I have already tested it, and found that it does not do that in some bodies without others.
And cautery with it [gold] is good, and better than with iron, as they said.
However, when you are heating cautery of gold in fire, it is not clear to you when it is heated according to the quantity you desire, due to the redness of the gold; and when it is quickly cooled, and if you add more heat to it, it melts in the fire, and turns into sheets.
Therefore, the craftsman falls into that occupation [or: predicament] due to that; and because of this, cautery with iron is quicker and closer to us, for the correctness of the procedure.
