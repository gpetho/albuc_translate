And that which comes from a fall or blows or similar things, the ancients spoke of methods of treatment with grand words, where there is not much benefit in any of them;
but I have shortened it according to my understanding, and have fashioned instruments in various ways.
I say that for the gibbosity that comes on the chest in front, there is no remedy nor health;
and also that which comes directly on both sides, and one cannot treat except properly that which comes on the back, as I say;
and the patient should be lying on their face in a level place, and have padding beneath them so they do not injure themselves;
then firmly fix a firebrand in the ground at the head, and another at the feet, and tie a cord beneath the armpits of the patient, and another at the hips, and at the knees, and at the ankles;
and two assistants hold the cords, one at the head and the other at the feet, across the two firebrands;
and let them be firmly fixed, the firebrands as we have said, and the physician places his palms firmly on the spine until it returns to place.
And if it does not return by this treatment, then take a board three cubits long, and dig beneath the wall, and let the patient be on their side, and put the end of the board under the wall, then place the middle of the board on the spine; and let the physician place his feet on the other end of the board, and press firmly, until it returns to its place;
and if you wish, you can do this with a winch that is turned by hand. And you should place two firebrands at the patient's head, an arm's length away, and towards the feet two others, and there should be between each firebrand, above and below, a handspan of space in length;
and each firebrand should have a hole through which the winch runs, and the firebrands should be firmly fixed in the ground so that they do not move;
and between each two firebrands there should be a turning bar, around which the ropes turn, and on the two turning bars, two small bars a handspan long, to turn the winch;
and then tie the rope that is through the armpits to the winch towards the head, and the other rope towards the feet to the other winch, and each of the assistants turns his winch with all the rope, and the physician places the spine straight as we have described.
And when it is straight, apply a desiccative plaster with plenty of egg, then the compresses, and then the splint of a small board three fingers long, so that it covers the place of the gibbosity and the healthy part of the vertebra, then tie it with a bandage, as we have said, and the patient should consume a light diet until he is healed;
and if any problem arises at the end of the healing process, it is necessary to administer softening and soothing medications, along with the splint that we mentioned previously; and sometimes a piece of lead is applied there, and sometimes the appearance arises in the last vertebra of the back, and it is thought to be a dislocation; and it is the bone that is showing, therefore one should not be presumptuous about it: perhaps it would cause death.
Know that at the hip joints and at the shoulder joints only dislocations occur, not like in other joints;
and the hip joint is dislocated in four ways: inwards and outwards, and forwards and backwards;
and more inwards than elsewhere, and rarely dislocated forwards and backwards.
And the sign of its inward dislocation is that when the injured leg is placed next to the healthy one, it is longer, and the patient's knee appears higher than the healthy one, and the patient cannot bend their knee to the buttocks; and the area close by is swollen with a hot abscess, because the head of the femur comes there.
And the sign of one that dislocates outwards is that these symptoms are opposite to the previous ones.
And the sign of one that occurs forwards is that they can extend their leg fully; however, they cannot bend the knee without pain, and if one wants to make them walk, they cannot move forward, and retain their urine, and the groin swells, and when they walk, they step on their heel.
And the sign of the one that occurs backwards, is that they cannot extend or bend their knee, and that leg is more crooked than the other, and the groin is softened, and the head of the femur is positioned under the ribs.
The reduction of this type of dislocation is such that you consider if the dislocation is old, and it remains fixed in its position, there is no cure; and there should be no attempt (to reduce it).
And in the one where the dislocation is fresh, and it's one of the four types of dislocations, you must hurry to replace the joint, extending it inwards and outwards, and moving it right and left; perhaps it will return (to its place), and no other treatment will be needed.
And if it does not return in this way, then you must prepare a strong assistant, and extend the leg strongly with two hands, or with a strap that is tied above the knee; and another assistant should extend his hand from above in such a way that he puts his hands under his armpits, then tightens it with a soft band above the thigh; and another should hold the end of the strap;
and they all extend, upwards and downwards, at the same time, until the patient is lifted off the ground at once, and remains hanging.
This method of extension is common to the four types, and if the dislocation has returned, as we have said, it is good.
If not, then a treatment appropriate to its type must be applied.
When the dislocation is inwards, you should lay the patient on his healthy side, then place the strap on the root of the thigh, and the place that is below the groin, then extend the strap upwards towards the groin, and towards the navel;
then have another assistant take his arms, and embrace the thick part of the injured thigh, and extend it outwards strongly: it will return to its place immediately;
and this type of dislocation is easier than the others.
And if you cannot fix it in this way that we have described, then you must tie the two feet of the patient together with a strong soft strap on both heels, and on both knees, and the injured leg should be extended two fingers more than the other;
then let him be hung by the top of a piece of wood, and be two arm-lengths above the ground;
then order an assistant to grasp the top of the thigh, and let another assistant hang with the patient, who firmly grasps the thigh, for the joint will return to its place quickly and suddenly.
And when the dislocation is in the nape of the neck, the patient should lie down as we have described for someone with gibbosity, and tighten a strap on the affected leg firmly, and on the weight as well;
then place two firebrands, one at the head and the other at the feet, then insert a firebrand between the patient's thighs amidst the mold/support; and have wet cloths placed around it, so that it doesn't injure the patient, then each assistant should stretch towards him, and the physician should set the dislocation evenly with his hand.
If it returns (to its place), that is good.
If not, put (him) on a table, and press as we have described for gibbosity, and have the patient lie on their healthy side.
And when the dislocation is forward, one must extend the affected leg quickly, and according to what I have said; and the physician should put his effort on the diseased groin, then press with the other hand, and extend it downwards towards the knee.
And if the dislocation is backwards, one should not extend the patient downwards, nor lift them off the ground, but rather they should be as if the hip were dislocated backwards, as we have said about lying on both hips; and (the patient) should be on their face, and the limb extended as we have said previously, and one should press on the joint against the table; and it will be correct when the patient extends their legs, and they are even; then bring the thighs together, and apply the plaster, and bind it with a bandage, so that the hip does not move; and (the patient) should rest for three or four days, then unbind it, and apply the plaster again;
and if you see that they are even, bind it as before, and leave it for three days;
then unbind it, and have him walk gently, until the limb is strengthened.
of the dislocation of the knee
The knee can be dislocated in three ways: namely, inwards and outwards, and backwards;
and it does not dislocate forwards.
And the sign is that you ask the patient to bend their leg at the thigh, and if it does not bend at the thigh, you know that it is dislocated;
and the restoration of all of them is that you have the patient sit upright, and extend their leg if they can, and put your back against their face, and put their foot on your feet;
then take their knee in your palms, and pull between your fingers, then bend both sides of the knee strongly; and another assistant extends their foot, until the knee returns to its place, and the sign is that they can bend their leg at the thigh easily;
and apply the plaster, and bind the leg to the thigh, and tie them together tightly for three or four days, then untie it;
and the patient should not exert themselves to walk, until the limb is strengthened.
And if the limb is not aligned, then the assistant applies strong traction, as we have described in the treatment of the hip, until it is healed.
of the dislocation of the heel
The heel is sometimes sprained, and sometimes completely dislocated, both inwardly and outwardly.
And the sign is that the ankle is swollen, and leans forward on the side where it is dislocated:
The reduction is easy, and one should extend it gently with one's hands when it is only sprained.
And when it is completely dislocated, you must have the patient sit upright, and an assistant hold him firmly by the leg, above the ankle;
then pull the foot towards you with your right hand, and then with your left twice, then stretch it upwards, and gently push the foot upwards;
and do this twice, as we have said, pushing and pulling the foot;
if it returns, that's good;
if not, repeat the procedure, because it will return;
and if it cannot return this way, have the patient lie on their back on the ground, and firmly fix a stake in the ground between their thighs, and tie cloths around it so that it does not injure the patient;
then let the assistant hold the thigh, and another assistant extend the foot with their hands or a cord, that they tie above the ankle; then each assistant extends evenly, and the stake should always be between the patient's thighs to hold the patient steady when extending;
then the physician evenly reduces the dislocation with their hands, and another assistant holds the healthy leg underneath, and the dislocation will return quickly.
And when it is in place, apply the plaster and padding on top, and tighten firmly, and tie the foot to the leg, and watch the nerves that are on the ankle behind, so that the bandage is not too tight;
then leave it for two or three days, and if the bandage loosens, tighten it;
then untie it on the third or fourth day, and forbid the patient from walking for four days; and if he walks before that time, it is not certain that it will not dislocate again.
And if hot abscesses develop there, you must treat them as we have said elsewhere, until they are healed.
of the fingers When the fingers are dislocated, they must be gently extended without force, and this is an easy thing.
And if the dislocation is in any joint of the back of the foot, you must make the patient sit on a flat surface or on a table, completely upright, and place your foot on the part of the joint that sticks out, then press firmly with your foot, until it returns;
and when it is even, and nothing sticks out, place a two-legged table under the sole of the foot, then bind it tightly, and leave it for three days, then untie it; and let him rest until he is healed.
of wound and dislocation together
When a wound occurs with the dislocation, and one wants to treat it, it often happens that the patient dies:
so do not be presumptuous in the treatment, unless you are wise in the art and you have practiced it for a long time, and unless you are compassionate and merciful and careful, not reckless;
and at the beginning, use soothing things for a hot abscess, and leave the patient in God's hands, if you see no hope of health; and when you see hope, then immediately return the limb to its place, before a hot abscess develops there;
and when the limb is in place, then administer things that soothe a hot abscess, and treat the wound, as appropriate, with drying ointments;
and if along with the dislocation there is a fracture, and small bone fragments separated, remove them, and do what we have said about simple maladies;
and beware of entering the path of fear, according to what I have said, for this benefits you according to your Creator, and for the safety of your soul and your body.
