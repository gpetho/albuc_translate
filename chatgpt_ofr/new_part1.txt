We say to you at the start of this book that whoever is not knowledgeable of the aforementioned matters should guard against falling into error and killing people, as I have seen many times with those who engage in this science and yet go about it without knowledge or experience.

Therefore, it is necessary that you take care, so that foolish doctors cannot speak ill of you. The third chapter is about setting dislocations and twisting, and similar things; and it is organized from head to foot, with the forms of the instruments.

Before we recall the work, it is fitting that I speak of the quality of the aid and of the harm for each constitution.

Je dis que li qualiteis de l’aide, et dou nuisement, est li plus longe parole et subtilz science et li secrez reponus; et de ceu parlerent li plusors des saiges, et s’acorderent a ceu; et me soffisent poc lor paroles, car je redous la longesce.  
I say that the qualities of aid and of harm are the longest discourse and the most subtle science, and the concealed secret; and on this many wise men have spoken, and agreed on it; but I am satisfied with few of their words, for I fear lengthiness.

I say that cautery universally aids every constitution where there is matter, except for two constitutions: namely, a hot constitution without matter, and a dry constitution without matter.

En complexion chaude et seche ensamble matiere, deviserent aucun et dirent que cautere i est grans aide; et li autre dirent le contraire: que cautere ne vaut a maladie que vient de chalor et de sechour.  
In a hot and dry constitution together with matter, some stated and said that cautery is a great aid; and others said the opposite: that cautery is not useful for an illness that comes from heat and dryness.

For the nature of fire is hot, and a hot illness should not be cured by heat, nor by dryness; and the one who says otherwise says that cautery gives great aid to hot and dry illnesses that come in people’s bodies.

Burning medicines pass from one member to another as they spread, and they bring to the member grievous diseases of difficult cure; and by chance they may kill the patient. Fire, due to its nobility and the goodness of its substance, does not do this unless there is an excess of it.

And I have found this through experiment and through the long service and study I have devoted to the art, based on the truth of things; and thus I excuse myself for the length of this discussion, as it does not please me to deprive you of the secret that is revealed in fire, and the quality of the work, how it drives out diseases from the body, as the philosopher has said in demonstration; now receive it in your thoughts.

And never think about this, dear son, in the way foolish doctors do; for the illness that is cured by cautery does not return at all; and they say this is so; but it is not as they think, because cautery is nothing other than a medicine that alters the constitution and dries the humors that are the cause of the pain, and is worth more than medicine due to the speed of its effect and the virtue of its work and its power.

About the cautery of the olive.  

Cist cauteres vaut a moistor et a froidor sus lou cerveil, et en i a dous c’on fait par la cause d’une maladie qui est apelee «soda», et par la cause de maintes humors qui dessendent dou chief as eulz, et as oreilles, et por multitude de songes et dolor de dens et dolor de gorge, et meismement a toutes maladies qui vienent de froidor si com paralisie et sode et apoplexie et semblans maladies.  
This cautery is good for moisture and coldness in the brain, and there are two kinds performed due to an illness called "soda" and because of many humors that descend from the head to the eyes and ears, and for a multitude of dreams, toothache, sore throat, and especially for all illnesses that come from cold, such as paralysis, soda, apoplexy, and similar illnesses.

The form of this cautery is that the patient should be purged with a cleansing medicine for the head for three or four nights, according to what the strength, age, and condition of the patient can bear; and then shave the patient's head.  

Et puis le fai seoir croisiet entre tes mains, et mettre ses mains sor som peis; aprés met la racine de ta palme sus la racine de son neis entre ses II eulz; et lai ou venra tes dois moiens, si saigne d’enche puis eschaufe le cautere qui est apelé «olivare», de coi ci est li forme:  
Then make him sit cross-legged with his hands on his chest; afterward, place the base of your palm on the base of his nose between his two eyes, and where your middle fingers rest, mark there with ink, then heat the cautery called "olive," of which this is the form:  

Et le met sor le leu signiet d’encre, et fai en dessendant et en estraignant ta main poc et poc en trestornant; puis lieve ta main isnelement, et rewarde le leu se tu vois l’os I sol petit; et se tu ne lou vois, si rencomence le cautere tant que tu voies l’os si com je lou t’ai dit; aprés pren I poc de seil, et met en awe et molle coton dedens, et met sor le leu, et li laisse III jors; aprés met sus cotun moilliet en burre; et laisse sus tant que li eschaudure soit ostee; aprés cure la plaie de l’oignement qui est apelez «tetrafarmaçon», tant qu’il soit sanez.  
And place it on the spot marked with ink, descending and pressing your hand little by little while turning; then quickly lift your hand and inspect the spot to see if you glimpse the bone just a bit; and if you do not see it, repeat the cautery until you see the bone as I have told you; then take a little salt, put it in water, moisten cotton with it, place it on the spot, and leave it for three days; after that, place cotton moistened with butter on it; and leave it until the burn is removed; afterward, treat the wound with an ointment called "tetrafarmaçon" until it is healed.  

Et se dirent li ancien, que quant plus demoure overte li plaie, plus s’espurge; et por ce si aide mues.  
And the ancients said that the longer the wound remains open, the more it purges; and this helps much.

And some said that one should leave the cautery in place until part of the bone is burnt, and then remove what is burnt from the bone, and treat it.  

Et dient li autre c’on doit le cautere tant lassier sus, et bouter si fort c’on abaite de l’os ausi large comme I vertoil que les femmes mettent en lor fusez por ceu que les humors issent mues hors; et doit on lassier la plaie longement overte; aprés le doit on curer et sodeir.  
And others say that one should leave the cautery in place and press so firmly that the bone is abraded as wide as a spindle whorl that women put on their spindles, so that the humors may flow out; and the wound should be left open for a long time; afterward, it should be cleaned and closed.  

Et ces II manieres de cauteres ne me semblent mie estre bones dou tout en tout, fuers qu’en aucuns homes ou il ait voie de paor; et mues me semble que je lou doie laissier; car li chiés li est afoiblis par les solutions continuees de nature, si com nos le tesmoignons ens autres membres; et meisme quant li chiés dou malaide est foibles par nature, et li premiere semblance de cautere est plus salve et muedre por moi, et de celi use je oevre en donques emsamble salut.  
And these two methods of cautery do not seem to me to be good in every case, except in some men where there is a way to fear; and it seems to me that I should abandon it, for the head is weakened by continuous natural discharge, as we observe in other body parts; and especially when the patient's head is weak by nature, the first type of cautery is safer and better to me, and I use it with combined safety and health.

dou cautere dou chief  
On cautery of the head  

Quant en une partie dou chief vient dolor sodaine, et est estendue li maladie jusc’a l’oil, et li chiés est espurgiez par medicines mondifians, et on a fait les autres cures que j’ai dit en la division des maladies, et tout ceu ne profite mie: adonques li fai cauteres selonc II manieres, c’est a savoir de medicine ague et de feir.  
When sudden pain arises in one part of the head and the illness extends to the eye, and the head has been purged by cleansing medicines, and all other treatments described in the division of illnesses have been done and none have helped, then apply cautery in two ways, namely with a sharp medicine and with iron.  

Li cauteres de medicine ague si est que tu preignes une doce d’alz, et oste l’escorce et le recope I poc as II chiés, et puis fent la temple ou li dolor tient en teil maniere que tu puisses reponre la doce d’alz desoz le cuir; et quant elle sera reponuee si le lie et l’estraint bien et le laisse ensi XII houres; et puis le deslie et trait fuers la doce d’ail, et laisse la plaie ensi II jors ou III; puis met sus cotun plongiet en burre tant que porreture en isse.  
The cautery with sharp medicine is done by taking a clove of garlic, peeling it, and cutting a little off the two ends, then cutting open the temple where the pain is in such a way that you can place the garlic clove under the skin; when it is placed, bind and compress it well and leave it for twelve hours; then unbind it, remove the garlic clove, and leave the wound like that for two or three days; afterward, place cotton soaked in butter on it until the discharge comes out.  

Aprés le cure d’oignement tant qu’il soit sanez; et se tu vues si fai ceu d’aucunes medicines comburans que j’ai escrites ou dozime traitier des medicines comburans.  
Then treat it with ointment until it is healed; and if you wish, use one of the burning medicines I have described in the twelfth treatise on burning medicines.
